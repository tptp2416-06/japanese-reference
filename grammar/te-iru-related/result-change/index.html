    〜ている - Resultative Change¶

    The change (暗くなる) occurs before the reference time (着くころ). The 〜ている form — 暗くなってる — expresses that at the reference time, the post-change state is the reality. The speaker is making a prediction about what state will be in effect at a future reference point.

    Change Verbs — The Core Set¶

    Verbs that take the resultative 〜ている meaning typically describe instantaneous changes — things that happen at a moment, not over a duration. Once the change occurs, the new state continues.

    Verb Meaning (Change) 〜ている Meaning (Resultative State)
    死しぬ To die 死しんでいる Is dead (not "is dying")
    結婚けっこんする To get married 結婚けっこんしている Is married (not "is getting married")
    開あく To open 開あいている Is open (not "is opening")
    閉しまる To close 閉しまっている Is closed (not "is closing")
    落おちる To fall 落おちている Has fallen / is on the ground
    消きえる To disappear 消きえている Has disappeared / is gone
    汚よごれる To get dirty 汚よごれている Is dirty (not "is getting dirty")
    乾かわく To dry 乾かわいている Is dry (not "is drying")
    沸わく To boil 沸わいている Is boiling / has boiled (the water is hot)

    The literal translation trap

    English speakers often want to translate 死んでいる as "is dying." That is almost always wrong. "Is dying" describes an ongoing process — the person is still alive, approaching death. 死んでいる means the person is already dead — the change is complete, the state is in effect. The English "is dead" captures it; "has died" also works. "Is dying" does not.

    Adjective + なる as a Change Verb¶

    The pattern 〜くなる (i-adjective adverbial form + なる) and 〜になる (na-adjective/noun + になる) function exactly like change verbs. The change is the transition into the state; 〜ている describes being in that state.

    Base Meaning Change Form 〜ている Resultative Meaning
    暗くらい Dark 暗くらくなる 暗くらくなっている Is dark (has become dark)
    暖あたたかい Warm 暖あたたかくなる 暖あたたかくなっている Is warm (has warmed up)
    静しずかだ Quiet 静しずかになる 静しずかになっている Is quiet (has become quiet)
    元気げんきだ Well / energetic 元気げんきになる 元気げんきになっている Is well (has recovered)

    Progressive vs. Resultative — Side-by-Side¶

    The same 〜ている form means completely different things depending on the verb type. Here is the contrast:

    Verb Verb Type 〜ている Meaning
    食たべる Action 食たべている Is eating (action in progress)
    走はしる Action 走はしっている Is running (action in progress)
    死しぬ Change 死しんでいる Is dead (state resulting from completed change)
    開あく Change 開あいている Is open (state resulting from completed change)
    着きる Change (punctual) 着きている Is wearing (state resulting from having put on)
    知しる Change (punctual) 知しっている Knows (state resulting from having learned)

    着ている and 知っている are resultative — don't read them as progressive

    Two especially common traps: - 着ている means "is wearing" (the state after putting clothes on), not "is putting on clothes" (which would be 着ている in some contexts, but usually 着替えている or similar). - 知っている means "knows" (the state after learning), not "is knowing." The verb 知る means "to come to know / to learn" — a punctual change. 知っている is the post-change state: "to have learned and therefore know."

    Examples — Resultative 〜ている in Context¶

    Japanese English
    窓まどが開あいているから、ちょっと寒さむいね。 The window is open, so it's a bit cold, isn't it.
    Literal: Window opened-state-is because, a-little cold, right?
    ~Context: The opening happened earlier. The speaker finds the resulting open state and comments on it.~
    彼かれはもう結婚けっこんしているよ。 He's already married.
    Literal: He already married-state-is.
    ~Context: The wedding happened in the past. The married state continues.~
    道みちが混こんでるから、少すこし遅おくれるかも。 The roads are congested, so I might be a bit late.
    Literal: Road congested-state-is because, a-little late-might.
    ~Context: 混む (to become congested) is a change. 混んでいる describes the post-change congested state.~
    もう暗くらくなってるから、気きをつけて帰かえってね。 It's already dark out, so be careful going home.
    Literal: Already dark-become-state-is because, careful go-home.
    ~Context: Same pattern as the reference sentence. The darkening happened earlier; the darkness is the present state.~
    財布さいふが落おちてるよ。 Your wallet has fallen — it's on the ground.
    Literal: Wallet fallen-state-is, you-know.
    ~Context: The falling happened earlier. The speaker finds the wallet in its fallen state.~

    The Contraction — 〜ている → 〜てる¶

    In casual spoken Japanese, 〜ている regularly contracts to 〜てる. This is not slang or substandard — it is the default pronunciation in everyday conversation.

    Full Form Contracted Meaning
    暗くらくなっている 暗くらくなってる Has become dark / is dark
    開あいている 開あいてる Is open
    結婚けっこんしている 結婚けっこんしてる Is married
    知しっている 知しってる Knows

    The contraction involves dropping the い after the te-form. For godan verbs, the te-form ending contracts:

    • 〜んでいる → 〜んでる （死しんでいる → 死しんでる）
    • 〜っている → 〜ってる （入はいっている → 入はいってる）
    • 〜いている → 〜いてる （開あいている → 開あいてる）

    The reference sentence uses the contracted form

    着くころには暗くなってるかも。The 暗くなってる is the contracted form of 暗くなっている. In casual speech, the full form 暗くなっている would sound slightly stiff or deliberate. The contraction is the natural choice between friends, family, or in relaxed self-talk.

    Common Mistakes¶

    1. Reading resultative 〜ている as progressive¶

    The single most common error. Learners apply the "is doing" meaning to every 〜ている they see, including change verbs where that reading makes no sense.

    Incorrect reading

    もう死しんでいる。

    ~"He is dying." — Wrong. The person is already dead. "Is dying" would describe an ongoing process; 死んでいる describes a completed state.~

    Correct reading

    "He is dead." / "He has died."

    ~The change (death) occurred. The state (being dead) is in effect. Resultative 〜ている.~

    2. Using plain form where 〜ている is needed¶

    Using the dictionary form of a change verb when you mean the resulting state.

    Incorrect

    もう暗くらくなるから、帰かえろう。

    ~"It's going to get dark soon, so let's go home." — 暗くなる describes the change about to happen. Grammatically fine on its own, but...~

    Correct (if the intended meaning is state)

    もう暗くらくなってるから、帰かえろう。

    ~"It's already dark, so let's go home." — 暗くなっている describes the current state. The darkness is already here.~

    3. Using 〜ている with stative verbs¶

    Verbs like ある (to exist), いる (to exist, animate), and できる (to be able) describe states, not actions or changes. They rarely appear with 〜ている because the state is already built into the verb.

    Ungrammatical

    そこにあっている。

    ~"It is existing there." — ある is already a state. Adding 〜ている is redundant and ungrammatical.~

    Correct

    そこにある。

    ~"It is there." Plain form for stative verbs.~

    Japanese Mindset — States Over Actions¶

    Japanese has a strong preference for describing the world in terms of states rather than actions. The resultative 〜ている is one of the clearest expressions of this tendency.

    In English, "It's dark" is a simple adjective construction — the darkness is a property, not an outcome. In Japanese, 暗くなっている embeds the same observation in a temporal logic: it became dark, and that darkness is now the state of the world. The change is acknowledged even when only the state matters.

    This is not just grammar — it reflects a deeper cognitive framing. Where English often foregrounds the present condition, Japanese frequently frames the condition as the result of a prior change. The 〜ている form is the grammatical tool that makes this framing possible.

    • State as outcome. 結婚している means "is married" — the state is the outcome of a wedding. 死んでいる means "is dead" — the state is the outcome of dying. The change is always implied, even when not mentioned.
    • Temporal precision. The distinction between 暗くなる (the change) and 暗くなっている (the state) lets Japanese speakers place events precisely in time relative to each other. In the reference sentence, this distinction is critical: the darkening precedes the arrival. The 〜ている is what establishes that sequence.
    • Continuation of state. The 〜ている in both progressive and resultative uses shares a core meaning: the situation described by the verb stem has continuing relevance. For action verbs, the action continues. For change verbs, the resulting state continues. The form is unified around this idea of ongoing validity.

    Distinguishing Progressive from Resultative — A Practical Guide¶

    When you encounter an unfamiliar verb with 〜ている, ask:

    1. Is the verb an action that takes time? (食べる、走る、読む、書く) → Progressive. "Is doing."
    2. Is the verb a change that happens at a moment? (死ぬ、開く、落ちる、結婚する) → Resultative. "Has become / is in the resulting state."
    3. Is the verb an adjective + なる? (暗くなる、暖かくなる、静かになる) → Resultative. "Has become / is in that state."
    4. Is the verb already a state? (ある、いる、できる) → Plain form is used; 〜ている is usually absent or means something else.
    Test Progressive Resultative
    "Is [verb]ing" makes sense in English? Yes No (or sounds weird)
    "Has [verb]ed" or "is [adjective]" fits better? No Yes
    The verb describes a process or a moment? Process (takes time) Moment (instantaneous change)

    See Also¶

    Same Grammar Family¶

    • 〜ている (progressive) — "is doing" for action verbs
    • 〜てある — resultative with an agent implied; "has been done deliberately" (contrast with 〜ている for change verbs)
    • 〜ていた — past state; "was in the state of having become X"
    • 〜てしまう — regrettable or complete action; often combines with 〜ている for "has unfortunately ended up in X state"

    Related Patterns¶

    • 〜くなる / 〜になる — the change form that feeds into resultative 〜ている
    • 〜ことになる — "it has been decided that" — a resultative use of なる
    • 〜ているところだ — "is just in the middle of doing" — emphasises the progressive over the resultative reading

    Contrast With¶

    • Plain form vs. 〜ている for change verbs — the core distinction this page explains
    • 〜た (past) vs. 〜ている — past describes the change event; 〜ている describes the resulting state. 死んだ = "died" (the event). 死んでいる = "is dead" (the state).
