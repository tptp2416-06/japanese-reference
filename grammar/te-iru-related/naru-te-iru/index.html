    なる as a Change Verb — The Full Picture¶

    〜くなる / 〜になる + ている — "Has Become" and "Is Now"¶

    Relationship to Main Page¶

    This page extends: 〜ている for Resultant States

    This supplementary page deepens the treatment of なる as a change verb introduced in the main resultative 〜ている page. Where the main page covers the broader distinction between progressive and resultative 〜ている, this page focuses exclusively on なる — its grammar, its combinations with different word classes, and the specific resultative meanings that arise when 〜ている attaches to it.

    Read the main page first if you haven't already. This page assumes you understand the core distinction between progressive 〜ている (action verbs) and resultative 〜ている (change verbs).

    Why なる Deserves Its Own Page¶

    なる is the single most productive change verb in Japanese. It combines with:

    • Every i-adjective (暗くなる, 暖かくなる, 大きくなる…)
    • Every na-adjective (静かになる, 元気になる, 有名になる…)
    • Every noun (医者になる, 春になる, 問題になる…)
    • Many verb forms (〜ことになる, 〜ようになる, 〜なくなる…)

    Each of these combinations can take 〜ている to describe the resulting state of that becoming. The pattern is infinitely productive and appears constantly in everyday speech. Mastering なる + ている unlocks a huge portion of natural Japanese expression.

    Quick Summary¶

    At a glance

    • なる means "to become" — it describes a change from one state to another.
    • 〜くなる attaches to i-adjectives (adverbial 〜く form + なる).
    • 〜になる attaches to na-adjectives and nouns (に particle + なる).
    • When 〜ている attaches to any なる construction, the meaning is resultative: "has become X and is now in that state."
    • The contracted form 〜てる is standard in casual speech: 暗くなってる, 医者になってる, etc.
    • 〜ていた describes a past resulting state: "had become X / was X (at that past reference time)."

    The Grammar — How なる Combines¶

    Base Patterns¶

    Source Word Change Form Meaning 〜ている Form Resultative Meaning
    i-adjective 〜くなる To become [adj] 〜くなっている Has become [adj] / is [adj]
    na-adjective 〜になる To become [adj] 〜になっている Has become [adj] / is [adj]
    Noun 〜になる To become [noun] 〜になっている Has become [noun] / is now [noun]

    Formation Rules¶

    i-adjectives → 〜くなる¶

    Remove the final い and add く + なる.

    i-adjective Stem + く + なる + ている Meaning
    暗くらい (dark) 暗く 暗くなる 暗くなっている Has become dark / is dark
    暖あたたかい (warm) 暖かく 暖かくなる 暖かくなっている Has warmed up / is warm
    大おおきい (big) 大きく 大きくなる 大きくなっている Has grown / is big (now)
    良よい (good) 良く 良くなる 良くなっている Has improved / is better
    寒さむい (cold) 寒く 寒くなる 寒くなっている Has gotten cold / is cold
    早はやい (early/fast) 早く 早くなる 早くなっている Has gotten earlier/faster

    na-adjectives → 〜になる¶

    Remove だ (or use the base stem) and add に + なる.

    na-adjective Stem + に + なる + ている Meaning
    静しずかだ (quiet) 静かに 静かになる 静かになっている Has become quiet / is quiet
    元気げんきだ (well/energetic) 元気に 元気になる 元気になっている Has recovered / is well
    有名ゆうめいだ (famous) 有名に 有名になる 有名になっている Has become famous / is famous
    好すきだ (liked) 好きに 好きになる 好きになっている Has come to like / is in love
    便利べんりだ (convenient) 便利に 便利になる 便利になっている Has become convenient / is convenient
    大変たいへんだ (tough) 大変に 大変になる 大変になっている Has gotten tough / is difficult

    Remember to drop だ

    The dictionary form of na-adjectives typically ends in だ (静かだ, 元気だ), but になる attaches to the stem without だ. 静かだ → 静かになる, not 静かだになる.

    Nouns → 〜になる¶

    Nouns use the same に + なる pattern as na-adjectives.

    Noun + に + なる + ている Meaning
    医者いしゃ (doctor) 医者になる 医者になっている Has become a doctor / is now a doctor
    春はる (spring) 春になる 春になっている Has become spring / it's now spring
    夜よる (night) 夜になる 夜になっている Has become night / it's night now
    問題もんだい (problem) 問題になる 問題になっている Has become a problem / is an issue
    大人おとな (adult) 大人になる 大人になっている Has grown up / is an adult now
    話題わだい (hot topic) 話題になる 話題になっている Has become a trending topic / is being talked about

    The Meaning Spectrum — なる vs. なっている¶

    The core distinction is temporal: the change itself vs. the post-change state. But the nuance shifts slightly depending on the word class.

    i-adjectives: State Arrival¶

    〜くなる is the transition. 〜くなっている is the arrival in that state, with the state persisting.

    Form Example Meaning
    暗くなる もうすぐ暗くなる。 "It will get dark soon." The darkening is about to happen.
    暗くなっている もう暗くなっている。 "It's already dark." The darkening happened; the darkness is here.
    暗くなっていた 帰る頃ころには暗くなっていた。 "By the time I went home, it had gotten dark." Past reference time; darkness was already in effect.

    na-adjectives: Condition Onset vs. Condition in Effect¶

    Form Example Meaning
    静かになる 夜は静かになる。 "It gets quiet at night." General statement about the change.
    静かになっている もう静かになっている。 "It's already quiet." The quietness is the current state.
    静かになっていた 図書館はもう静かになっていた。 "The library had already gotten quiet." Past state.

    Nouns: Identity Change vs. Current Identity¶

    With nouns, 〜になっている often carries a nuance of "has ended up being" — the change may be unexpected, noteworthy, or the result of a process.

    Form Example Meaning
    医者になる 将来医者になりたい。 "I want to become a doctor in the future." The aspiration.
    医者になっている 気づいたら医者になっていた。 "Before I knew it, I'd become a doctor." The result; maybe unexpected or just a reflection.
    問題になる それが問題になるかも。 "That might become a problem." The change might occur.
    問題になっている それはもう問題になっている。 "That's already become a problem / That is already an issue." The problematic state is in effect.

    話題になっている — a fixed expression

    話題になっている is a standard phrase meaning "has become a topic of conversation / is being talked about / is trending." It almost always appears in the 〜ている form because it describes a current state of being in the public conversation. 話題になる exists but is less common — it would describe the moment something starts trending, which is harder to pinpoint.

    The Temporal Logic — Visualised¶

    Every なる + ている sentence implies the same timeline structure: Timeline: ──────────────────────────────────────────→

    [State A] ──[Change]──→ [State B persists] ──→ ↑ ↑ ↑ (before) なる なっている (the (the post-change transition) state, ongoing)

    • 前に (before the change): The world was in State A (明るい / 静かじゃない / 学生, etc.).
    • なる (the change): The transition from A to B occurs at a moment or over an interval.
    • なっている (the post-change state): State B is now the reality. It began at the moment of change and continues.

    When you use なっている, you are placing the listener's attention after the change, in the world where State B is already the condition.

    Extended Examples — なる + ている in Context¶

    i-adjective + なっている¶

    Japanese English
    暖あたたかくなってるから、コートいらないかも。 It's warmed up, so you might not need a coat.
    The warming already happened. The current state is warm.
    彼かれ、前まえより大おおきくなってるね。 He's gotten bigger than before, hasn't he?
    The growth happened over time; the new larger state is what the speaker observes now.
    最近さいきん、物価ぶっかが高たかくなってる。 Prices have been getting higher lately.
    The change is ongoing and the resulting high-price state is in effect.
    前まえより日本語にほんごが上手じょうずくなってる！ Your Japanese has gotten better than before!
    Not "is getting better" — the improvement is observed as an accomplished state.

    na-adjective + なっている¶

    Japanese English
    薬くすりが効きいて、楽らくになってる。 The medicine kicked in — I'm feeling better.
    楽だ (comfortable / at ease) → 楽になる (to become comfortable) → 楽になっている (is in a comfortable state).
    町まちがきれいになってるね。掃除そうじしたのかな。 The town has gotten clean, hasn't it? I wonder if someone cleaned.
    The change (cleaning) happened; the speaker observes the resulting clean state.
    彼女かのじょ、最近さいきんきれいになってるよね。 She's gotten prettier lately, hasn't she?
    きれいになっている — the speaker observes a current state that is the result of a change.
    説明せつめいを聞きいて、事情じじょうが複雑ふくざつになっていることがわかった。 After hearing the explanation, I understood that the situation had become complicated.
    The complication happened before the understanding. なっている marks the state at the time of discovery.

    Noun + なっている¶

    Japanese English
    気きがついたら外そとは暗くらくなっていて、もう夜よるになっていた。 Before I realised, it had gotten dark outside — it had already become night.
    夜になっていた — past resultative. At the moment of noticing, night was already the state in effect.
    彼かれはいつの間まにか有名人ゆうめいじんになっている。 He's become a celebrity before anyone knew it.
    The change is complete; the celebrity state is current.
    その店みせはもう閉店へいてんになっているよ。 That shop has already closed down / is already out of business.
    閉店になる (to close down, of a business) — the change happened; the closed state is in effect.
    気候変動きこうへんどうはもう深刻しんこくな問題もんだいになっている。 Climate change has already become a serious problem.
    The transition to "serious problem" status has occurred; that status is current.

    Special Patterns — なる with Verb Phrases¶

    なる also combines with verb-derived structures, creating idiomatic patterns that frequently appear with 〜ている.

    〜ようになる — "Come to Do / Reach the Point of Doing"¶

    Describes a change in ability, habit, or circumstance — reaching a state where something happens.

    Form Example Meaning
    〜ようになる 日本語にほんごが話はなせるようになる To reach the point of being able to speak Japanese
    〜ようになっている 日本語にほんごが話はなせるようになっている Has reached the point of being able to speak Japanese / can now speak Japanese
    最近さいきん、早はやく起おきられるようになっている。 Lately, I've gotten to the point where I can wake up early.
    The change (from can't to can) has occurred; the ability state is current.

    〜ことになる — "It Has Been Decided That / Things Have Worked Out So That"¶

    Describes a decision or outcome — often external, beyond the speaker's control.

    Form Example Meaning
    〜ことになる 来月らいげつ転勤てんきんすることになる It is decided that I will be transferred next month
    〜ことになっている 来月らいげつ転勤てんきんすることになっている It has been decided that I'm being transferred next month / The arrangement is that I'll be transferred
    会議かいぎは来週らいしゅうの月曜げつように行おこなわれることになっている。 It has been decided that the meeting will be held next Monday.
    The decision was made earlier. The arrangement is the current state. 〜ことになっている is the standard way to state an existing arrangement.

    〜なくなる — "To Stop Doing / To No Longer Be"¶

    The negative form of なる constructions. Describes the transition into a state of absence or cessation.

    Source Change 〜ている Meaning
    i-adj: 寒い 寒くなる (become cold) 寒くなっている Is cold
    Negative: 寒くない 寒くなくなる (stop being cold) 寒くなくなっている Has stopped being cold / is no longer cold
    Verb: 食べる 食べなくなる (stop eating) 食べなくなっている Has stopped eating / no longer eats
    彼かれは最近さいきんお酒さけを飲のまなくなっている。 He's stopped drinking alcohol recently.
    The change (cessation) happened; the non-drinking state persists.

    なっていた — Past Resultative State¶

    The past form 〜ていた places the resulting state at a past reference time — the world at that past moment already had the change completed.

    Form Temporal Meaning Example
    なっている Change completed; state in effect now もう暗くなっている。 "It's already dark now."
    なっていた Change completed before a past reference time; state was in effect then 着いたときにはもう暗くなっていた。 "When I arrived, it was already dark."
    Japanese English
    気きがついたときには、もう手遅ておくれになっていた。 By the time I noticed, it had already become too late.
    The becoming-too-late happened before the noticing. なっていた places the "too late" state in the past, prior to the noticing event.
    彼かれが着ついたとき、パーティーはもう終おわっていた。 When he arrived, the party had already ended.
    (Not a なる sentence, but the same resultative logic: the ending preceded the arrival. 終わっている is resultative; 終わっていた is past resultative.)

    Real Speech vs. Textbook Form¶

    In casual spoken Japanese, 〜ている regularly contracts. This applies to all なる constructions.

    Full Form Contracted Meaning
    暗くらくなっている 暗くらくなってる Has become dark / is dark
    静しずかになっている 静しずかになってる Has become quiet / is quiet
    医者いしゃになっている 医者いしゃになってる Has become a doctor / is a doctor
    〜ようになっている 〜ようになってる Has come to be the case that…
    〜ことになっている 〜ことになってる It has been arranged that…

    The contraction is the default in casual conversation. The full form sounds slightly more deliberate, explanatory, or emphatic. Learners should recognise both and produce the contracted form in casual settings.

    Common Mistakes¶

    1. Using plain form when the state is in effect¶

    Incorrect

    もう暗くらくなるから、電気でんきをつけよう。

    ~"It's going to get dark soon, so let's turn on the lights." — 暗くなる is the change about to happen. But if it's already dark…~

    Correct for current state

    もう暗くらくなってるから、電気でんきをつけよう。

    ~"It's already dark, so let's turn on the lights." — 暗くなっている describes the current state.~

    2. Using になっている for future arrangements that aren't 〜ことになっている¶

    Incorrect

    明日あしたは雨あめになっている。

    ~"Tomorrow has become rain / is in a state of rain." Weird. The change hasn't happened yet; you're making a prediction, not describing a current state.~

    Correct

    明日あしたは雨あめになるだろう / なりそうだ。

    ~"Tomorrow will probably become rain / looks like it'll rain." Use なる (change form) for future predictions, not なっている.~

    Exception: 〜ことになっている

    明日雨が降ることになっている is fine — it means "it has been arranged / it is forecast that it will rain tomorrow." The ことになっている is an existing arrangement or determination; the rain itself is not yet a state.

    3. Confusing 〜くなる and 〜になる¶

    Incorrect

    暖あたたかいになる。

    ~暖かい is an i-adjective. It takes 〜くなる, not 〜になる.~

    Correct

    暖あたたかくなる。

    ~i-adjective adverbial form is 〜く. 暖かい → 暖かく + なる.~

    Incorrect

    静しずかくなる。

    ~静か is a na-adjective. It takes 〜になる, not 〜くなる.~

    Correct

    静しずかになる。

    ~na-adjective uses に + なる. 静か + に + なる.~

    Japanese Mindset — State Orientation¶

    Japanese has a strong preference for describing the world in terms of current states that resulted from prior changes. The なる + ている pattern is one of the most natural expressions of this tendency.

    An English speaker might say "It's dark," framing the darkness as a simple property of the present. A Japanese speaker saying 暗くなっている embeds the same observation in a timeline — the darkness is the outcome of a change. Both describe the same reality, but the Japanese version carries an implicit awareness of how things got this way.

    This becomes especially relevant with nouns and social statuses. 医者になっている ("has become a doctor / is now a doctor") frames the profession as the result of a process — study, training, qualification. The identity is not just a label; it's an outcome. Similarly, 問題になっている ("has become a problem") frames the problem status as something that arose — it wasn't always there; it came into being.

    • Change is acknowledged, even when only the state matters. The なる in なっている quietly references the transition, even when the speaker's focus is on the present.
    • States are temporary, even when they seem permanent. 医者になっている implies a before (not a doctor) and potentially an after (retirement, career change). The 〜ている keeps the state anchored in time rather than treating it as an eternal property.
    • なっている is the natural choice for observations. When you notice something has changed — the room is quiet, the lights are on, someone has grown — なっている is the form that registers the change as completed and the new state as the reality.

    Quick Reference — All なる + ている Patterns¶

    Source Change Form Resultative Form Meaning
    i-adj 〜くなる 〜くなっている Has become [adj] / is [adj]
    na-adj 〜になる 〜になっている Has become [adj] / is [adj]
    Noun 〜になる 〜になっている Has become [noun] / is now [noun]
    Verb (ability) 〜ようになる 〜ようになっている Has come to be able to / now does
    Verb (arrangement) 〜ことになる 〜ことになっている Has been decided that / is arranged that
    Negative (i-adj) 〜くなくなる 〜くなくなっている Has stopped being [adj]
    Negative (verb) 〜なくなる 〜なくなっている Has stopped doing / no longer does
    Past state — 〜くなっていた / 〜になっていた Had become / was (at a past time)
    Casual contraction — 〜てる All of the above, in casual speech
