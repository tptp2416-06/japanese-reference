    Extra Modest Expressions¶

    謙けん譲じょう語ご I (Kenjōgo) — Show Respect by Lowering Your Own Status¶

    1. Overview & Nuance¶

    Extra Modest Expressions (謙けん譲じょう語ご) are a category of 敬けい語ご used when speaking about your own actions — or the actions of your in-group — in order to show respect to the listener. Rather than elevating the listener directly (which is the job of Sonkeigo), humble speech works by lowering the speaker, creating vertical social distance that flatters the listener by comparison.

    The psychological intent is crucial: you are symbolically bowing through language. By choosing a verb form that means "I humbly do X" instead of simply "I do X," you communicate awareness of the other person's higher status — in age, position, or social role such as "customer."

    In Japanese society, respect is often expressed through the concept of distance. This isn't about being weak; it's about being culturally intelligent. Using these forms demonstrates that you understand the social hierarchy and your place within it.

    How it differs from 丁てい寧ねい語ご¶

    Form Purpose Direction of Respect
    Teineigo (-masu) General politeness; neutral distance Flat — simply polite
    Kenjōgo (Extra Modest) Lower yourself to elevate the listener ↓ Speaker goes down
    Sonkeigo (Honorific) Directly elevate the listener's actions ↑ Listener goes up

    A simple way to think about it: -masu is polite neutrality. Kenjōgo is a verbal bow. If Teineigo is business casual, Kenjōgo is a pressed suit.

    2. Conjugation Rules¶

    2a. Special Modest Verbs¶

    Several high-frequency verbs have completely irregular humble forms that must be memorized. These are the ones you will encounter most often in real life — in customer service, business, and formal introductions.

    Plain Verb Meaning Humble Form With -masu
    行いく / 来くる to go / to come 参まいる 参まいります
    いる to be / exist (animate) おる おります
    ある to exist (inanimate) ございます ございます
    です to be (copula) でございます でございます
    する to do 致いたす 致いたします
    ている ongoing / continuous ております ております
    言いう to say 申もうす 申もうします / 申もうし上あげます
    食たべる / 飲のむ / もらう to eat / drink / receive いただく いただきます
    知しっている to know 存ぞんじております 存ぞんじております
    見みる to see / look 拝はい見けんする 拝はい見けんします

    2b. The Productive Pattern: お + Verb Stem + する／いたす¶

    For verbs that don't have a special humble form, Japanese provides a productive template. Three steps:

    1. Take the -masu stem. Remove ます from the polite form.
      例: 書かきます → 書かき

    2. Add お before the stem. This prefix is a respect marker.
      例: お書かき

    3. Attach する (or the more formal いたす) after the stem.
      例: お書かきする → お書かきいたします

    Plain Verb -masu Stem Standard Humble Extra Formal Humble
    持もつ (to carry) 持もち お持もちする お持もちいたします
    送おくる (to send) 送おくり お送おくりする お送おくりいたします
    待まつ (to wait) 待まち お待まちする お待まちいたします
    呼よぶ (to call) 呼よび お呼よびする お呼よびいたします

    Sino-Japanese verbs use ご, not お

    For する verbs with Chinese-origin nouns like 連れん絡らくする, 説せつ明めいする, 案あん内ないする, the prefix changes from お to ご, and you skip the -masu stem logic entirely: ご + noun + する／いたす.

    例: ご連れん絡らくいたします = "I will (humbly) contact you."
    例: ご案あん内ない致いたします = "I will (humbly) guide you."

    3. Progressive Usage Ladder¶

    The following examples escalate in social weight. Notice how the same grammar is recycled across vastly different contexts — the grammar itself is fixed; what changes is who you're talking to.

    Level 1 — Strict / Formal (Interview, Ceremonial Self-Introduction)¶

    Example 1 · Self-Introduction

    田た中なかと申もうします。アメリカから参まいりました。
    "My name is Tanaka. I have come from America."

    Classic job interview opener. 申もうします replaces 言いいます; 参まいりました replaces 来きました.

    Example 2 · Interview Statement

    現げん在ざい、ABC会がい社しゃで働はたらいております。
    "I am currently working at ABC Company."

    おる is used in its て-form → 〜ており(ます), the humble equivalent of the progressive 〜ている.

    Example 3 · Showing Respect for Expertise

    お名な前まえは以い前ぜんから存ぞんじております。
    "I have known your name for some time."

    存ぞんじております is the humble form of 知しっています. Crucial in first meetings with someone famous or senior.

    Level 2 — Business Polite (Client, Superior, Customer Service)¶

    Example 4 · Phone / Email

    明あ日した、資し料りょうをお送おくりいたします。
    "I will send the documents tomorrow."

    Textbook example of the お + stem + いたす pattern. Standard in client email.

    Example 5 · Offering Help

    お荷に物もつ、お持もちしましょうか。
    "Shall I carry your luggage for you?"

    The humble pattern combines naturally with 〜ましょうか to make humble offers. Very common in hotels and stores.

    Example 6 · Customer Service

    少しょう々しょうお待まちください。担たん当とう者しゃにご連れん絡らくいたします。
    "Please wait a moment. I will contact the person in charge."

    Notice the ご + 連れん絡らく + いたす pattern for a Sino-Japanese verb.

    Example 7 · Referring to Your In-Group (Uchi-Soto in Action)

    社しゃ長ちょうの山やま田だはただいま席せきを外はずしております。
    "Our president, Yamada, is currently away from his desk."

    Even though Yamada is your boss, you use humble language about him to an outside customer. This is the uchi-soto rule in action — your boss belongs to your group relative to the outsider.

    Level 3 — Social Context: Humble Forms in Everyday Formal Speech¶

    When does humble speech appear outside the office?

    The full 致いたします register would feel stiff between friends. But 〜ております and お + stem + する appear in softer social contexts — introducing yourself at a PTA meeting, speaking to your partner's parents, or making a humble offer to a neighbor.

    Example 8 · Meeting a Partner's Parents

    東とう京きょうで英えい語ごを教おしえております。
    "I teach English in Tokyo."

    〜ております softens a casual fact into something appropriate for a respectful first meeting.

    Example 9 · Polite Social Offer

    タクシーをお呼よびしましょうか。
    "Shall I call a taxi for you?"

    Works when hosting older guests — more respectful than タクシーを呼よびましょうか, but not as stiff as business speech.

    Example 10 · Expressing Gratitude

    おいしいお茶ちゃをいただきました。ありがとうございます。
    "I received some delicious tea. Thank you very much."

    いただく is the quiet workhorse of humble speech — used constantly and perfectly natural even in moderately formal social situations.

    Example 11 · Awaiting a Reply (Closing an Email)

    連れん絡らくをお待まちしております。
    "I am humbly awaiting your contact."

    Uses て + おります (the modest て-form). Perfect for closing emails where you are waiting for a decision or reply.

    Example 12 · Acknowledging a Superior's Message

    そのメールは、もう拝はい見けんしました。
    "I have already humbly looked at that email."

    拝はい見けん is the humble version of 見みる. Shows you have paid careful attention to the superior's message.

    4. The Keigo Hierarchy — Side-by-Side¶

    Memorizing these triplets is one of the fastest ways to cement the logic of keigo. The golden rule: my actions → Kenjōgo. Their actions → Sonkeigo. Neutral / general description → Teineigo.

    Meaning Kenjōgo (Humble — self) Teineigo (Polite — neutral) Sonkeigo (Honorific — other)
    to be / exist おります います いらっしゃいます
    to go / come 参まいります 行いきます / 来きます いらっしゃいます
    to do 致いたします します なさいます
    to say 申もうします 言いいます おっしゃいます
    to eat / drink いただきます 食たべます / 飲のみます 召めし上あがります
    to see 拝はい見けんします 見みます ご覧らんになります
    to know 存ぞんじております 知しっています ご存ぞんじです
    to exist (inanimate) ございます あります N/A
    to be (copula) でございます です N/A

    5. The Mistake Monitor¶

    Social Pitfall — The Uchi-Soto Rule

    Humble speech is for you and your in-group (内うち) — never for the listener or their group (外そと). A common early mistake:

    ❌ お客きゃく様さまが参まいりました。 (lowers the customer — the opposite of your intent)
    ✅ お客きゃく様さまがいらっしゃいました。 (Sonkeigo — correct)

    Conversely, when referring to your own boss while speaking to an outside client, use humble speech for your boss — because your boss is in your group relative to the outsider. This is counterintuitive for learners from hierarchical cultures that always elevate the boss.

    Remember: use modest speech for your boss when talking about your boss to a client, but use honorific speech when talking directly to your boss.

    Grammatical Pitfall — Keigo Double-Dipping (二に重じゅう敬けい語ご)

    Do not stack humble and honorific structures, or apply the お〜する pattern on top of a verb that already has a special humble form.

    ❌ お申もうしいたします。 (申もうす is already humble — don't add お〜いたす on top)
    ✅ 申もうします。 / 申もうし上あげます。

    Another classic error — applying humble speech to the listener's action:

    ❌ 先せん生せいはコーヒーをいただきますか。 (uses humble "receive/drink" for the teacher)
    ✅ 先せん生せいはコーヒーを召めし上あがりますか。 (Sonkeigo — elevating the teacher's action)

    Grammar Pitfall — Using お〜する When a Special Verb Exists

    If a verb has an irregular humble form, use it — don't invent a pattern-based version.

    ❌ お食たべします。 (invented pattern — unnatural)
    ✅ いただきます。 (correct special humble form for eating/drinking/receiving)

    Social Mismatch — Using Keigo with Friends

    Extra Modest forms are for formal settings with a clear social gap. Using 致いたします or おります with close friends creates a cold, yoso-yososhii distance.

    Stick to casual or standard polite speech with peers.

    6. Practice Drill¶

    1. Translate to humble Japanese: "My name is Smith. I come from Canada." (formal self-introduction)
    2. Translate to humble Japanese: "I will send the report by email tomorrow." (to a client)
    3. Convert: 「今いま、会かい議ぎ室しつにいます。」(talking about your own location to a boss on the phone)
    4. Convert: 「駅えきまで車くるまで送おくりましょうか。」(humble offer to drive someone to the station)
    5. Spot and fix the error: 部ぶ長ちょうの鈴すず木きはただいまいらっしゃいません。(said to an outside client)

    7. Answer Key¶

    Click to reveal answers

    Question 1
    スミスと申もうします。カナダから参まいりました。
    言いう → 申もうす for stating one's own name; 来くる → 参まいる for humbly "coming." Past tense 参まいりました is correct because the arrival is a completed fact at the moment of speaking. Crucially, drop the -san from your own name when being modest to outsiders.

    Question 2
    明あ日した、レポートをメールでお送おくりいたします。
    送おくる has no special humble form, so use the productive pattern: お + 送おくり (stem) + いたす. Using いたす rather than する raises the register to business-appropriate. Both お送おくりします and お送おくりいたします are grammatically fine; the latter is more formal.

    Question 3
    今いま、会かい議ぎ室しつにおります。
    いる → おる. The -masu form is おります. Used for describing your own location or state — common on the phone.

    Question 4
    駅えきまで車くるまでお送おくりしましょうか。
    Classic お + stem + する pattern combined with 〜ましょうか to make a humble offer. The humble pattern works best when your action directly benefits the listener — which is exactly the case here.

    Question 5
    ❌ 部ぶ長ちょうの鈴すず木きはただいまいらっしゃいません。
    ✅ 部ぶ長ちょうの鈴すず木きはただいま席せきを外はずしております。/ おりません。
    Error: いらっしゃいません is Sonkeigo (honorific) — but Buchou Suzuki is your in-group relative to the outside client. You must switch to humble speech: おりません, or the more natural 席せきを外はずしております ("is away from his desk"). This is the uchi-soto rule at its trickiest — elevating your own boss to an outsider is a social faux pas in Japanese.
