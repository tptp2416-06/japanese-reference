    N4-Core expectation hazu nominalization

    〜はず — Supposed To Be The Case¶

    電車でんしゃはもう来きたはずなんだけど…¶

    JLPT Level¶

    JLPT Level — N4 Core / Lower N3 Extended

    〜はず itself is solid N4 — the grammar of logical expectation based on evidence, schedule, or reasoning. But it sits at the center of a web of N3-level expressions that modulate certainty, impossibility, and conclusion. Mastering はず means distinguishing it from べき (obligation), に違いない (subjective certainty), and わけだ (conclusion).

    Tier Pattern Description Example
    N5 — Foundation 〜と思う Personal opinion / prediction 雨あめが降ふると思おもう。
    N4 — Foundation 〜そうだ / 〜ようだ / 〜みたい Conjecture (hearsay, formal, casual) 雨あめみたいだ。
    N4 — Foundation 〜かもしれない Possibility 雨あめかもしれない。
    N4 — Core ← You are here 〜はずだ Logical expectation based on evidence or schedule もう来きたはずだ。
    N4 — Core 〜べきだ Obligation / strong social expectation 謝あやまるべきだ。
    N3 — Extended 〜はずがない Impossibility — "no way that…" 知しっているはずがない。
    N3 — Extended 〜に違いない Subjective certainty — "must be…" 彼かれがやったに違ちがいない。
    N3 — Extended 〜わけだ Conclusion / natural result 遅刻ちこくしたわけだ。
    N3 — Extended 〜わけがない Impossibility — "no way that…" 分わかるわけがない。
    N3 — Extended 〜つもりだ Intention — "plan to…" 行いくつもりだ。

    Quick Summary¶

    At a glance

    • 〜はずだ expresses a logical expectation: given the evidence, the schedule, or the normal course of events, something ought to be true. It is not a guess — it is a reasoned prediction.
    • The expectation can be frustrated: はずなのに marks the gap between what should be and what is. This is one of the most natural Japanese expressions of mild indignation or confusion.
    • はずがない (N3) pushes the logic to impossibility: "There is no basis for expecting X." It is calmer and more analytical than わけがない.
    • Related N3 expressions form a certainty spectrum: はずだ (logical expectation) → に違いない (strong subjective certainty) → わけだ (conclusion from reasoning) → べきだ (moral/social obligation).
    • The core logic: はず nominalizes the preceding clause and treats the expectation as a thing that can exist, be missing, or be violated.

    Core Meaning¶

    Core Rule

    Use 〜はずだ when you want to say that something ought to be true based on schedule, evidence, or logical inference — not because you want it to be true, but because the facts point to it.

    電車でんしゃはもう来きたはずだ。 The train should have arrived by now [based on the timetable].
    Literal: Train already came expectation is.
    Context: Looking at the clock, checking the schedule. The speaker is not guessing — they are following logical inference.
    彼かれは知しっているはずだ。 He ought to know [based on his position / prior information].
    Context: He was in the meeting, he read the email, he is the manager. The facts support the expectation.
    もう届とどいたはずなんだけど… It should have arrived already, but… [frustrated expectation].
    Context: The package was sent express. The tracking says delivered. But it's not here. The なんだけど trails off into confusion.
    そんなはずない。 That can't be right / There's no way that's the case.
    Context: Casual contraction of はずがない. Strong denial of a logical possibility.

    The Expectation & Certainty Landscape (N3–N4)¶

    Because the user explicitly asked for related grammar up to Lower N3, here is the mapped terrain. These expressions cluster around the same semantic space — what we think is true — but they differ in evidence type, social loading, and speaker stance.

    Expression Core Meaning Evidence Type Speaker Stance Certainty JLPT
    〜はずだ "Should be / supposed to be" Objective (schedule, logic, evidence) Detached, analytical High (but violable) N4
    〜はずがない "Can't be / no way" Absence of logical basis Analytical denial Absolute (negative) N3
    〜に違いない "Must be / undoubtedly" Subjective intuition, strong impression Personal conviction Very high N3
    〜わけだ "So that means / naturally" Logical conclusion from premises Explanatory High (conclusive) N3
    〜わけがない "No way / impossible" Absence of explanatory basis Dismissive / shocked Absolute (negative) N3
    〜べきだ "Should / ought to" Social rule, moral duty Prescriptive High (obligation) N4
    〜つもりだ "Intend to / plan to" Internal decision Self-directed High (volitional) N4
    〜かもしれない "Might / maybe" Insufficient evidence Cautious Low N4
    〜そうだ (conjecture) "Seems / looks like" Direct sensory evidence Observational Medium N4
    〜ようだ "Appears that" Formal inference Analytical, distant High N4
    〜だろう "Probably" General probability Assertive guess Medium N4

    How They Differ in Practice¶

    Scenario Natural Choice Why
    Train timetable says 9:00; it's 9:05. もう来きたはずだ Schedule-based expectation
    I see wet umbrellas; I didn't hear rain. 雨あめが降ふったに違ちがいない Sensory inference + conviction
    He missed the meeting; he wasn't informed. 知しらなかったわけだ Conclusion from facts
    He missed the meeting; he's the organizer. 知しっているはずがない Logical impossibility
    You broke it; apologize. 謝あやまるべきだ Social obligation
    I'm going tomorrow. 行いくつもりだ Personal intention

    はず vs. に違いない

    Both express high certainty, but はず is defeasible — it can be wrong without the speaker losing face. に違いない is committed — the speaker stakes their intuition on it. If it rains after you said 雨あめが降ふるはずだ, you were following the forecast. If you said 雨あめが降ふるに違ちがいない and it doesn't rain, your intuition was wrong.

    はずがない vs. わけがない

    Both mean "impossible." はずがない is analytical: "The facts don't support it." わけがない is emotional: "There's no explanation that could make that true." はずがない sounds like a calm rebuttal; わけがない sounds like shocked dismissal.

    In Real Life¶

    In Real Life

    Situation: ケン is waiting at the platform for the 18:15 express. It's 18:22. The train is not here. There was no announcement. He checks his phone — no delay alerts. He turns to another commuter and speaks with a slight laugh of disbelief.

    > 18時じ15分ふん発はつのはずなんだけど…

    "It's supposed to be the 18:15 departure, but…"

    Why はず fits here: Ken is not guessing the train's existence — he read the timetable. The はず encodes that the timetable created an expectation, and reality is violating it. The なんだけど softens the complaint into shared bewilderment.

    What makes this natural: The trailing ellipsis is functional — it invites the listener to supply the obvious conclusion ("…and yet it's not here"). This is classic Japanese indirection: the expectation is stated, the failure is implied.

    What はずなのに would add: If Ken said はずなのに来こない, the frustration would be sharper. The のに adds disappointed contrast. By using なんだけど instead, he keeps the tone collaborative rather than angry.

    How It Works¶

    はず is a noun meaning "expectation," "probability," or "reasonable assumption." Grammatically, it nominalizes the preceding clause and then predicates it with the copula (だ, です, だった).

    Layer 1 — Nominalization: The plain-form clause before はず becomes a noun phrase: 来きたはず = "the expectation of having come."

    Layer 2 — Copula attachment: だ or です treats this expectation as a factual claim: "The expectation exists [and I am asserting it]."

    Layer 3 — Frustration or denial: Adding がない removes the expectation; adding なのに contrasts it with reality; adding なんだけど softens it into hesitation.

    もう来きたはずだ。 It should have arrived by now [assertion].
    The expectation is presented as a standalone fact.
    もう来きたはずなのに。 It should have arrived by now, but… [frustrated contrast].
    The のに sets the expectation against a violated reality. The sentence trails off because the contradiction is obvious.
    もう来きたはずなんだけど… It should have arrived by now, but… [hesitant, seeking confirmation].
    The なんだけど invites the listener to resolve the gap. Less confrontational than のに.
    来きたはずがない。 There's no way it came [analytical impossibility].
    The expectation is denied at its root. Calm, logical rejection.

    Real Speech vs. Textbook Form¶

    Textbooks teach はずだ as "should." In real speech, it is more often the grammar of schedule anxiety, social bookkeeping, and mild outrage. Native speakers use it most frequently when reality fails to match records.

    Form Example Note
    Textbook (simple) 彼かれは来くるはずだ。 "He should come." — grammatically correct, slightly stiff
    Real speech (schedule) もう終おわったはずなんだけど… "It should be done already, but…" — the natural habitat of はず
    Real speech (denial) そんなはずないよ。 "That can't be." — casual contraction, daily use
    Real speech (retroactive) 知しってたはずじゃなかった？ "Wasn't that supposed to be known?" — retroactive questioning
    Real speech (softened) あるはずなんですけど… "It should be here, but…" — polite hesitation

    Grammar Analysis¶

    Function in Context¶

    • 18時じ15分ふん発はつのはず: The noun phrase 18時じ15分ふん発はつ modifies はず through の. The expectation is specifically about the departure time.
    • なんだけど: The explanatory の + だ + けど softens the assertion. The speaker is not declaring the timetable; they are sharing a puzzle.
    • Without はず: 18時じ15分ふん発はつなんだけど… would mean "It's the 18:15 departure, but…" — a factual description without the expectation layer. The はず adds the logical prediction that is being violated.
    • Without なんだけど: 18時じ15分ふん発はつのはずだ is a flat assertion. It sounds like Ken is reading the timetable aloud rather than sharing confusion.
    約束やくそくしたはずなのに、来こなかった。 Even though we were supposed to have made plans, he didn't come.
    Context: The speaker remembers agreeing to meet. The はずなのに frames the no-show as a logical violation, not just a disappointment.

    Construction¶

    Preceding Form Attachment Example
    Verb (plain present) verb + はずだ 行いくはずだ
    Verb (plain past) past + はずだ 行いったはずだ
    i-Adjective (plain) adj + はずだ 高たかいはずだ
    な-Adjective stem + な + はずだ 静しずかなはずだ
    Noun noun + の + はずだ 月曜日げつようびのはずだ
    Noun (past state) noun + だった + はずだ 学生がくせいだったはずだ

    No だ before はず with nouns/na-adjectives

    Like のに and ので, nouns and na-adjectives require な (or の for nouns) before はず. 学生がくせいはずだ is ungrammatical; 学生がくせいのはずだ is correct.

    Negative and Extended Forms¶

    Form Pattern Example Meaning
    Negative expectation はずがない / はずはない 知しっているはずがない No way that…
    Past frustrated はずだった 来きたはずだった Was supposed to have come
    Explanatory softener はずなんだけど あるはずなんだけど Should be, but…
    Contrastive はずなのに 終おわったはずなのに Even though it should be finished…
    Retroactive はずじゃなかった？ 行いったはずじゃなかった？ Wasn't it supposed to have gone?
    Casual denial そんなはずない そんなはずないよ No way / That can't be

    Japanese Mindset¶

    Japanese social harmony depends heavily on shared expectations being met. When they are not, the violation is often expressed indirectly. はず is the primary grammar for this: it allows the speaker to state what should have happened without directly accusing reality of being wrong.

    The form also reveals a cultural preference for evidence-based interaction. Saying 来くるはずだ is safer than saying 来くると思おもう because the former anchors the prediction in external facts (timetable, prior agreement) rather than personal opinion.

    • Expectation as social contract. When you say 約束やくそくしたはず, you are not just describing memory — you are invoking a shared commitment. The はず makes the agreement feel objective and binding.
    • Indirect complaint. はずなのに lets the speaker complain without complaining. The grammar does the accusing: "The facts promised X, yet Y occurred."
    • Analytical face-saving. If the expectation is wrong, the speaker can retreat to "The schedule said so" rather than "I was wrong." はず externalizes the prediction.

    Nuance Comparison¶

    Grammar Point Core Meaning Evidence / Basis Emotional Register JLPT
    〜はずだ Supposed to be / expected Objective (schedule, logic, facts) Analytical, slightly frustrated if violated N4
    〜はずがない Can't be / no way Absence of logical basis Calm denial N3
    〜に違いない Must be / undoubtedly Subjective intuition, sensory inference Confident, committed N3
    〜わけだ So that means / naturally Logical conclusion from context Explanatory, clarifying N3
    〜わけがない Impossible / no way Absence of explanatory basis Shocked, dismissive N3
    〜べきだ Should / ought to Social rule, moral duty Prescriptive, judgmental N4
    〜つもりだ Intend to / plan to Internal decision Self-directed, volitional N4
    〜かもしれない Might / maybe Insufficient evidence Cautious, hedging N4
    〜そうだ (conjecture) Seems / appears Direct sensory evidence Observational N4
    〜ようだ Appears that Formal inference Analytical, distant N4

    はず vs. べき: Both can translate to "should." はず is about prediction (what will be). べき is about prescription (what must be done). 来くるはずだ = "I expect him to come." 来くるべきだ = "He ought to come [morally]."

    はず vs. つもり: はず is external expectation; つもり is internal intention. 行いくはずだ = "Circumstances suggest he will go." 行いくつもりだ = "I plan to go."

    はずがない vs. わけがない: Both deny possibility. はずがない sounds like a calm fact-check: "The data doesn't support it." わけがない sounds like emotional rejection: "That makes no sense!"

    Register and Formality¶

    The expectation grammar is register-neutral, but the packaging shifts.

    もう来きたはずなんだけど…

    "It should be here by now, but…"

    Shift: Explanatory の + だ + けど. Standard casual hesitation.

    そんなはずないよ。

    "That can't be right."

    Shift: Omitted が. そんなはずがない contracted to そんなはずない. Very natural in speech.

    もう届とどいたはずなのですが…

    "It should have arrived already, however…"

    Shift: のですが replaces なんだけど. Polite but retains the expectation-frustration structure.

    本日ほんじつお届とどけのはずでございましたが…

    "It was supposed to be delivered today, however…"

    Shift: Full keigo wrapping. お届とどけ (humble noun), はずでございましたが (polite copula + contrastive). The core はず remains.

    Examples¶

    電車でんしゃはもう来きたはずだ。 The train should have arrived by now [timetable logic].
    Context: Checking the clock against the schedule. The speaker trusts the system.
    彼かれは知しっているはずなのに、黙だまっている。 He ought to know, and yet he's staying silent.
    Context: Frustrated contrast. The はずなのに marks the gap between expected knowledge and actual behavior.
    そんなはずないよ。 That can't be right / No way.
    Context: Casual, immediate denial of a claim. The はずがない is contracted in speech.
    約束やくそくしたはずなんだけど… We were supposed to have made plans, but…
    Context: The speaker remembers the agreement and is confused by the violation. The なんだけど invites listener collaboration.
    終おわったはずだった。 It was supposed to have been finished.
    Context: Retroactive realization of failed expectation. The past copula だった anchors the expectation to a previous time frame.
    あるはずなんですが、見みつからない。 It should be here, but I can't find it.
    Context: Polite service interaction. The speaker is searching for something that logic says exists.
    来きたはずじゃなかった？ Wasn't it supposed to have come?
    Context: Seeking confirmation of a shared expectation. The retroactive question format.
    安やすいはずだ。 It should be cheap [based on market logic].
    Context: Predicting price based on category or prior knowledge. Analytical rather than hopeful.

    Common Mistakes¶

    1. Using はず for pure speculation without evidence¶

    Misuse

    明日あしたは晴はれるはずだ。 (said with no forecast, just hope)

    ~If the speaker has no basis, this sounds like false confidence. はず requires evidence. For pure hope, use といいな or かもしれない.~

    Correct

    予報よほうでは明日あしたは晴はれるはずだ。 (Based on the forecast, it should be sunny.)

    2. Confusing はずだ with べきだ¶

    Obligation vs. prediction error

    彼かれは来くるべきだ。 (meaning "I predict he will come")

    ~来くるべきだ means "He ought to come [morally/socially]." It is a judgment, not a prediction. For prediction, use 来くるはずだ.~

    Correct

    彼かれは来くるはずだ。 (I expect him to come.) 彼かれは来くるべきだ。 (He should come [and it's wrong if he doesn't].)

    3. Using はずがない when わけがない is more natural¶

    Register mismatch

    分わかるはずがない！ (shouted in shock)

    ~Grammatically fine, but はずがない is calm and analytical. In emotional shock, 分わかるわけがない！ is more natural.~

    Correct

    分わかるわけがない！ (There's no way I could understand! — emotional)

    4. Omitting の/な before はず with nouns/na-adjectives¶

    Grammatical error

    学生がくせいはずだ。

    ~Nouns require の: 学生がくせいのはずだ. Na-adjectives require な: 静しずかなはずだ.~

    Correct

    学生がくせいのはずだ。

    Hidden Points¶

    • はずなんだけど is the sound of schedule anxiety. In train stations, offices, and delivery tracking, this phrase is the background radiation of Japanese daily life. It is not dramatic; it is routine expectation management.
    • そんなはずない is often used to reject compliments. すごいね！→ そんなはずないよ。 ("No way I'm amazing!") This is humble deflection encoded in expectation grammar.
    • はずだった carries retroactive disappointment. It is stronger than はずだ because it anchors the failed expectation to a specific past moment. 来きたはずだった = "It was supposed to come [and I was counting on it then]."
    • はず can be used for social bookkeeping. 約束やくそくしたはず is not just memory — it is a gentle accusation. The speaker is saying "The record shows we agreed."
    • はずがない softens direct contradiction. Saying 違ちがう (wrong) is blunt. Saying そんなはずがない (that can't be the case) wraps the denial in logic, making it more palatable.
    • The noun はず historically meant "arrowhead" and by extension "target / expectation." The etymological image is of aiming at a target. Missing the target (はずれ) is the opposite concept — which is why はずがない means "no target to aim at," i.e., impossible.

    Learner Stage¶

    Stage What It Looks Like
    Now — N4 Produce はずだ correctly with all predicate types; use はずなのに for frustrated contrast; distinguish はず from べき and つもり; recognize はずがない as impossibility
    Developing — N4→N3 Deploy はず naturally in schedule and social contexts; choose between はずがない and わけがない based on emotional register; integrate はず into the broader certainty spectrum (に違いない, わけだ, etc.)
    Automatic — N3→N2 はず arrives as a logical expectation marker before conscious analysis. You hear 来きたはずなのに and immediately register "frustrated schedule adherence." The distinction between はず and に違いない is intuitive.
    Mastery You control the full certainty spectrum with precision — using はず for evidence-based prediction, に違いない for gut conviction, わけだ for conclusions, べき for moral prescription, and はずがない vs. わけがない to calibrate denial from analytical to emotional. You also know when trailing なんだけど softens and when のに sharpens.
