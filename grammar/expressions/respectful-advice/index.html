    N4-Upper keigo respectful-language sonkeigo

    Respectful Request — お/ご〜ください¶

    ご覧らんください。 / お座すわりください。¶

    JLPT Level¶

    JLPT Level — Upper N4 / Lower N3

    The pattern itself is N4 — a core 尊敬語そんけいご (sonkeigo / respectful language) structure. But the calibration of when to use it versus neutral 〜てください or directive 〜なさい, and the wago/kango prefix distinction, push it into lower-N3 territory for active production. Service-industry fluency depends on this pattern.

    Tier Pattern Description Example
    N5 — Foundation 〜てください Neutral request — "please do X" 座すわってください。
    N4 — Foundation 〜なさい Directive — "do X" (authority to subordinate) 座すわりなさい。
    N4 — Core ← You are here お/ご〜ください Respectful request — elevates the listener's action お座すわりください。
    N4 — Foundation お/ご〜する Humble — lowers speaker's action お座すわりします。
    N3 — Extended お/ご〜いただけますか Humble request for listener's action お座すわりいただけますか。
    N2 — Awareness お/ご〜になられる Double keigo (often avoided) お座すわりになられる。

    Quick Summary¶

    At a glance

    • お/ご〜ください is a 尊敬語そんけいご (sonkeigo) pattern: it elevates the listener's action while politely requesting it. It does not mean "please" in a casual sense — it means "I humbly ask that you, who are above me, do this elevated action."
    • お attaches to 和語わご (wago / native Japanese) verb stems: 座すわる → お座すわりください, 休やすむ → お休やすみください.
    • ご attaches to 漢語かんご (kango / Sino-Japanese) verb stems or nouns: 利用りようする → ご利用りようください, 覧らんする → ご覧らんください.
    • This pattern is customer-facing, service-industry, and upward-directed. You use it when speaking to guests, clients, superiors, or anyone whose status you want to honor.
    • It is not the humble form. お座すわりします (I will sit — humble) and お座すわりください (Please sit — respectful) are opposite social directions. Confusing them is a major keigo error.

    Core Meaning¶

    Core Rule

    Use お/ご〜ください when you want to request that someone of higher status or someone you are serving perform an action, while grammatically elevating that action. The request is polite but structurally framed as an honor being granted to you by their action.

    お座すわりください。 Please be seated [respectful, to a guest or superior].
    Literal: Humble-prefix + sit-stem + please-do [respectful].
    Context: Restaurant host to customer. Doctor to patient. Receptionist to visitor. The host elevates the act of sitting.
    ご覧らんください。 Please take a look [respectful, to a client or superior].
    Literal: Humble-prefix + see-stem + please-do [respectful].
    Context: Shop staff showing merchandise. Presenter indicating a slide. The act of looking is elevated.
    ご確認かくにんください。 Please confirm [respectful, business correspondence].
    Literal: Humble-prefix + confirm-stem + please-do [respectful].
    Context: Email to a client. The writer elevates the client's act of checking.
    お召めし上あがりください。 Please partake / Please eat [ultra-respectful, service].
    Literal: Humble-prefix + eat-stem + please-do [respectful].
    Context: High-end restaurant. 召めし上あがる is the respectful verb for "eat"; adding お/ご〜ください double-elevates.

    In Real Life¶

    In Real Life

    Situation: ユミ works at the front desk of a hotel. A guest has just completed check-in. She gestures toward the elevator bank and speaks.

    > エレベーターはあちらでございます。どうぞ、お掛かけになりまして、お休やすみください。

    "The elevator is over there. Please make yourself comfortable and rest."

    Why お休やすみください fits here: Yumi could have said 休やすんでください — grammatically correct, neutrally polite. But that would treat the guest as a peer. お休やすみください elevates the guest's rest into an honored action. The お marks the verb as wago; the ください frames it as a respectful request.

    What makes this natural: The pattern appears in the standard hospitality script. It is not a spontaneous sentence — it is a social ritual encoded in grammar. The guest expects this level of elevation; anything less feels like a downgrade.

    The どうぞ that precedes: どうぞ ("by all means") is a common partner for お/ご〜ください. It adds warm invitation to the respectful structure. どうぞお座すわりください is a standard pairing.

    How It Works¶

    The pattern is built from three layers: prefix + stem + request marker. Each layer carries social meaning.

    Layer 1 — Prefix (お vs ご): The prefix elevates the following word. お is for native Japanese vocabulary; ご is for Sino-Japanese. This is not optional — using the wrong prefix is immediately recognizable as incorrect.

    Layer 2 — Stem: The verb is reduced to its stem form (the form used before ます). For する verbs, the noun/stem is used (利用りようする → 利用りよう). This stripped form is then "wrapped" in the honorific prefix.

    Layer 3 — ください: This is the imperative form of くださる, the respectful verb "to give (to me/someone below)." By using ください, the speaker frames the action as something the honored listener will "give" by performing it. The social geometry is: "You will do this for me, and I am grateful."

    座すわってください。 Please sit [neutral request].
    The listener is a peer or the situation is casual. No elevation.
    お座すわりください。 Please be seated [respectful request].
    The listener is a guest or superior. Their sitting is elevated.
    お座すわりします。 I will be seated [humble].
    The speaker lowers their own action. Social direction is reversed.

    Real Speech vs. Textbook Form¶

    Textbooks often present お/ご〜ください as "please do X." In real speech, it is a service-industry default and an upward-deference marker. It is less common in peer conversation than in structured service contexts.

    Form Example Note
    Textbook (neutral) 窓まどを開あけてください。 "Please open the window" — classroom example
    Real speech (respectful) 窓まどをお開あけください。 "Please open the window" — hotel staff to guest
    Real speech (humble request) 窓まどをお開あけいただけますか。 "Could you open the window for me?" — even more deferential
    Real speech (directive) 窓まどを開あけなさい。 "Open the window" — parent to child

    Grammar Analysis¶

    Function in Context¶

    • お座すわりください: 座すわる (to sit) is wago, so it takes お. The stem is 座すわり. ください elevates the imperative.
    • ご覧らんください: 覧らんする (to view) is kango, so it takes ご. The stem/noun is 覧らん. ください elevates the imperative.
    • ご利用りようください: 利用りようする (to use) is kango, so it takes ご. The stem/noun is 利用りよう.
    • お召めし上あがりください: 召めし上あがる (to eat — respectful verb) is wago, so it takes お. The stem is 召めし上あがり. This is already a respectful verb; adding お/ご〜ください makes it ultra-respectful.
    • The subject of these sentences is always the listener (you), but it is never stated. The grammar of 尊敬語そんけいご implies "you, the honored person."
    • Without the prefix: 座すわりください is ungrammatical. The お/ご is required for the respectful frame.
    お掛かけください。 Please sit down [hanging / seated position].
    Context: 掛かける (to hang / to set) in お掛かけになる means "to seat oneself." お掛かけください is a slightly more formal variant of お座すわりください in some service contexts.

    The wago/kango boundary

    Most errors involve applying お to kango or ご to wago. If the word can be written in pure hiragana or has native Japanese etymology, it likely takes お. If it is a two-kanji compound or clearly Sino-Japanese, it takes ご.

    Some words are lexicalized chunks

    ご覧らん, ご注意ちゅうい, お待まち are so common they should be learned as complete units rather than assembled from rules each time.

    Do not use お/ご〜ください for your own actions

    This is 尊敬語そんけいご (respectful), not 謙譲語けんじょうご (humble). It points upward at the listener. Using it for yourself (お座すわりください when you are the one sitting) is a social-direction error.

    Japanese Mindset¶

    Japanese keigo is not "polite Japanese" — it is a social positioning system. お/ご〜ください does more than soften a command. It restructures the relationship between speaker and listener.

    When a hotel clerk says お座すわりください, they are not merely saying "Sit down." They are saying: "You are a guest of high standing, and your act of sitting is an honor. I am grateful that you will do it." The grammar encodes an entire service philosophy.

    • Elevation as hospitality. The prefix お/ご lifts the listener's action out of the everyday. Sitting becomes "being seated." Looking becomes "viewing." Eating becomes "partaking." The everyday is ceremonialized.
    • Request as gift. ください derives from くださる (to give from above). The listener is framed as giving the action to the speaker. Even when the listener is clearly following an instruction, the grammar treats them as bestowing a favor.
    • The wago/kango split as cultural taxonomy. The fact that native and Sino-Japanese vocabulary take different prefixes reveals how deeply Japanese grammar encodes the origin of words. The particle system respects etymology.

    Nuance Comparison¶

    Grammar Point Core Meaning Social Direction Register Trigger Context JLPT
    お/ご〜ください "Please do X" [respectful] Elevates listener Formal, service, upward Speaking to guests, clients, superiors N4
    〜てください "Please do X" [neutral] Neutral request Polite, general Peers, general requests N5
    〜なさい "Do X" [directive] Speaker directs listener Authority to subordinate Parent to child, teacher to student N4
    お/ご〜する "I do X" [humble] Lowers speaker Humble Describing one's own actions to superiors N4
    お/ご〜いただけますか "Could you do X?" [humble request] Humble request for listener's action Ultra-polite Asking superiors for permission/action N3
    お/ご〜なさい "Do X" [respectful directive] Elevated directive Formal, slightly old-fashioned Formal instruction, written N4
    〜ていただけますか "Could I receive X?" [humble] Receiving frame Polite Requesting someone do something for you N3

    お/ご〜ください vs 〜てください: Both mean "please do." The difference is elevation. 座すわってください is a polite request to a peer. お座すわりください is a respectful request to a guest. Using 〜てください with a hotel guest sounds slightly brusque; using お/ご〜ください with a close friend sounds oddly ceremonial.

    お/ご〜ください vs お/ご〜する: These are opposite directions. お座すわりください = "You, please sit" (respectful). お座すわりします = "I will sit" (humble). The prefix is the same; the social direction is reversed by the main verb (ください vs する).

    Alternatives¶

    〜てください (neutral request)¶

    The standard polite request. No elevation.
    Example 座すわってください。
    English "Please sit."
    Intent Polite but neutral. The listener is a peer or the context is casual.
    vs. お/ご〜ください Removes the ceremonial elevation. Appropriate for colleagues, friends, or everyday service.

    When to use this instead

    Use 〜てください when speaking to peers, subordinates in casual contexts, or when the situation does not require service-industry formality.

    〜なさい (directive)¶

    Authority speaking downward. Not polite in the deferential sense — it is a command wrapped in grammar.
    Example 座すわりなさい。
    English "Sit down."
    Intent Directive. The speaker has authority.
    vs. お/ご〜ください なさい commands; お/ご〜ください requests. Never use なさい with guests or superiors.

    お/ご〜いただけますか (humble request)¶

    Asks the listener to "give" the action in an even more humble frame.
    Example お座すわりいただけますか。
    English "Could you please be seated?"
    Intent The speaker positions themselves as receiving the listener's action. More humble than お座すわりください.
    vs. お/ご〜ください いただけますか adds a receiving frame (謙譲語けんじょうご layer on top of 尊敬語そんけいご). Appropriate for very high-status listeners or formal written requests.

    お/ご〜なさい (respectful directive)¶

    A middle ground between request and directive. Slightly old-fashioned.
    Example お休やすみなさい。
    English "Do rest." / "Good night."
    Intent Elevated directive. Common in set phrases (お休やすみなさい = good night). Less common for spontaneous requests.
    vs. お/ご〜ください なさい is more decisive; ください is more inviting.

    Register and Formality¶

    The respectful request is formal by definition, but its warmth can be modulated.

    お座すわりください。

    "Please be seated."

    Shift: Used with a close friend. Sounds mock-formal or sarcastic unless the context is genuinely ceremonial.

    どうぞ、お座すわりください。

    "Please, be seated."

    Shift: Standard service-industry warmth. The どうぞ adds invitation; the お座すわりください provides the elevation.

    ご確認かくにんのほど、よろしくお願ねがいいたします。

    "We kindly ask that you please confirm."

    Shift: ご確認かくにん + のほど (formal periphrasis) + よろしくお願ねがいいたします (humble closing). The core pattern ご確認かくにんください is embedded in a more elaborate formal structure.

    どうぞ、お召めし上あがりくださいませ。

    "Please, do partake."

    Shift: ください + ませ adds historical/formal flourish. Found in luxury ryokan, traditional restaurants, and theatrical speech.

    Examples¶

    お待まちください。 Please wait [respectful, to a client].
    Context: Receptionist asking a guest to wait. 待まつ → お待まち. The wait is elevated.
    ご確認かくにんください。 Please confirm [respectful, business email].
    Context: Standard closing request in business correspondence. 確認かくにんする → ご確認かくにん.
    ご利用りようの際さいは、お気きを付つけください。 When using [this], please be careful [respectful].
    Context: Instruction manual or public notice. Both ご利用りよう (kango) and お気きを付つけ (wago) use the pattern.
    お体からだにお気きを付つけください。 Please take care of yourself [respectful, warm].
    Context: Farewell, especially to someone unwell or departing. Highly lexicalized expression of care.
    ご了承りょうしょうください。 Please acknowledge / Please understand [respectful, formal].
    Context: Asking for acceptance of terms, apologies, or inconvenient facts. Business and legal contexts.
    お読よみいただけますでしょうか。 Might you be able to read this [humble request]?
    Context: Ultra-polite alternative to お読よみください. Adds いただけますか (receiving frame).
    どうぞ、お掛かけください。 Please have a seat [respectful, warm].
    Context: お掛かけになる is the respectful verb for sitting; お掛かけください is a slightly more formal variant of お座すわりください in some hospitality contexts.
    ご注意ちゅういください。 Please be careful / Please note [respectful, public].
    Context: Announcements, signs, warnings. The listener's attention is elevated.
    お帰かえりください。 Please return [respectful, rare].
    Context: More commonly お帰かえりくださいませ in very formal hospitality, or simply お気きを付つけてお帰かえりください. Direct "please go home" can sound dismissive, so this form is used cautiously.

    Common Mistakes¶

    1. Using お with kango or ご with wago¶

    Incorrect prefix

    お利用りようください。

    ~利用りよう is kango (Sino-Japanese). It requires ご, not お.~

    Correct

    ご利用りようください。

    Incorrect prefix

    ご座すわりください。

    ~座すわる is wago (native Japanese). It requires お, not ご.~

    Correct

    お座すわりください。

    2. Confusing 尊敬語そんけいご (respectful) with 謙譲語けんじょうご (humble)¶

    Social direction error

    お座すわりします → said to a guest as an invitation to sit.

    ~This means "I will sit" (humble). You have just announced that you, the staff member, will be sitting. The guest is left standing.~

    Correct

    お座すわりください。 = "Please sit" (respectful, to guest). お座すわりします。 = "I will sit" (humble, about self).

    3. Using お/ご〜ください in casual peer contexts¶

    Register mismatch

    友達ともだちに「お座すわりください」

    "Please be seated, friend." — Sounds mock-formal or sarcastic. Friends say 座すわって or 座すわってよ.

    Appropriate

    座すわって。 / 座すわってください。 (neutral/casual polite)

    4. Using polite ます stem instead of plain stem¶

    Stem error

    お座すわりますください。

    ~Ungrammatical. The stem is 座すわり, not 座すわります.~

    Correct

    お座すわりください。

    Hidden Points¶

    • お/ご〜ください can be used in written public notices without a specific addressee. ご注意ちゅういください on a sign addresses the general public. The respectful frame treats any reader as an honored guest.
    • The pattern is often shortened in very casual respectful speech. A host might say どうぞ、お座すわり — dropping ください but retaining the elevation. This is warm and intimate rather than incomplete.
    • Some verbs resist the pattern. Verbs that are inherently humble or already respectful may sound redundant or odd with お/ご〜ください. いらっしゃる (respectful "to go/come") does not take お/ご〜ください; you say いらっしゃってください or simply お越こしください.
    • Double keigo (お/ご〜なさいます / お/ご〜なられる) exists but is often avoided. お座すわりになられます is grammatically possible but sounds excessive to many native speakers. It is used in ultra-formal contexts but can feel like overcorrection.
    • お/ご〜くださいませ is a historical/formal variant. Adding ませ (imperative of ます) creates an even more elevated tone: お座すわりくださいませ. This survives in luxury hospitality and period drama.
    • The pattern can be used sarcastically or aggressively. With the wrong intonation, お帰かえりください can mean "Please leave" (dismissive). The respectful grammar wrapped around a rejection creates social dissonance — a classic Japanese passive-aggressive move.
    • お待まちください is often used when the speaker is actually making the listener wait. The grammar treats the wait as an honored action the listener is performing, which softens the imposition. This is keigo as emotional management.

    Learner Stage¶

    Stage What It Looks Like
    Now — N4 Produce お/ご〜ください correctly with common verbs; distinguish wago/kango for prefix selection; avoid confusing with お/ご〜する (humble); use in service-industry contexts
    Developing — N4→N3 Deploy naturally in customer-facing role-play; choose between 〜てください and お/ご〜ください based on social distance; understand お/ご〜いただけますか as a more humble alternative
    Automatic — N3→N2 The pattern arrives as a single service-industry unit. You no longer assemble it from parts. You hear ご確認かくにんください and immediately register "business email to client."
    Mastery You control the full request spectrum: 〜て (casual), 〜てください (neutral), お/ご〜ください (respectful), お/ご〜なさい (directive-respectful), お/ご〜いただけますか (humble request). You know when お座すわりください is warm hospitality and when it is social distancing. You never confuse お座すわりください with お座すわりします.
