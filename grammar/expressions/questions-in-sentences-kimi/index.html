    N4-Upper embedded-questions kadouka nominalization

    Embedded Questions — 〜か / 〜かどうか¶

    彼かれが来くるかどうか、分わからない。¶

    JLPT Level¶

    JLPT Level — Upper N4 / Lower N3

    The basic pattern of embedding a plain question with 〜か is N4. But the full system — distinguishing 〜か from 〜かどうか, knowing when particles drop, and controlling the scope of uncertainty — is solidly lower N3. Native speakers make these choices automatically; learners often stumble over whether to include どうか or whether が should stay.

    Tier Pattern Description Example
    N5 — Foundation Plain questions Standalone questions with か 彼かれは来きますか？
    N4 — Foundation 〜か (embedded) Embedding a question as a noun clause 彼かれが来くるか分わからない。
    N4 — Core ← You are here 〜かどうか Explicit yes/no embedding — "whether or not" 彼かれが来くるかどうか分わからない。
    N3 — Extended Particle dropping が/は often omitted in embedded clauses 彼かれ来くるかどうか… (very casual)
    N3 — Extended Double embedding 〜か〜か + どうか in lists 行いくか行いかないか、分わからない。
    N2 — Awareness 〜かのように "As if…" — hypothetical embedding 知しっているかのように話はなす。

    Quick Summary¶

    At a glance

    • 〜か turns a question into a noun-like clause that can be inserted into a larger sentence — as the object of 知しる, 分わかる, 聞きく, 確認かくにんする, etc.
    • 〜かどうか explicitly embeds a yes/no question, marking both alternatives: "whether [X] or not." It is clearer, more explicit, and often preferred in formal contexts.
    • With question words (何なん, 誰だれ, どこ, etc.), only 〜か is used — never 〜かどうか. 何なんを食たべるかどうか知しらない is ungrammatical because "what" is not a yes/no question.
    • With yes/no questions, both 〜か and 〜かどうか work, but the nuance differs: 〜か is neutral and compact; 〜かどうか is explicit about the uncertainty between two outcomes.
    • Particles が and は are often dropped in embedded clauses, especially in casual speech. 彼かれが来くるか becomes 彼かれ来くるか in fast conversation.

    Core Meaning¶

    Core Rule

    Use 〜か to embed any question (wh- or yes/no) into a larger statement. Use 〜かどうか when you want to explicitly mark that a yes/no question is being embedded — emphasizing the uncertainty between the positive and negative outcome.

    彼かれが来くるか分わからない。 I don't know if he'll come [embedded yes/no].
    Literal: He come-question don't-know.
    Context: Neutral embedding. The か alone carries the yes/no ambiguity.
    彼かれが来くるかどうか分わからない。 I don't know whether he'll come or not [explicit yes/no].
    Literal: He come-question-or-not don't-know.
    Context: The どうか makes the two alternatives explicit. Slightly more formal and clearer than か alone.
    何なんが起おきたか知しりたい。 I want to know what happened [embedded wh-question].
    Literal: What happened-question want-to-know.
    Context: Wh-question embedding. Only か is possible here — かどうか cannot follow 何なん.
    日本にっぽん語ごが話はなせるかどうか聞きいてください。 Please ask whether [they] can speak Japanese or not.
    Literal: Japanese speak-can-question-or-not ask-please.
    Context: Explicit yes/no embedding in a request. The かどうか makes the binary nature of the inquiry clear.

    In Real Life¶

    In Real Life

    Situation: ユイ is organizing a weekend picnic. She needs to know if her friend ケン is coming because she has to buy food. She texts another friend who might know.

    ケンが来くるかどうか、分わからない？

    "Do you know whether Ken is coming or not?"

    Why かどうか fits here: Yui could have said ケンが来くるか分わからない — grammatically fine, but slightly less explicit. The かどうか makes the binary nature of the uncertainty clear: he either comes or he doesn't, and she needs to know which. The comma pause after かどうか creates a small space of suspense before the main question.

    What the どうか adds: It transforms a vague "if" into a clear "whether or not." This is especially useful when the listener needs to understand that both outcomes matter — not just the positive one.

    What makes this natural: In planning contexts, かどうか is extremely common because plans require binary decisions. The explicitness prevents misunderstanding.

    How It Works¶

    Embedding a question in Japanese is structurally simple but pragmatically layered. The question clause is nominalized by か (and optionally どうか), then placed in a grammatical slot that normally takes a noun — typically as the object of a verb like 知しる, 分わかる, 聞きく, 確認かくにんする, 決きめる, or 考かんがえる.

    Layer 1 — The embedded clause: Take a plain-form question (not the polite 〜ますか form) and replace the question mark with か. 彼かれは来きますか？ → 彼かれが来くるか.

    Layer 2 — The explicit marker (optional): For yes/no questions, add どうか after か to make both alternatives explicit: 彼かれが来くるかどうか.

    Layer 3 — The main clause: The embedded clause functions as the object (or sometimes subject) of the main predicate. 彼かれが来くるかどうか + 分わからない.

    彼かれが来くるか Whether he comes [compact]
    The か alone carries the uncertainty. The listener understands it's a yes/no question from context.
    彼かれが来くるかどうか Whether he comes or not [explicit]
    The どうか spells out the negative alternative. More formal, more precise, more planning-friendly.

    Real Speech vs. Textbook Form¶

    Textbooks often present 〜か and 〜かどうか as interchangeable for yes/no questions. In real speech, the choice is meaningful: 〜か is neutral and compact; 〜かどうか is explicit and often carries a sense of "I need a definitive answer."

    Form Example Note
    Textbook (neutral) 彼かれが来くるか分わからない。 Standard example — grammatically correct, fully natural
    Textbook (explicit) 彼かれが来くるかどうか分わからない。 Standard example — grammatically correct, fully natural
    Real speech (wh-embedded) 何なんが起おきたか知しりたい。 Wh-question — only か possible
    Real speech (particle drop) 彼かれ来くるかどうか… Casual particle drop — が omitted
    Real speech (double form) 行いくか行いかないか、決きめていない。 Both alternatives stated explicitly

    Grammar Analysis¶

    Function in Context¶

    • ケンが来くるかどうか is the embedded clause functioning as the object of 分わからない. The comma after かどうか is optional but common — it marks the boundary between the embedded question and the main predicate.
    • 分わからない is the main predicate — "don't know." It requires a noun-like object: "I don't know [the answer to this question]."
    • The どうか explicitly marks the negative alternative: "come or not-come." Without it, the negative alternative is implied but not stated.
    • The question clause uses plain form (来くる), not polite form (来きます). Embedding 〜ますか is ungrammatical: ケンが来きますか分わからない is wrong.
    • Without かどうか: ケンが来くるか分わからない is softer and more compact. It doesn't emphasize the binary nature as strongly. In casual speech, this is often preferred.
    これが正ただしいか確認かくにんしてください。 Please check whether this is correct.
    Literal: This correct-question confirm-please.
    Context: 〜か alone is sufficient because the context makes the yes/no nature obvious. Adding どうか would be redundant but not wrong.

    Construction¶

    Question Type Embedded Form Example かどうか Possible?
    Yes/no (plain) verb-plain + か 来くるか Optional
    Yes/no (explicit) verb-plain + かどうか 来くるかどうか Yes
    Yes/no (negative explicit) verb-plain + か + verb-nai + か 来くるか来こないか Yes (literary)
    Wh-question (何なん) 何なん + predicate + か 何なんを食たべるか No
    Wh-question (誰だれ) 誰だれ + predicate + か 誰だれが来くるか No
    Wh-question (どこ) どこ + predicate + か どこに行いくか No
    Wh-question (いつ) いつ + predicate + か いつ帰かえるか No
    Wh-question (どれ) どれ + predicate + か どれがいいか No
    Wh-question (なぜ) なぜ + predicate + か なぜ怒おこったか No

    かどうか is only for yes/no

    かどうか cannot follow question words (何なん, 誰だれ, どこ, etc.) because those are not yes/no questions. 何なんを食たべるかどうか知しらない is ungrammatical.

    Plain form required

    The embedded clause must use plain form, not polite 〜ます form. 来きますか分わからない is ungrammatical. The politeness of the main sentence is carried by the main verb: 来くるか分わかりません (polite).

    Particle Dropping in Embedded Clauses¶

    In embedded questions, が and は are frequently dropped — especially in casual speech.

    Full Casual / Fast Speech
    彼かれが来くるか 彼かれ来くるか
    雨あめが降ふるかどうか 雨あめ降ふるかどうか
    彼女かのじょが何なんを言いったか 彼女かのじょ何なん言いったか

    The drop is optional

    Omitting particles is casual but not ungrammatical in standard Japanese. In formal writing, particles are usually retained.

    Japanese Mindset¶

    Japanese is a language that nominalizes freely. Where English uses "whether" or "if" as conjunctions, Japanese turns the entire question into a noun-like block and drops it into a sentence slot. This reflects a broader pattern: Japanese prefers clause nominalization over conjunction chaining.

    The かどうか pattern also reveals a cultural preference for explicit binary framing. In planning, decision-making, and formal contexts, Japanese speakers often want to mark both alternatives clearly — not just "if yes" but "whether yes or no." This prevents the ambiguity of English "if," which can mean both "whether" and "in the event that."

    • Questions as objects. In Japanese, a question is not just an utterance act — it can be a grammatical object, a thing to be known, decided, or checked. 来くるか is "the matter of whether [someone] comes." This reification is natural in Japanese but takes adjustment for English speakers.
    • Binary explicitness as clarity. かどうか marks both poles of the uncertainty. In contexts where miscommunication would be costly (plans, medical questions, legal matters), this explicitness is preferred.
    • Particle drop as intimacy. Dropping が in embedded questions signals casualness and trust. The listener can parse the clause without grammatical scaffolding. This is a subtle intimacy marker.

    Nuance Comparison¶

    Grammar Point Core Meaning Explicitness / Nuance Trigger Context JLPT
    〜か (embedded) "Whether…" / "if…" [embedded] Neutral, compact; leaves alternatives implied General embedding; wh-questions; casual yes/no N4
    〜かどうか "Whether… or not" Explicit binary; both alternatives marked Yes/no questions where clarity matters; formal contexts N4
    〜か〜ないか "Whether… or not" [literary] Both alternatives stated in full Written Japanese; emphatic; literary N3
    〜かのように "As if…" Hypothetical embedding; pretend knowledge Describing behavior that assumes something N2
    〜かと思おもった "I wondered if…" Past self-questioning; relief or surprise Reacting to something that resolved an uncertainty N3
    〜かもしれない "Maybe…" Possibility, not embedding Expressing uncertainty directly (not as object) N4

    The closest lookalike is 〜かもしれない. But かもしれない is a sentence-ending possibility marker (彼かれが来くるかもしれない — "He might come"), not an embedded clause. The grammatical function is completely different.

    Alternatives¶

    〜か (compact embedding)¶

    The minimal form. Same function, less explicit about the binary.
    Example 彼かれが来くるか分わからない。
    English "I don't know if he'll come."
    Intent Neutral, compact, sufficient for most contexts
    vs. かどうか Less explicit. In casual speech, often preferred. Can feel slightly less formal or less urgent.

    When to use this instead

    Use 〜か alone when the yes/no nature is obvious from context, when speaking casually, or when brevity matters. It is never wrong for yes/no embedding.

    〜か〜ないか (literary double form)¶

    States both alternatives explicitly with full verbs.
    Example 行いくか行いかないか、決きめていない。
    English "I haven't decided whether to go or not."
    Intent Literary, emphatic, written style
    vs. かどうか More formal and weighty than かどうか. Common in novels, essays, and dramatic speech. Sounds heavy in casual conversation.

    〜かもしれない (possibility marker)¶

    Not an embedding pattern — a sentence-ending expression of possibility.
    Example 彼かれが来くるかもしれない。
    English "He might come."
    Intent Direct expression of uncertainty
    vs. embedded か This is a main-clause pattern, not an embedded clause. It cannot function as the object of 知しる or 分わかる.

    Register and Formality¶

    The explicitness of かどうか makes it slightly more formal than か alone, but both are acceptable across most registers.

    ケン来くるか分わからない。

    "Don't know if Ken's coming."

    Shift: Particle drop (が omitted), か alone. Ultra-casual, text-message friendly.

    ケンが来くるかどうか、知しってる？

    "Do you know whether Ken is coming or not?"

    Shift: かどうか adds explicitness even in casual speech. The comma pause is common. The 知しってる is casual.

    ケンさんが来くるかどうか、分わかりますか。

    "Do you know whether Ken is coming or not?"

    Shift: Standard polite. The かどうか is appropriate here. The main verb 分わかります carries the politeness.

    ケン様さまがご出席しゅっせきされるかどうか、ご確認かくにんいただけますでしょうか。

    "Might we ask you to confirm whether Ken will be in attendance or not?"

    Shift: Full keigo. 来くる → ご出席しゅっせきされる (respectful); 確認かくにんする → ご確認かくにんいただけますでしょうか (humble request). The かどうか is retained even in ultra-formal contexts — it is not casual.

    Step Pattern What It Adds Example

    N5 — Foundation Question words (何なん, 誰だれ, etc.) Wh-question formation 何なんを食たべますか？

    N3 — Extended 〜か〜ないか Literary double-form 行いくか行いかないか。

    Examples¶

    何なんが起おきたか知しりたい。 I want to know what happened.
    Literal: What happened-question want-to-know.
    Context: Wh-question embedding. Only か is possible. 何なんが起おきたかどうか would be ungrammatical.
    誰だれが来くるか、まだ知しらせてない。 I haven't been told who's coming yet.
    Literal: Who come-question, yet inform-not-done.
    Context: Wh-question as object of passive communication. The comma marks the embedded clause boundary.
    これが正ただしいか確認かくにんしてください。 Please check whether this is correct.
    Literal: This correct-question confirm-please.
    Context: Yes/no embedding with か alone. The context makes the binary nature obvious, so どうか is unnecessary.
    日本にっぽん語ごが話はなせるかどうか、聞きいてください。 Please ask whether they can speak Japanese or not.
    Literal: Japanese speak-can-question-or-not, ask-please.
    Context: Explicit yes/no embedding in an instruction. The かどうか makes the binary inquiry clear.
    行いくか行いかないか、決きめていない。 I haven't decided whether to go or not.
    Literal: Go-question go-not-question, decide-not-doing.
    Context: Literary double form. Both alternatives stated explicitly. More emphatic than 行いくかどうか.
    彼かれが怒おこっているかのように、黙だまっていた。 He remained silent as if he were angry.
    Literal: He angry-is-question-as, silent-was.
    Context: N2 pattern — hypothetical embedding. The かのように creates a simile of unconfirmed state.
    明日あすまでに終おわるかどうか、怪あやしい。 It's doubtful whether it'll be finished by tomorrow.
    Literal: Tomorrow-by finish-question-or-not, doubtful.
    Context: かどうか with adjectival predicate (怪あやしい). The embedded clause functions as the subject.

    Common Mistakes¶

    1. Using かどうか with wh-questions¶

    Ungrammatical

    何なんを食たべるかどうか知しらない。

    何なん is a wh-word, not a yes/no question. かどうか cannot follow wh-questions. Use 何なんを食たべるか知しらない.

    Correct

    何なんを食たべるか知しらない。

    "I don't know what [they] will eat." — Wh-embedding with か only.

    2. Using polite form inside the embedded clause¶

    Ungrammatical

    彼かれが来きますか分わからない。

    Embedded clauses must use plain form. 来きますか is a standalone polite question, not an embeddable clause.

    Correct

    彼かれが来くるか分わからない。

    "I don't know if he'll come." — Plain form inside the embedding; politeness is carried by the main verb.

    3. Confusing embedded か with sentence-ending かもしれない¶

    Structural error

    彼かれが来くるか知しらないかもしれない。

    This tries to embed 知しらないかもしれない ("might not know"), which is possible but means something different. If the intent is "I don't know if he might come," the structure is wrong.

    Correct (for intended meaning)

    彼かれが来くるかどうか分わからない。

    "I don't know whether he'll come or not." — Clear embedding.

    Collocation Notes¶

    Collocation Meaning
    〜か分わからない "Don't know whether…" — the most common embedding pattern
    〜かどうか分わからない "Don't know whether… or not" — explicit uncertainty
    〜か知しりたい "Want to know whether/what…" — curiosity embedding
    〜か聞きく "Ask whether…" — inquiry embedding
    〜か確認かくにんする "Check whether…" — verification embedding
    〜か決きめる "Decide whether…" — decision embedding
    〜か考かんがえる "Think about whether…" — deliberation embedding
    〜かどうか気きになる "Care about whether… or not" — emotional investment
    〜かどうか関係かんけいない "Doesn't matter whether… or not" — dismissal
    〜かのように "As if…" — hypothetical resemblance (N2)

    These collocations are worth learning as complete units. 〜か分わからない and 〜かどうか分わからない are daily-use patterns.

    Hidden Points¶

    • 〜か can embed statements that aren't literally questions. 彼かれが来くるかどうか is not a direct question — it's a nominalized clause. But it retains the semantic shape of a question: uncertainty, alternatives, information gap.
    • The comma after かどうか is prosodic. It represents a breath between the uncertainty and the main predicate. In fast speech it may disappear; in careful speech it marks the clause boundary clearly.
    • 〜かどうか can be used sarcastically. 行いくかどうか、知しったことじゃない ("Whether I go or not is none of your business") uses the explicit binary to create distance and finality.
    • Double embedding with lists: 行いくか留とまるか、決きめていない ("I haven't decided whether to go or stay") embeds two alternatives with two か markers. This is standard for multiple-choice embedding.
    • 〜か can embed exclamations rhetorically. 何なんて言いったか忘わすれた ("I forgot what [you] said") embeds a question the speaker once knew. The か marks the information as previously questioned, now lost.
    • The plain-form requirement is absolute. Even in ultra-formal keigo, the embedded clause remains plain: ご出席しゅっせきされるかどうか (not ご出席しゅっせきされますかどうか). The politeness is always on the main predicate, never inside the embedding.

    Learner Stage¶

    Stage What It Looks Like
    Now — N4 Produce 〜か and 〜かどうか correctly; distinguish wh-embedding from yes/no embedding; use plain form inside embedded clauses; avoid かどうか with question words
    Developing — N4→N3 Drop particles naturally in casual embedded clauses; choose between か and かどうか based on explicitness needs; use double-form 〜か〜ないか in writing
    Automatic — N3→N2 Embedded questions arrive as single units — you no longer parse them as "question + か." The choice between か and かどうか becomes intuitive.
    Mastery You deploy the full embedding spectrum: か (compact), かどうか (explicit), か〜ないか (literary), かのように (hypothetical). You know when particle dropping sounds natural and when it sounds sloppy. You never put ます inside an embedded clause.
