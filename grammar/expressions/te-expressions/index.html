    N3-Extended N4-Core N5-Foundation auxiliary-verbs te-form

    〜て＋Auxiliary — The Complete Spectrum¶

    てくれてありがとう・てよかった・てほしい・etc.¶

    JLPT Level¶

    JLPT Level — N5 Foundation through N3 Extended

    The te-form itself is N5. The auxiliary verbs attached to it create a staircase of interpersonal, emotional, and directional meaning that dominates N4 and N3. Mastering the te-form auxiliaries means mastering how Japanese speakers talk about ongoing action, social obligation, emotional relief, and change over time.

    Tier Pattern Description Example
    N5 — Foundation ている / てください / てもいい / てはいけない Progressive, request, permission, prohibition 食たべている。座すわってください。
    N5 — Foundation てくる・ていく / てみる / ておく / てしまう Directional, attemptive, preparation, completion 買かってくる。やってみる。
    N4 — Core ← You are here てあげる・てもらう・てくれる Giving and receiving of actions 手伝てつだってくれた。
    N4 — Core てほしい / てもらえませんか Desire directed outward; humble request 来きてほしい。教おしえてもらえませんか。
    N4 — Core てくれてありがとう / てすみません / てよかった Gratitude, apology, emotional evaluation 待まってくれてありがとう。
    N4 — Core てある Resultant state (deliberate) 準備じゅんびがしてある。
    N3 — Extended てくる・ていく (grammaticalized) Change over time / continuation into future 慣なれてきた。頑張がんばっていく。
    N3 — Extended てほしくない / てもらってもいいですか Negative desire; soft permission request 来きてほしくない。
    N3 — Extended てみせる / てばかりいる / てくれ Demonstrative; habitual negative; casual imperative やってみせる。遊あそんでばかりいる。

    Quick Summary¶

    At a glance

    • The te-form is Japanese grammatical glue. By attaching auxiliary verbs to it, you create layered meanings: progressive time (ている), social direction (てあげる/てくれる), emotional weight (てよかった/てしまう), preparation (ておく), and change over time (てくる/ていく).
    • N5 patterns describe basic action states: what is happening, what you may do, what you must not do, trying, preparing, finishing.
    • N4 patterns introduce the interpersonal layer: who benefits, who receives, who gives, what you want from others, and how you feel about what happened.
    • N3 patterns add temporal depth and emotional shading: things that start to happen, things that continue, things you show others, habits you criticize, and requests stripped to casual directness.
    • The te-form auxiliary system is not a list to memorize — it is a social and temporal coordinate system. Every choice places the action in a specific relationship to time, people, and feeling.

    N5 — Foundation: Action States¶

    1. ている — Progressive & Resultant State¶

    Ongoing action or a state that remains after a change.

    食たべている。 I am eating [progressive].
    ドアが開あいている。 The door is open [resultant state].
    知しっている。 I know [cognitive state].

    Two faces of ている

    With action verbs, ている means "in the middle of doing." With change-of-state verbs, it means "has become and remains."

    2. てください — Polite Request¶

    Standard polite way to ask someone to do something.

    座すわってください。 Please sit.
    見みてください。 Please look.
    静しずかにしてください。 Please be quiet.

    3. てもいい — Permission¶

    Grants permission or asks for it. Often paired with か for questions.

    入はいってもいいですか。 May I come in?
    写真しゃしんを撮とってもいい。 You may take photos.
    ここに座すわってもいい？ Is it okay if I sit here?

    4. てはいけない — Prohibition¶

    States what must not be done. Formal variant: てはいけません.

    触さわってはいけない。 Do not touch.
    入はいってはいけません。 You must not enter.
    嘘うそをついてはいけない。 You must not lie.

    5. てくる・ていく — Directional¶

    Physical movement toward or away from the speaker.

    買かい物ものに行いってくる。 I'm going shopping [and will return].
    持もっていく。 I'll take this [away with me].
    連つれてくる。 I'll bring [someone] here.

    N5: physical direction only

    At N5, てくる and ていく describe literal movement. The grammaticalized "change over time" meanings are N3.

    6. てみる — Attemptive¶

    Trying something to see what happens.

    食たべてみる。 I'll try eating it.
    考かんがえてみる。 I'll think about it.
    やってみよう。 Let's give it a try.

    7. ておく — Preparation¶

    Doing something in advance for future convenience.

    予約よやくしておく。 I'll make a reservation [in advance].
    準備じゅんびしておく。 I'll prepare [ahead of time].
    言いっておく。 I'll mention it [so it's handled].

    8. てしまう — Completion¶

    Finishing an action, often with regret or accidental nuance.

    食たべてしまった。 I ate it all [and now it's gone / I regret it].
    忘わすれてしまった。 I completely forgot.
    壊こわしてしまった。 I broke it [accidentally].

    N4 — Core: Interpersonal & Emotional¶

    9. てあげる — Outward Giving¶

    Doing something for someone else. The speaker is the giver.

    手伝てつだってあげる。 I'll help you [generous, slightly superior].
    教おしえてあげた。 I taught [them].
    買かってあげよう。 I'll buy it [for them].

    てあげる can sound patronizing

    It implies the speaker is doing a favor from a position of ability or generosity. Use carefully with superiors.

    10. てもらう — Inward Receiving¶

    Having someone do something for the speaker. The speaker benefits.

    先生せんせいに教おしえてもらった。 I was taught by my teacher [received the favor].
    友達ともだちに送おくってもらう。 I'll have my friend send it.
    母ははに作つくってもらった。 I had my mother make it.

    11. てくれる — Inward Giving¶

    Someone does something for the speaker (or speaker's group). Unsolicited benefit.

    友達ともだちが手伝てつだってくれた。 A friend helped me [I'm grateful].
    彼かれが教おしえてくれた。 He taught me [I benefited].
    誰だれかが拾ひろってくれた。 Someone picked it up for me.

    The gratitude foundation

    てくれる is the emotional engine of Japanese social grammar. It encodes that the action came toward the speaker as a gift.

    12. てほしい — Directed Desire¶

    Wanting someone to do something. Desire aimed at another person's action.

    早はやく来きてほしい。 I want you to come quickly.
    静しずかにしてほしい。 I want you to be quiet.
    一緒いっしょに行いってほしい。 I want you to go with me.

    てほしい is not an order

    It expresses the speaker's wish. It is softer than a command but more emotionally exposed than てください.

    13. てもらえませんか — Humble Request¶

    Asking to receive someone's action. More polite than てください.

    写真しゃしんを撮とってもらえませんか。 Could you take a photo for me?
    送おくってもらえませんか。 Could you send it to me?
    教おしえてもらえませんか。 Could you teach me?

    14. てくれてありがとう — Gratitude¶

    Thanking someone for doing something. The くれて embeds the giving frame; ありがとう adds gratitude.

    手伝てつだってくれてありがとう。 Thanks for helping me.
    待まってくれてありがとう。 Thanks for waiting.
    送おくってくれてありがとう。 Thanks for sending it.

    The te-form chain

    手伝てつだってくれてありがとう = [help] + [give me] + [thanks]. The two te-forms chain naturally.

    15. てすみません — Apology¶

    Apologizing for causing trouble through an action.

    待またせてすみません。 Sorry for making you wait.
    邪魔じゃましてすみません。 Sorry for interrupting.
    壊こわしてすみません。 Sorry for breaking it.

    16. てよかった — Emotional Relief¶

    Glad that something happened. Retrospective positive evaluation.

    会あえてよかった。 I'm glad we could meet.
    行いってよかった。 I'm glad I went.
    話はなせてよかった。 I'm glad we could talk.

    てよかった vs. てよくなかった

    The negative form (会あえてよくなかった) is grammatically possible but rarely used. When people regret something, they usually say 行いかなければよかった ("I shouldn't have gone").

    17. てある — Deliberate Resultant State¶

    A state that was intentionally created and remains.

    窓まどが開あけてある。 The window has been left open [on purpose].
    準備じゅんびがしてある。 Preparations have been made.
    予約よやくがしてある。 A reservation has been placed.

    てある vs. ている

    窓まどが開あいている = the window is open [state]. 窓まどが開あけてある = the window has been opened [by someone, and left that way intentionally].

    N3 — Extended: Temporal Depth & Emotional Shading¶

    18. てくる (Grammaticalized) — Change Over Time¶

    An action or state begins and moves toward the present. "Start to ~ / come to be ~ing."

    雨あめが降ふってきた。 It started raining [rain came].
    寒さむくなってきた。 It's getting colder.
    慣なれてきた。 I'm getting used to it.

    From physical to temporal

    N5: 買かってくる = go buy and come back. N3: 慣なれてきた = become accustomed over time. The directional verb has grammaticalized into a marker of emergent change.

    19. ていく (Grammaticalized) — Continuation Into Future¶

    An action continues forward from now. "Keep ~ing / proceed with ~ing."

    自分じぶんのペースでやっていく。 I'll keep going at my own pace.
    覚おぼえていけばいいから。 You just need to keep learning.
    これからも頑張がんばっていく。 I'll keep doing my best from here on.

    N3 continuation

    Where N5 持もっていく means "take away," N3 やっていく means "continue doing into the future." The spatial metaphor has become temporal.

    20. てほしくない — Negative Desire¶

    Not wanting someone to do something.

    来きてほしくない。 I don't want you to come.
    触さわってほしくない。 I don't want you to touch it.
    心配しんぱいしてほしくない。 I don't want you to worry.

    21. てもらってもいいですか — Soft Permission Request¶

    Asking if it's okay to receive someone's action. Softer than direct request.

    使つかってもらってもいいですか。 Would it be alright if I had you use this?
    見みてもらってもいい？ Is it okay if I get you to look at this?
    持もってもらってもいいですか。 Could I ask you to hold this?

    22. てみせる — Demonstrative¶

    Doing something to show someone, or to prove a point.

    やってみせる。 I'll do it [and show you].
    作つくってみせる。 I'll make it [to demonstrate].
    勝かってみせる。 I'll win [just watch me].

    23. てばかりいる — Habitual Criticism¶

    Doing nothing but ~, with negative judgment.

    遊あそんでばかりいる。 All [they] do is play.
    文句もんくを言いってばかりいる。 All [they] do is complain.
    食たべてばかりいる。 All [they] do is eat.

    The criticism is baked in

    てばかりいる is almost always used to criticize someone for an imbalance in their behavior.

    24. てくれ — Casual Imperative¶

    Direct request, often male-coded or urgent. The くれ is the imperative of くれる.

    静しずかにしてくれ。 Be quiet [direct].
    見みてくれ。 Look [at this].
    待まってくれ。 Wait [for me].

    Not polite

    てくれ is casual and potentially rough. Do not use with superiors or strangers.

    25. てもらえる — Potential Receiving¶

    Asking if one can receive a favor. The potential form of てもらう.

    教おしえてもらえる？ Can you teach me? / Can I get you to teach me?
    手伝てつだってもらえる？ Can you help me?
    送おくってもらえる？ Can you send it?

    Casual potential

    てもらえる drops the formal ます and creates a casual but still deferential request.

    Japanese Mindset¶

    The te-form auxiliary system reveals how Japanese conceptualizes action as socially embedded and temporally unfolding. English tends to isolate verbs in time and person; Japanese chains them into continuums.

    • Action as gift. てくれる, てもらう, てあげる frame every action as a transfer of benefit. Even something as simple as "teaching" becomes a social transaction: who gave, who received, who is grateful.
    • Action as preparation. ておく treats the present as a servant of the future. You do things now so that future-you or future-someone will be okay.
    • Action as emergence. てくる and ていく treat time as a landscape you move through. Changes approach you (てくる) or you carry actions forward (ていく).
    • Action as emotion. てよかった and てしまう show that Japanese grammar does not separate "what happened" from "how I feel about it." The feeling is in the verb ending.

    Nuance Comparison¶

    Pattern Core Meaning Social Direction Emotional Weight JLPT

    てもらえませんか Could you do for me? Humble receiving High (polite) N4

    てくれる Does for me Gift toward speaker High (gratitude) N4

    Common Mistakes¶

    1. Confusing てあげる with てくれる direction¶

    Social direction error

    先生せんせいが教おしえてあげた。 (said by student about teacher)

    ~てあげる implies the speaker (or subject) is giving from above. A teacher teaching a student is not あげる from the student's perspective — it is くれる (the teacher gave the student the favor).~

    Correct

    先生せんせいが教おしえてくれた。

    2. Using てほしい as an order¶

    Register mismatch

    黙だまってほしい！ (to a superior)

    ~While grammatically correct, てほしい exposes the speaker's desire directly. With a superior, 静しずかにしていただけますか is safer.~

    Appropriate

    少すこし静しずかにしていただけますか。

    3. Confusing てある with ている¶

    State error

    ドアが開あいてある。 (when no one opened it intentionally)

    ~てある requires deliberate human action. If the door opened by wind, use 開あいている or 開あいた.~

    Correct

    ドアが開あいている。 (state) ドアが開あけてある。 (someone opened it and left it)

    4. Using N5 directional てくる when N3 emergent is needed¶

    Physical vs. temporal

    日本語にほんごが話はなせてくる。 (sounds like "Japanese comes speaking")

    ~For emergent ability, 話はなせるようになってきた is clearer. 話はなせてくる is not standard.~

    Correct

    日本語にほんごが話はなせるようになってきた。

    Hidden Points¶

    • ておく often implies "without saying." 言いっておく means "I'll say it [and leave it at that]." The おく adds a sense of finality — the speaker handles something so the listener doesn't have to think about it again.
    • てくれてありがとう is structurally recursive. 手伝てつだってくれて = [help] + [give me] + [te-form connecting to thanks]. The chain is: action → giving frame → gratitude frame. This is standard Japanese emotional syntax.
    • てよかった carries survivor's relief. 生いきていてよかった is not just "I'm glad I'm alive" — it is the grammar of having survived something specific. The て marks the continuity of life as a thread that could have been cut.
    • てばかりいる is gendered in perception. When used for women, it can sound more judgmental than when used for men. The pattern itself is neutral, but social reception varies.
    • てみせる is performative. It transforms an action into a demonstration. The speaker is not just doing — they are staging the doing for an audience.
    • てほしい cannot be used for third-person desire. You cannot say 彼かれが来きてほしい to mean "He wants [someone] to come." For third-person desire, use たがる or reported speech: 彼かれは来きてほしがっている / 来きてほしいと言いっている.

    Examples¶

    買かい物ものに行いってくる。 I'm going shopping [and coming back].
    N5 directional. Physical movement with return implied.
    先生せんせいに教おしえてもらった。 I was taught by my teacher [received favor].
    N4 receiving. The teacher is the giver; the speaker is the beneficiary.
    手伝てつだってくれてありがとう。 Thanks for helping me.
    N4 gratitude. The くれて embeds the giving direction; ありがとう completes the emotional transaction.
    会あえてよかった。 I'm glad we could meet.
    N4 emotional evaluation. Retrospective relief that the meeting occurred.
    雨あめが降ふってきた。 It started raining.
    N3 emergent change. The rain is approaching the speaker's temporal sphere.
    自分じぶんのペースでやっていく。 I'll keep going at my own pace.
    N3 continuation. The action projects forward into future time.
    遊あそんでばかりいる。 All they do is play.
    N3 habitual criticism. The ばかり emphasizes imbalance; いる grounds it in ongoing behavior.
    やってみせる。 I'll do it [and show you].
    N3 demonstrative. The action is staged for an observer.
    準備じゅんびがしてある。 Preparations have been made.

    Per-Entry Hidden Points¶

    Insert these immediately after their respective entries in the main body.

    After てあげる (N4 — Outward Giving)¶

    Hidden Point — The patronizing trap

    てあげる frames the speaker as doing a favor from a position of generosity or ability. With peers it sounds kind; with superiors it can sound condescending ("Let me do this for you, since you apparently can't"). When offering help to a boss or elder, お手伝てつだいしましょうか or お手伝てつだいできますか is safer than 手伝てつだってあげます.

    After てくれる (N4 — Inward Giving)¶

    Hidden Point — The gratitude engine

    てくれる is not merely "someone did something" — it is "someone did something for me." This makes it the emotional foundation of Japanese social grammar. Even a neutral action like "reminding" becomes a gift when framed with くれる: 思おもい出ださせてくれた ("You reminded me [and I benefited]"). Native speakers feel gratitude structurally, not just lexically.

    After てほしい (N4 — Directed Desire)¶

    Hidden Point — Emotional exposure

    てほしい reveals the speaker's inner want directly. It is softer than a command but more vulnerable than てください because it admits dependency. Saying 来きてほしい to a romantic partner is intimate; saying it to a colleague is slightly forward unless softened with のですが or んですけど.

    After てもらえませんか (N4 — Humble Request)¶

    Hidden Point — The receiving frame as social grace

    This pattern does not ask "Can you do X?" It asks "Can I receive X from you?" By framing yourself as the recipient, you elevate the listener to the giver. This is why 教おしえてもらえませんか sounds humbler than 教おしえてください despite both being requests — the social geometry is reversed.

    After てよかった (N4 — Emotional Relief)¶

    Hidden Point — Survivor's grammar

    会あえてよかった carries a subtle implication that the meeting might not have happened — schedules, distance, or circumstances could have prevented it. The よかった is not generic happiness; it is retrospective relief that a possibility became real. This makes it powerful after reunions, recoveries, and near-misses.

    After てある (N4 — Deliberate Resultant State)¶

    Hidden Point — The human intention requirement

    てある cannot describe natural states. You cannot say 花はなが咲さいてある because flowers bloom without human agency. You can say 窓まどが開あけてある because someone opened it deliberately. This intentionality filter is absolute — it is the single most reliable way to distinguish てある from ている.

    After てくる (N3 — Emergent Change)¶

    Hidden Point — The approach metaphor

    The N3 grammaticalized てくる uses the physical metaphor of movement toward the speaker. Change "comes" to you over time: 寒さむくなってきた = coldness approached and arrived at my experience. This is why てくる feels more immediate and personal than the neutral なる.

    After ていく (N3 — Continuation Into Future)¶

    Hidden Point — The sustainability frame

    やっていく reframes a task not as something to complete but as something to continue carrying. This is deeply Japanese — the emphasis is on forward endurance rather than achievement. 頑張がんばっていく is not "I will try hard and succeed"; it is "I will keep striving as I move forward."

    After てばかりいる (N3 — Habitual Criticism)¶

    Hidden Point — The imbalance judgment

    The criticism in てばかりいる is not about frequency alone — it is about imbalance. 遊あそんでばかりいる implies the person should be doing something else (studying, working) but isn't. The grammar encodes a social expectation of balanced behavior and finds it violated.

    After てくれ (N3 — Casual Imperative)¶

    Hidden Point — The favor demand

    てくれ is grammatically "Do [it] and give [me the action]." It treats the listener as someone who owes the speaker a favor, or at least as someone close enough to receive blunt requests. It is not inherently rude between close male friends, but it is never appropriate for service, workplace, or strangers.

    Enhanced Example Notes¶

    Replace the existing Examples section with these expanded entries:

    買かい物ものに行いってくる。 I'm going shopping [and coming back].
    N5 directional. Physical movement with return implied. The くる promises the speaker's return, which is why this phrase is standard for leaving the house temporarily. Saying 買かい物ものに行いく without てくる sounds more open-ended — you might not come straight back.
    先生せんせいに教おしえてもらった。 I was taught by my teacher [received favor].
    N4 receiving. The teacher is the giver; the speaker is the beneficiary. The に marks the source of the received action. This is the standard way to describe learning from someone without sounding arrogant — you frame yourself as having been given knowledge.
    手伝てつだってくれてありがとう。 Thanks for helping me.
    N4 gratitude. The くれて embeds the giving direction (toward me); ありがとう completes the emotional transaction. This is more natural than 手伝てつだってありがとう, which omits the benefactive layer and sounds slightly flat.
    会あえてよかった。 I'm glad we could meet.
    N4 emotional evaluation. Retrospective relief that the meeting occurred. Often used at farewells after reunions, implying the meeting was uncertain or precious. Without てよかった, 会あえた is merely factual; with it, the meeting becomes emotionally weighted.
    雨あめが降ふってきた。 It started raining.
    N3 emergent change. The rain is approaching the speaker's temporal sphere. This is more vivid than 雨あめが降ふり始はじめた because it implies the rain reached you personally — your plans, your location, your experience.
    自分じぶんのペースでやっていく。 I'll keep going at my own pace.
    N3 continuation. The action projects forward into future time. やっていく reframes the task as sustainable journey rather than deadline-driven achievement. This is a distinctly Japanese way of expressing long-term commitment without pressure.
    遊あそんでばかりいる。 All they do is play.
    N3 habitual criticism. The ばかり emphasizes imbalance; いる grounds it in ongoing behavior. The criticism is not "they play often" but "they play to the exclusion of what they should be doing." Context determines whether the tone is parental, jealous, or genuinely concerned.
    やってみせる。 I'll do it [and show you].
    N3 demonstrative. The action is staged for an observer. Often used defensively ("I'll prove I can") or pedagogically ("Watch how it's done"). The みせる adds performative pressure — the outcome will be witnessed and evaluated.
    準備じゅんびがしてある。 Preparations have been made.
    N4 resultant state. Someone prepared deliberately, and the state persists. This is the standard "ready to go" announcement. Contrast with 準備じゅんびしている, which emphasizes ongoing preparation rather than completed readiness.
    静しずかにしてくれ。 Be quiet [direct].
    N3 casual imperative. The くれ is imperative of くれる, framing the request as a demand for favor. Between close friends this is blunt but acceptable; in any other context it is rude. The softening alternative is 静しずかにしてくれない？ or 静しずかにしてもらえない？
