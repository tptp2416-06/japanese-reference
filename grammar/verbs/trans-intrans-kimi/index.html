    N4-Core particles transitivity verb-pairs

    Transitive vs. Intransitive Verbs¶

    他動たどう詞し (tadoushi) & 自動じどう詞し (jidoushi)¶

    JLPT Level¶

    JLPT Level — Core N4 / Upper N4

    The individual paired verbs appear from N5 onward, but the systematic understanding of transitivity as a grammatical architecture — particle choice, causative overlap, passive ambiguity, and the conceptual shift from English — is solidly N4. Many learners reach N2 without truly internalizing the Japanese transitivity mindset.

    Tier Pattern Description Example
    N5 — Foundation Basic paired verbs Individual memorization (開あける/開あく, etc.) ドアを開あける / ドアが開あく
    N4 — Foundation を vs が with transitivity Core particle geometry 本ほんを読よむ / 雨あめが降ふる
    N4 — Core ← You are here Systematic transitivity Understanding the three morphological patterns and the conceptual split from English 落おとす・落おちる・落おとされる
    N3 — Extended Exceptional を + intransitive Motion verbs, point of departure 家いえを出でる、道みちを歩あるく
    N3 — Extended Causative-passive overlap 落おとさせられる — "being made to drop" 子供こどもに落おとさせられた
    N2 — Awareness Transitivity in keigo 尊敬そんけい語ご・謙譲けんじょう語ご often alter transitivity お知しりになります (intransitive respectful)

    Quick Summary¶

    At a glance

    • Transitive verbs (他動たどう詞し) take a direct object marked by を. They describe an action performed by an agent on something: ドアを開あける, 本ほんを読よむ, 電気でんきをつける.
    • Intransitive verbs (自動じどう詞し) do not take a direct object. They describe events that happen, states that obtain, or changes that occur: ドアが開あく, 雨うが降ふる, 電気でんきがつく.
    • Japanese treats this distinction as lexical and absolute. Where English often uses the same verb for both ("I opened the door / The door opened"), Japanese almost always has two distinct verbs: 開あける (transitive) and 開あく (intransitive).
    • The single most important mindset shift: Stop thinking "Who did it?" and start thinking "What happened?" Japanese sentences often describe events without agents. English sentences usually demand one.
    • There are three major morphological patterns for paired verbs, plus a handful of irregulars. Learning the patterns is more efficient than memorizing pairs in isolation.

    Core Meaning¶

    Core Rule

    Use を + transitive verb when an agent acts on a target. Use が + intransitive verb when an event occurs or a state changes. Do not use を with standard intransitive verbs, and do not use が with transitive verbs unless the verb is passive or potential.

    太郎たろうがドアを開あけた。 Taro opened the door [transitive, agent + object].
    Subject: 太郎たろう (agent). Object: ドア (target). Verb: 開あける (transitive). The door did nothing — Taro acted on it.
    ドアが開あいた。 The door opened [intransitive, event].
    Subject: ドア (thing undergoing change). No object. Verb: 開あく (intransitive). No agent is mentioned. The door simply changed state.
    電気でんきをつけた。 I turned on the light [transitive].
    Agent implied (I). Object: 電気でんき. Verb: つける (transitive).
    電気でんきがついた。 The light came on [intransitive].
    Subject: 電気でんき. No agent. Verb: つく (intransitive). The light entered a state of being on.

    The English Speaker's Trap: A Deep Dive¶

    English speakers face a specific and persistent set of illusions when learning Japanese transitivity. Addressing these directly is essential for comprehension.

    Trap 1: The "Same Verb" Illusion¶

    English frequently uses identical forms for transitive and intransitive meanings:

    English Transitive Intransitive
    open I opened the door. The door opened.
    break I broke the glass. The glass broke.
    start I started the engine. The engine started.
    change I changed my mind. My mind changed.
    melt I melted the butter. The butter melted.

    This creates a powerful expectation that one verb can do both jobs. Japanese almost never allows this. The door opening and you opening the door are lexically distinct events.

    English-calibrated error

    ドアを開あいた。

    ~This treats 開あく as transitive. It is not. 開あく is strictly intransitive. The correct transitive is 開あける: ドアを開あけた。~

    Trap 2: The Passive Substitution Urge¶

    When English speakers want to say "The door was opened" without naming who did it, they reach for passive grammar:

    English-calibrated error

    ドアが開あけられた。

    ~Grammatically possible (passive of 開あける), but bizarrely unnatural for a simple state change. In Japanese, if the door simply opened — wind, spring mechanism, someone unknown — you say ドアが開あいた. The passive 開あけられた implies someone specifically opened it and you want to obscure or emphasize their agency.~

    Natural Japanese

    ドアが開あいた。

    ~The intransitive verb is the default for "the door opened." No passive needed. No agent implied.~

    Mindset correction: Japanese intransitive verbs cover the semantic territory where English uses both intransitive ("the door opened") and passive ("the door was opened"). The passive in Japanese is reserved for when you specifically want to mention that an agent acted, but demote them grammatically.

    Trap 3: The Adjective Confusion¶

    Many English states are expressed as adjectives or participles, while Japanese uses intransitive verbs:

    English (adjective/participle) Japanese (intransitive verb)
    The window is broken. 窓まどが壊こわれている。
    The door is open. ドアが開あいている。
    He is dead. 彼かれが死しんでいる。
    The meat is cooked. 肉にくが焼やけている。

    English-calibrated error

    窓まどは壊こわれた。

    ~This is grammatical but means "The window broke" (event), not "The window is broken" (state). For the state, Japanese uses the progressive: 窓まどが壊こわれている。~

    Mindset correction: Japanese intransitive verbs often describe resultant states through the 〜ている form, where English uses adjectives. The verb itself encodes the change; the progressive encodes the resulting condition.

    Trap 4: The "Happening" vs "Doing" Orientation¶

    English is agent-oriented. A standard English sentence has a subject who does something. Japanese is event-oriented. A standard Japanese sentence has a topic or subject about which something happens.

    English mindset Japanese mindset
    "Who broke it?" "What state is it in?"
    "I melted the ice." "The ice melted." (default if no agent matters)
    "The window was broken by the wind." "The window broke from the wind." (風かぜで窓まどが壊こわれた)

    The critical reframe: When describing the world, ask not "Who acted?" but "What changed?" If the change itself is the point, Japanese selects the intransitive verb automatically. If the agent's role is the point, Japanese selects the transitive.

    The Japanese Mindset: Event vs. Agent¶

    Japanese transitivity is not merely a grammatical rule — it reflects a cognitive preference for describing events over assigning agency.

    • The intransitive as default. When a Japanese speaker sees a door open, they describe the door's state change: ドアが開あいた. The English speaker asks "Who opened it?" or says "It was opened." The Japanese speaker often doesn't care about the opener — the event is the information.
    • Transitivity as marked choice. Using a transitive verb (ドアを開あけた) is a claim about agency. It says "Someone did this, and I am telling you who or that they did." This is more specific and more loaded than the intransitive.
    • The を particle as agency marker. を does not just mark objects — it marks the target of directed energy. When you say ドアを開あける, you are not just describing a door and an opening; you are describing a vector of force from an agent to a target.
    • The が particle as change marker. が with intransitive verbs marks the locus of change — the thing that enters a new state. It is not "doing" anything; it is "becoming" something.

    This mindset explains why Japanese has so many more intransitive verbs than English, and why they are used so naturally in daily speech. The world, in Japanese grammar, is full of things that change, happen, and obtain — not just things that people do.

    How It Works: The Three Patterns¶

    Japanese transitive/intransitive pairs follow three major morphological patterns. Recognizing these patterns accelerates vocabulary acquisition dramatically.

    Pattern A: ~aru (intr) ↔ ~eru (tr)¶

    The most common pattern. Intransitive verbs end in ~aru (often ~waru, ~karu, ~garu, ~maru, ~baru, ~taru, ~raru). Transitive verbs end in ~eru (often ~u-ru, ~ku-ru, ~gu-ru, ~su-ru, ~tsu-ru, ~nu-ru, ~bu-ru, ~mu-ru, ~ru-ru).

    下さがる (sagaru) 下さげる (sageru) fall (price) / lower
    開あく (hiraku) 開あける (akeru) open (naturally) / open (by hand)
    閉しまる (shimaru) 閉しめる (shimeru) close (naturally) / close (by hand)
    止とまる (tomaru) 止とめる (tomeru) stop (moving) / stop (something)
    始はじまる (hajimaru) 始はじめる (hajimeru) begin (event) / begin (something)

    The aru/eru pattern is productive

    Once you recognize the sound correspondence, you can often guess the partner verb. If you know 上あがる, you can predict 上あげる. If you know 止とめる, you can predict 止とまる.

    Overlap between Pattern A and B

    Some verbs appear in both patterns (e.g., 壊こわれる/壊こわす). This is because the reru/su pattern is historically related to the aru/eru pattern through sound changes. Don't worry about the overlap — just recognize that ~su transitive verbs often pair with ~reru intransitive verbs.

    Adjective + する is a major transitive pattern

    When Japanese lacks a dedicated transitive verb for an adjective's change, it uses [adjective stem + ku] + する. This is productive and essential: 大おおきくする, 小ちいさくする, etc.

    The Particle Geometry: を vs が¶

    The particle choice is the immediate signal of transitivity. This is non-negotiable in standard Japanese.

    Particle Role Example
    を Direct object of transitive verb ドアを開あける
    が Subject of intransitive verb ドアが開あく
    が Subject of passive verb ドアが開あけられた
    が Subject of potential verb ドアが開あけられる
    に Indirect target / location ドアに鍵かぎをかける
    で Means / location of action ドアを鍵かぎで開あける

    The を/が swap is the first transitivity test

    If you can swap を and が and the sentence still makes sense with a different verb, you have a transitivity pair: - ドアを開あけた (transitive) ↔ ドアが開あいた (intransitive)

    The Exceptional を: Motion and Departure¶

    Some intransitive verbs of motion take を to mark the point of departure or the space traversed. This is not true transitivity — the verb remains intransitive, but を has a special function here.

    Verb を usage Meaning
    出でる (deru) 家いえを出でる leave home (point of departure)
    降おりる (oriru) 電車でんしゃを降おりる get off the train (space exited)
    卒業そつぎょうする (sotsugyou suru) 大学だいがくを卒業そつぎょうする graduate from university
    通とおる (tooru) 道みちを通とおる pass through the road
    渡わたる (wataru) 橋はしを渡わたる cross the bridge
    歩あるく (aruku) 道みちを歩あるく walk the road
    走はしる (hashiru) トラックを走はしる run the track
    飛とぶ (tobu) 空そらを飛とぶ fly through the sky
    泳およぐ (oyogu) 海うみを泳およぐ swim the sea
    散歩さんぽする (sanpo suru) 公園こうえんを散歩さんぽする stroll through the park
    旅行りょこうする (ryokou suru) 日本にっぽんを旅行りょこうする travel Japan
    避難ひなんする (hinan suru) 建物たてものを避難ひなんする evacuate the building
    退院たいいんする (taiin suru) 病院びょういんを退院たいいんする leave the hospital
    退学たいがくする (taigaku suru) 学校がっこうを退学たいがくする drop out of school
    辞職じしょくする (jishoku suru) 会社かいしゃを辞職じしょくする resign from the company
    退職たいしょくする (taishoku suru) 会社かいしゃを退職たいしょくする retire from the company
    離婚りこんする (rikon suru) 妻つまを離婚りこんする divorce one's wife (colloquial)
    結婚けっこんする (kekkon suru) 彼女かのじょと結婚けっこんする marry her (に/と, not を)

    The departure を is a special case

    These を-marked phrases are not direct objects in the transitive sense. The verbs remain intransitive (they don't take a を-marked thing and change its state). The を marks the boundary crossed or the space traversed. This is an N3 exception, but recognizing it early prevents confusion.

    Nuance Comparison: Transitivity vs. Passive vs. Causative¶

    The same event can be described through multiple grammatical lenses. Choosing between them is a core N3+ skill.

    Form Example Meaning Nuance
    Intransitive ドアが開あいた。 The door opened. Event-focused. No agent. The default description.
    Transitive 太郎たろうがドアを開あけた。 Taro opened the door. Agent-focused. Taro's action is the point.
    Passive ドアが太郎たろうに開あけられた。 The door was opened by Taro. Agent demoted but present. Emphasizes the door's experience.
    Causative 太郎たろうが花子はなこにドアを開あけさせた。 Taro made Hanako open the door. Taro controls Hanako's action.
    Causative-passive 花子はなこが太郎たろうにドアを開あけさせられた。 Hanako was made to open the door by Taro. Hanako's unwillingness or victimhood.
    Potential ドアが開あけられる。 The door can be opened. Capability. The door is openable.
    Potential (intrans) ドアが開あけられる。 (Same form, different reading) Ambiguous without context. Usually transitive potential.

    The passive is not the intransitive

    English speakers often conflate "The door was opened" (passive) with "The door opened" (intransitive). In Japanese: - ドアが開あいた = The door opened (natural intransitive) - ドアが開あけられた = The door was opened by someone (passive, agent implied or stated)

    The passive 開あけられた specifically implies an agent acted on the door. The intransitive 開あいた simply describes the door's state change.

    Common Mistakes¶

    1. Using the same verb for transitive and intransitive¶

    English transfer error

    ドアを開あいた。

    ~開あく is intransitive. It cannot take を. The transitive partner is 開あける.~

    Correct

    ドアを開あけた。 / ドアが開あいた。

    2. Using passive where intransitive is natural¶

    English passive substitution

    窓まどが割わられた。

    ~This means "The window was broken [by someone]." If you simply found a broken window and want to say "The window broke," use the intransitive: 窓まどが割われた。~

    Correct

    窓まどが割われた。 (intransitive, natural state change)

    3. Confusing を on motion verbs with transitivity¶

    Overgeneralization

    家いえを出でた。 → Treating 出でる as transitive.

    ~出でる is intransitive. 家いえを出でる uses を to mark the point of departure, not a direct object. The house is not being "exited" in the transitive sense — the verb describes the subject's movement away from it.~

    Understanding

    家いえを出でた = "I left home." (intransitive verb, special を usage)

    4. Using adjective + する incorrectly¶

    Pattern error

    部屋へやをきれいにした。 → This is actually correct.

    ~But learners sometimes say 部屋へやをきれいした, omitting the に. Adjectives require に before する when making something adjective-like.~

    Correct

    部屋へやをきれいにした。 / 部屋へやを静しずかにした。

    5. Thinking potential form changes transitivity¶

    Category error

    このドアが開あけられる。

    ~The potential form of a transitive verb (開あける → 開あけられる) is grammatically tricky. It often takes が on the object (ドアが開あけられる = "This door can be opened"), but the verb is still fundamentally transitive in meaning. Don't treat this as intransitive.~

    Understanding

    ドアが開あけられる = "The door can be opened" (potential, object marked with が due to potential grammar, but still transitive semantics)

    Hidden Points¶

    • Transitivity is lexical, not contextual. In English, "cook" can be transitive or intransitive depending on context: "I'm cooking" vs "I'm cooking rice." In Japanese, 料理りょうりする is transitive (requires an object or context), while 火ひが通とおる describes the cooking process intransitively. You cannot simply "use the same verb both ways" as in English.
    • The aru/eru pattern has sound symbolism. The ~aru ending often feels "lower" or "grounded" (intransitive, receiving), while ~eru feels "active" or "directed" (transitive, sending). This is not etymological fact but a useful mnemonic.
    • Some "transitive" verbs in English are intransitive in Japanese. "To graduate" is transitive in English ("I graduated college"), but 卒業そつぎょうする is intransitive and takes を only for the departure exception: 大学だいがくを卒業そつぎょうする.
    • The passive of transitive verbs often sounds unnatural for simple description. ドアが開あけられた implies a story: someone opened it, and there's a reason to mention that fact. For simple state description, ドアが開あいた is always preferred.
    • Transitivity affects keigo choices. 尊敬そんけい語ご often shifts transitivity. お知しりになります (intransitive respectful) vs ご存知ぞんじです (nominal respectful). The transitivity of the humble/respectful form may differ from the plain form.
    • Potential forms of transitive verbs take が on the object. This creates a surface similarity to intransitive sentences: 本ほんが読よめる ("I can read books" — potential) looks like 本ほんが落おちる ("The book falls" — intransitive). The grammar is different, but the particle pattern is identical.
    • Some intransitive verbs have no transitive partner. 死しぬ (die) has no simple transitive partner (殺ころす = kill is a different root). ある (exist) has no transitive partner. These gaps reveal conceptual boundaries.
    • The ~garu suffix on adjectives creates a transitive-like observation. 暑あつがる ("to show signs of being hot") allows third-person psychological description, something adjectives normally resist. This is a special transitivity workaround.

    Examples¶

    太郎たろうがドアを開あけた。 Taro opened the door [transitive, agent-focused].
    The point is Taro's action. The door is the target.
    ドアが開あいた。 The door opened [intransitive, event-focused].
    The point is the door's state change. No agent is relevant.
    風かぜで窓まどが割われた。 The window broke from the wind [intransitive, external cause].
    The で marks external cause, but the verb remains intransitive. English might use passive ("was broken by the wind"), but Japanese uses intransitive + cause marker.
    電気でんきをつけてもらった。 I had someone turn on the light [receiving frame, transitive].
    The transitive verb つける is used, but the receiving frame (てもらう) shifts the social focus to the beneficiary.
    電気でんきがついた。 The light came on [intransitive, natural].
    No agent. The light simply entered a state of being on.
    雨あめで試合しあいが中止ちゅうしになった。 The game was cancelled due to rain [intransitive + nominal].
    中止ちゅうしになる is an intransitive expression. English uses passive ("was cancelled"), but Japanese treats cancellation as an event that befalls the game.
    子供こどもに花瓶かびんを割わられた。 I had my vase broken by the child [passive, suffering].
    The indirect passive (迷惑めいわくの受身うけみ). The speaker is affected by someone else's transitive action. This is distinct from simple intransitive description.
    このボタンを押おすと、ドアが開あきます。 When you press this button, the door opens [causal intransitive].
    The transitive action (押おす) triggers an intransitive result (開あく). This is a standard mechanical description pattern.
    部屋へやをきれいにした。 I made the room clean [adjective + transitive suru].
    When no dedicated transitive verb exists, Japanese uses adjective + にする.
    家いえを出でる前まえに、電気でんきを消けした。 Before leaving home, I turned off the lights [departure を + transitive].
    家いえを出でる uses the special departure を. 電気でんきを消けした is standard transitive.

    Learner Stage¶

    Stage What It Looks Like
    Now — N4 Memorize core paired verbs (開あける/開あく, つける/つく, 壊こわす/壊こわれる, etc.); use を and が correctly with transitive and intransitive verbs; avoid passive substitution for simple state changes
    Developing — N4→N3 Recognize the three morphological patterns (aru/eru, reru/su, irregular); predict unknown pairs from known ones; handle motion-verb を exceptions naturally; distinguish passive from intransitive in production
    Automatic — N3→N2 Transitivity becomes an intuitive filter — you feel the wrongness of ドアを開あいた before analyzing it. You automatically select intransitive for event descriptions and transitive for agent descriptions.
    Mastery You control the full transitivity spectrum: intransitive (event), transitive (agency), passive (demoted agency), causative (compelled agency), and potential (capability). You know when English passive must become Japanese intransitive, when adjectives need する, and when motion verbs take exceptional を. You also understand how transitivity interacts with keigo and humility.
