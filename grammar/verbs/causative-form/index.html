    N4-Upper causative saseru verb-conjugation

    Causative Verb Form — させる / せる¶

    〜させる / 〜せる — Making or Letting Someone Do Something¶

    JLPT Level¶

    JLPT Level — Upper N4 / Lower N3

    The causative form itself is N4. The nuance layer — reading whether a causative sentence means coercion or permission, and choosing the right form for the social context — is solidly N3.

    Tier Pattern Description Example
    N4 — Foundation Godan causative 〜せる Replace the final う-row sound with あ-row + せる 書かく → 書かかせる "make/let someone write"
    N4 — Foundation Ichidan causative 〜させる Drop る and add させる 食たべる → 食たべさせる "make/let someone eat"
    N4 — Core ← You are here Causative in context Causative embedded in natural sentences — reading making vs. letting from context; causative + なきゃいけない for self-imposed obligation 今日中きょうじゅうに終おわらせなきゃいけない仕事しごとがあってさ。
    N3 — Extended Social nuance layer Distinguishing controlling vs. permissive causative; softening strategies; reading the relationship from the causative choice 子供こどもに好すきなものを食たべさせてあげた。"I let the child eat what they liked."
    N2 — Awareness Causative-passive 〜させられる "Being made to do something" — double stacking of causative and passive 残業ざんぎょうさせられた。"I was made to work overtime."

    Quick Summary¶

    At a glance

    • The causative form (させる / せる) expresses that someone causes another person to do something — either by making them (coercion or authority) or letting them (permission or facilitation).
    • Which meaning — making or letting — is determined entirely by context and the relationship between the people involved, not by the form itself.
    • In the input sentence, the causative is self-directed: 終わらせなきゃいけない means "I have to get [the work] finished" — the speaker is the one both causing and experiencing the obligation.
    • The causative also appears in common set expressions — させてください (please let me do X), させてもらう (I'll take the liberty of doing X) — that you'll encounter constantly even at N4 level.

    Core Meaning¶

    Core Rule

    Attach させる (ichidan / irregular verbs) or せる (godan verbs) to the verb stem to express that the subject causes someone else to perform the action — either by compelling them or by allowing them.

    親おやが子供こどもに野菜やさいを食たべさせた。 The parent made the child eat vegetables.
    Literal: Parent child-to vegetables eat-caused.

    ~Context: Clear coercion — the parent has authority, the child complies. Making, not letting.~

    今日中きょうじゅうに終おわらせなきゃいけない仕事しごとがあってさ。 I've got work I need to get finished by end of today.
    Literal: By-today's-end finish-must-cause work there-is, and...

    ~Context: The input sentence. Self-directed causative — the speaker is causing the work to reach completion. なきゃいけない adds obligation: "I have no choice but to get it done."~

    In Real Life¶

    In Real Life

    Situation: It's late afternoon on a weekday. ハル messages a friend to cancel plans they had for the evening.

    ごめん、今日ちょっと無理かも。今日中に終わらせなきゃいけない仕事があってさ。

    "Sorry, tonight might be tough for me. I've got work I have to get finished by end of day."

    Why this form fits here: 終わらせる is the causative of 終わる (to finish/end) — the speaker is framing themselves as the agent causing the work to reach completion. Combined with なきゃいけない, the sentence packages the obligation tightly: "there exists work that I must cause to be finished." It's not "I have to do work" (仕事しなきゃ) — it specifically frames the finishing as the goal.

    What makes this natural: The trailing さ (あってさ) softens the excuse and invites the listener into the situation rather than just stating a fact. The casual contractions (なきゃ for なければ) mark this as close-friend register. The self-directed causative is extremely common in deadline and obligation contexts.

    How It Works¶

    The causative form adds a layer of agency to a verb — it doesn't just describe an action, it describes the causing of an action. The subject of the causative verb is not the one performing the action but the one making or allowing the action to happen.

    The making vs. letting distinction is not encoded in the form itself — it comes from three sources: the relationship between the people involved, the particles used (に marks the person being caused; を can mark the person in some contexts), and surrounding context. A parent making a child eat vegetables and a parent letting a child eat cake are both expressed with the same causative form — the situation tells you which is which.

    Self-directed causative — as in the input sentence — is a special and very common case. When the subject and the caused agent are the same person, the causative expresses control over an outcome: 終わらせる means "cause [it] to end / get [it] finished." This is common with result-oriented verbs like 終わる, 止まる, 動く.

    Layer — なきゃいけない: This is a double-contracted obligation form. なければ → なきゃ (casual contraction) + いけない (cannot go = must). Together: "must do X / have no choice but to do X." Combined with the causative: "must cause X to happen / must get X done."

    子供こどもを泣なかせてしまった。 I ended up making the child cry.
    Literal: Child cry-caused ended-up.
    Self-directed causative of result — the speaker caused the crying unintentionally. てしまった adds regret.

    Real Speech vs. Textbook Form¶

    Textbooks present the causative cleanly: 先生が生徒に宿題をさせた ("The teacher made the student do homework"). Real speech is messier — the causative appears inside longer chains, in self-directed contexts, and in contracted forms that look nothing like the textbook version.

    Form Example Note
    Textbook (clean) 先生せんせいが生徒せいとに宿題しゅくだいをさせた。 Both agents explicit, clean causative — rarely this neat in real speech
    Self-directed 終おわらせなきゃ。 "Gotta get it finished" — subject and caused agent are the same, dropped entirely
    Embedded in noun phrase 終おわらせなきゃいけない仕事しごと Causative embedded as a noun modifier — common in real speech
    Permission request させてください／させてもらえますか Set expressions — learn these as chunks

    Grammar Analysis¶

    Function in Context¶

    • 終わらせなきゃいけない is the core predicate embedded in a noun-modifying clause: 終わらせなきゃいけない仕事 — "work that must be gotten finished." The causative is inside the relative clause, not the main predicate.
    • があってさ closes the sentence — 仕事がある ("there is work") + さ (casual sentence-final softener). The full sentence is a soft excuse delivered as a shared situation, not a formal apology.
    • The causative of 終わる (intransitive: "to end/finish") is 終わらせる — the speaker becomes the agent who causes the ending to happen. This is common with intransitive verbs where the speaker wants to express control over an outcome they don't directly perform.
    • なきゃ (contracted なければ) + いけない creates the obligation: "if I don't cause it to finish, that won't do / I must get it finished." The double negation (なければ + いけない) is a standard N4 obligation pattern.
    • The subject (I/speaker) is entirely dropped — recoverable from context. The caused agent (the work reaching completion) is also implicit. This level of ellipsis is natural at this register.
    仕事しごとを終おわらせなきゃ。 I've gotta get this work finished.
    Literal: Work finish-cause-must.
    Contracted casual form — natural in spoken deadlines and obligation contexts.

    Construction¶

    Verb Type Dictionary Form Causative Form What Changed
    Godan (u-verbs) 書かく 書かかせる Final く (k-row) → か (a-row) + せる
    Godan 飲のむ 飲のませる Final む (m-row) → ま (a-row) + せる
    Godan 終おわる 終おわらせる Final る (r-row) → ら (a-row) + せる
    Godan 話はなす 話はなさせる Final す (s-row) → さ (a-row) + せる
    Ichidan (ru-verbs) 食たべる 食たべさせる Drop る + させる
    Ichidan 見みる 見みさせる Drop る + させる
    Irregular: する する させる Fully irregular
    Irregular: くる くる こさせる Fully irregular

    Godan vs. Ichidan — the common confusion

    Godan causative ends in 〜せる. Ichidan causative ends in 〜させる. Learners often apply させる to all verbs — 書かさせる is wrong; the correct form is 書かせる. If the verb is godan, you only need せる after shifting the final vowel to the あ-row.

    終わる is godan, not ichidan

    終わる ends in る but it is a godan verb — its causative is 終わらせる (shift わ-row to わ + せる), not 終わさせる. This is one of the most common godan/ichidan misclassification errors.

    Causative Particles — に vs. を¶

    The person being caused to act is usually marked with に. In some casual contexts を appears instead — the nuance shifts slightly toward directness or emphasis on the object.

    Particle Example Nuance
    に 子供こどもに野菜やさいを食たべさせた。 Standard — child is the caused agent marked with に
    を 子供こどもを泣なかせた。 を marks the caused person when the verb is intransitive — cannot use に here

    を vs. に rule

    When the caused verb is intransitive (no direct object), mark the caused person with を. When the caused verb is transitive (has a direct object), mark the caused person with に to avoid double-を sentences.

    Japanese Mindset¶

    English "make someone do X" and "let someone do X" are structurally different sentences. Japanese packages both meanings into a single grammatical form and relies on context, relationship, and surrounding words to disambiguate. This reflects a broader Japanese tendency to leave the social relationship implicit in the structure rather than spelling it out.

    The causative also encodes something English often states separately: who holds agency over the action. When a manager says 残業させた, the listener immediately knows the manager had the authority to cause the overtime — the causative signals the power relationship without stating it. When a parent says 子供に好きなものを食べさせてあげた, the てあげる reveals it was permission, not coercion.

    • Agency over outcomes. The self-directed causative (終わらせる, 止まらせる) reflects a Japanese tendency to frame control over results as caused rather than simply performed. "Getting work finished" is not just doing work — it is causing the state of completion to occur.
    • Reading the relationship from the form. Native speakers read the social dynamic from the causative choice automatically. Learning to do the same is the real N3 skill — not just producing the form correctly but understanding what it reveals about the relationship between the people involved.

    Nuance Comparison¶

    Pattern Core Meaning Emotional / Pragmatic Nuance Trigger Context JLPT
    〜させる／〜せる Make or let someone do X Neutral — could be either; context determines Any causative relationship N4
    〜させてあげる Let someone do X (as a favor) Permissive, generous — speaker facilitates willingly Granting permission to someone below or equal N4
    〜させてもらう I'll take the liberty of doing X Humble, self-permissive — speaker requests indulgence Politely claiming permission for yourself N3
    〜させてください Please let me do X Polite request for permission Asking someone above or equal for permission N3
    〜させられる Be made to do X Passive — speaker is the one being caused; often unwilling Describing unwanted obligation or coercion [N2 — awareness only] N2
    〜てもらう Have someone do X for you Receiving-frame — speaker benefits from another's action Requesting or receiving help N4

    Alternatives¶

    〜てもらう (receiving frame)¶

    Instead of framing yourself as causing an action, frame yourself as receiving the benefit of someone else's action. Softer and less authoritative than the causative.
    Example ハルに手伝てつだってもらった。
    English "I had Haru help me / I received help from Haru."
    Intent Receiving frame — the speaker benefits; Haru's agency is respected
    vs. original Causative (手伝わせた) implies the speaker directed Haru to help. てもらう implies Haru helped willingly and the speaker received that help gratefully.

    When to use this instead

    Prefer てもらう when you want to acknowledge the other person's agency and goodwill. Using the causative when てもらう would be more natural can sound cold or presumptuous.

    〜なければならない (obligation, more formal)¶

    A more formal alternative to なきゃいけない for the same obligation meaning. Grammatically identical logic, different register.
    Example 今日中きょうじゅうに終おわらせなければならない仕事しごとがある。
    English "There is work that must be finished by end of today."
    Intent Same obligation, more formal delivery
    vs. original なきゃいけない is casual spoken; なければならない is written or formal spoken. Same meaning, different register dial.

    〜ないといけない (casual obligation variant)¶

    Another casual obligation form that swaps in ないと for なきゃ. Slightly different rhythm — both are equally natural in casual speech.
    Example 終おわらせないといけない仕事しごとがある。
    English "I've got work I've gotta finish."
    Intent Identical meaning to なきゃいけない — natural casual obligation
    vs. original Interchangeable in most casual contexts. なきゃ is slightly more contracted and emphatic; ないと is a touch softer.

    Learner trap — てあげる overuse

    Adding てあげる to every permissive causative (子供に食べさせてあげた) sounds natural in the right context, but overusing it can sound patronising — it emphasises that you were generous enough to allow something. When the permission is neutral or expected, leave てあげる out.

    Register and Formality¶

    今日中きょうじゅうに終おわらせなきゃいけない仕事しごとがあってさ。

    "I've got work I gotta finish by end of today."

    Shift: Input sentence as-is. Casual register — なきゃいけない contracted, さ as soft sentence closer.

    今日中きょうじゅうに終おわらせなければならない仕事しごとがありまして。

    "I have some work that I need to get finished by end of today."

    Shift: なきゃいけない → なければならない (formal obligation); あってさ → がありまして (polite て-form, soft trailing). More composed but the situational explanation function is preserved.

    本日中ほんじつちゅうに完了かんりょうさせなければならない業務ぎょうむがございまして、大変たいへん申もうし訳わけございません。

    "I have work that must be completed by end of today — I sincerely apologise for the inconvenience."

    Shift: 今日中 → 本日中 (formal date reference); 仕事 → 業務 (formal term for work duties); あって → ございまして (formal copula te-form); full apology formula added. Now a formal excuse to a client or superior.

    Grammar Lineage¶

    Step Pattern What It Adds Example
    N4 — Foundation Plain verb forms Core godan/ichidan conjugation system the causative builds on 書かく、食たべる、する
    N4 — Foundation て-form + あげる／もらう／くれる Giving and receiving frames — directionality of benefit 手伝てつだってもらった。"Had someone help me."
    N4 — Core ← You are here 〜させる／〜せる (causative) Adds a causer — subject makes or lets someone else do the action 終おわらせる。"Cause [it] to finish / get [it] done."
    N4 — Extended させてください／させてもらう Causative in set permission expressions — extremely high frequency させてください。"Please let me do this."
    N3 — Extended Causative + social nuance Reading making vs. letting from context; register-appropriate causative choice 子供こどもに好すきなものを食たべさせた。(Made? Let? Depends entirely on context.)
    N2 — Awareness 〜させられる (causative-passive) Double stacking — being made to do something against your will 残業ざんぎょうさせられた。"I was made to work overtime." [Coming soon]

    Examples¶

    子供こどもに好すきなものを食たべさせてあげた。 I let the child eat whatever they liked.
    Literal: Child-to liked-things eat-caused-gave.

    ~Context: Parent granting permission — てあげる confirms this is letting, not making. The parent held agency and chose to allow.~

    部長ぶちょうが全員ぜんいんに残業ざんぎょうさせた。 The manager made everyone work overtime.
    Literal: Department-head everyone-to overtime work-caused.

    ~Context: Authority causing compliance — coercion reading is clear from the power relationship. No one chose to stay late.~

    もう少し考えさせてください。 Please let me think about it a little more.
    Literal: A-little-more think-cause-please.

    ~Context: Set expression — させてください is a polite request for permission. Extremely common in business and everyday speech.~

    ハルが泣ないてて、私わたしまで泣なかされた。 Haru was crying and it made me cry too.
    Literal: Haru crying, me-even cry-was-caused.

    ~Context: Unintentional emotional causation — 泣かされた is a causative-passive-lite reading; the crying was caused without the speaker's intention or control. Preview of the N2 form.~

    今日きょうだけは好すきなだけ食たべさせて。 Just for today, let me eat as much as I want.
    Literal: Today only liked-amount eat-cause [please].

    ~Context: Causative as permission request — the speaker is asking to be allowed to eat freely. させて (causative te-form) as a casual request for permission.~

    Common Mistakes¶

    1. Applying させる to godan verbs¶

    Learners learn させる first and apply it to everything. Godan verbs take せる after the あ-row shift, not させる.

    Incorrect

    書かか[させる]{.mistake}

    ~Godan verbs don't take させる. This would sound ungrammatical to a native speaker.~

    Correct

    書かかせる

    ~Godan: shift final consonant to あ-row + せる. 書く → 書か + せる → 書かせる.~

    2. Misclassifying godan verbs ending in る¶

    Verbs like 終わる、帰る、走る end in る but are godan. Their causative takes せる after the あ-row shift, not させる.

    Incorrect

    終おわる → 終おわ[させる]{.mistake}

    ~終わる is godan. Applying ichidan rules produces an ungrammatical form.~

    Correct

    終おわる → 終おわらせる

    ~Godan あ-row shift: わ → わら + せる. 終わらせる.~

    3. Particle confusion — double を¶

    When the caused verb is transitive (already has a direct object marked with を), the caused person must be marked with に, not を — or you'll have two を-marked nouns in one sentence, which is ungrammatical.

    Incorrect

    子供こども[を]{.mistake}野菜やさいを食たべさせた。

    ~Two を in one clause — ungrammatical. 野菜を already takes the direct object slot.~

    Correct

    子供こどもに野菜やさいを食たべさせた。

    ~に marks the caused person when the caused verb is transitive. 野菜を keeps the direct object slot.~

    When NOT to Use It¶

    Using causative when てもらう is more natural

    If someone helped you voluntarily, using the causative to describe it sounds like you directed them — which can come across as cold or presumptuous.

    Avoid Use instead
    ハルに手伝てつだわせた。"I made/had Haru help." — implies authority or direction ハルに手伝てつだってもらった。"Haru helped me / I had Haru's help." — acknowledges Haru's goodwill

    Collocation Notes¶

    Collocation Meaning
    〜を終わらせる "Get X finished / cause X to end" — goal-oriented self-directed causative; extremely common in deadline contexts
    させてください "Please let me do X" — set polite permission request; learn as a fixed chunk
    させてもらう "I'll take the liberty of doing X / I'll be allowed to do X" — humble self-permission; very high frequency
    させてもらえますか "Would you allow me to do X?" — polite permission question
    〜を泣かせる "Make someone cry" — common emotional causative; 泣かせる is its own near-fixed expression
    〜に待たせる "Make someone wait" — 待たせてごめん ("Sorry for making you wait") is a daily-use set phrase

    ~These collocations are worth learning whole rather than assembling from the causative rule each time. させてください and 待たせてごめん especially will appear before you finish N4.~

    Hidden Points¶

    • The causative of intransitive verbs is extremely common. Intransitive verbs (those without a direct object — 終わる、泣く、止まる、動く) are frequently used in the causative to express causing an outcome rather than performing an action. 終わらせる ("cause it to end"), 止まらせる ("cause it to stop"), 動かせる ("cause it to move") are all natural and common. This is a productive pattern worth internalizing early.
    • させてもらう is a social lubricant. This form — literally "receive the causation of X / be allowed to do X" — is one of the most useful expressions at any level because it lets you claim permission for yourself in a humble, face-saving way. 確認させてもらいます ("I'll go ahead and check on that") is politer than 確認します and more natural than させてください in many professional contexts.
    • Causative + て-form chains. The causative te-form (させて／せて) is the gateway to させてあげる、させてもらう、させてください — three of the highest-frequency causative expressions. Master the causative te-form and you unlock all three at once.
    • 待たせてごめん is your first causative set phrase. Most learners encounter the causative naturally for the first time through this apology — "sorry for making you wait." It's worth knowing from day one even if you haven't formally studied the causative yet.

    Being made to do something — the causative and passive stacked together. Expresses unwilling obligation or coercion from an outside force. 残業させられた ("I was made to work overtime") is the classic example. This section will cover construction, nuance, and how to distinguish it from the plain causative.

    させてください — Permission Requests in Depth

    The させてください family of expressions (させてください、させてもらう、させてもらえますか、させていただく) covers one of the most nuanced areas of Japanese politeness. This section will cover when to use each form, the social distance each implies, and the keigo version させていただく.

    Causative in Formal and Keigo Contexts

    How the causative behaves in business Japanese and formal speech — including させていただく (the humble causative that has become ubiquitous in service and business contexts) and how native speakers actually feel about its overuse.

    Learner Stage¶

    Stage What It Looks Like
    Now — N4 Produce the causative form correctly for godan, ichidan, and irregular verbs; recognise it in sentences; understand the basic making/letting distinction; use 終わらせる, 待たせてごめん, and させてください as working chunks
    Developing — N4→N3 Read the making vs. letting distinction from context without conscious analysis; choose between causative and てもらう based on social nuance; start using させてもらう naturally for self-permission
    Automatic — N3→N2 The causative registers immediately as a social signal — you hear who has agency before the sentence finishes; させていただく sounds natural rather than stiff; you notice when someone uses the causative where てもらう would be warmer
    Mastery You feel the social weight of causative choice in real time — you know when using the causative instead of てもらう signals authority, and when it signals coldness; you deploy させてもらう and させてください situationally without thinking

    • 〜てあげる — you do something for someone else's benefit

    Builds From¶

    • Plain verb conjugation (N5/N4) — godan and ichidan forms that the causative modifies
    • て-form (N5) — causative te-form (させて) unlocks させてください, させてもらう, させてあげる
    • なければいけない / なきゃいけない (N4) — obligation form that frequently combines with causative
