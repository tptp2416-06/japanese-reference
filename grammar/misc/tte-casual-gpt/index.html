    って — Casual Topic Marker / “As for…”¶

    Reference Sentence¶

    日本にほんって本当ほんとうにきれいだよね。
    Japan is really beautiful, isn’t it?
    As for Japan, it’s really beautiful, huh?

    JLPT Level¶

    JLPT Level — N4 to Lower N3

    って is common from N4 onward, but using it naturally as a topic marker in real conversation feels more Lower N3.

    At the beginner level, って is often taught as a casual version of と for quoting.

    At the Lower N3 level, it becomes important to understand って as a natural spoken topic marker, similar to は but more casual, conversational, and emotionally immediate.

    The main uses of って are:

    Use Level Meaning Example
    Quote marker N4 “that…” / casual と 行いくって言いってた。
    Topic marker N4–N3 “as for…” / “speaking of…” 日本にほんってきれいだよね。
    Definition / explanation N3 “X means…” / “X is…” 友情ゆうじょうって大事だいじだよね。
    Surprise / reaction N3 “Wait, X?” / “You mean X?” 明日あしたって、休やすみなの？

    Quick Summary¶

    At a glance

    って is one of the most common casual spoken Japanese markers.

    In the reference sentence, it works like a soft casual topic marker:

    日本にほんって本当ほんとうにきれいだよね。
    “Japan is really beautiful, isn’t it?”

    It is close to は, but not exactly the same.

    The basic contrast is:

    Marker Feeling
    日本にほんは neutral / standard topic
    日本にほんって casual / conversational / “speaking of Japan…”
    日本にほんについては formal / “regarding Japan…”
    日本にほんなんだけど conversational topic setup / “about Japan…”

    Core Meaning¶

    Core meaning

    って casually picks something up as the topic of conversation.

    It often means “as for…,” “speaking of…,” “when it comes to…,” “you know, X is…,” or “about X…”

    In the reference sentence:

    日本にほんって本当ほんとうにきれいだよね。
    Japan is really beautiful, isn’t it?

    The speaker is not making a formal statement about Japan. They are bringing Japan into the conversation as a shared topic, then giving a personal reaction.

    A more literal structure would be:

    “Speaking of Japan / when it comes to Japan, it’s really beautiful, isn’t it?”

    But natural English usually just says:

    “Japan is really beautiful, isn’t it?”

    In Real Life¶

    Natural conversation use

    って is extremely common when the speaker wants to casually introduce, react to, define, or comment on a topic.

    It often appears when the speaker is sharing an impression, asking for confirmation, or softening the feeling of “I am now making X the topic.”

    Example Context¶

    Two people are looking at photos from a trip.

    Speaker 1: この写真しゃしん、すごくきれいだね。
    This photo is really pretty.

    Speaker 2: うん。日本にほんって本当ほんとうにきれいだよね。
    Yeah. Japan is really beautiful, isn’t it?

    Here, って sounds natural because Speaker 2 is not making a detached textbook-style statement. They are reacting emotionally and conversationally.

    How It Works¶

    Pattern¶

    Topic + って + comment / reaction / explanation

    The reference sentence breaks down like this:

    Part Function In the sentence
    Topic What the speaker brings up 日本にほん
    って Casual topic marker って
    Comment What the speaker says about it 本当ほんとうにきれいだよね

    The Basic Shape¶

    日本にほんって、きれいだよね。
    Japan is beautiful, isn’t it?

    この店みせって、けっこう人気にんきあるよね。
    This place is pretty popular, isn’t it?

    東京とうきょうって、夜よるでも明あかるいね。
    Tokyo is bright even at night, huh?

    Grammar Analysis¶

    って as a Casual Topic Marker¶

    In:

    日本にほんって本当ほんとうにきれいだよね。

    って marks 日本にほん as the topic.

    The sentence is built like this:

    Piece Role Meaning
    日本にほん Topic Japan
    って Casual topic marker as for / speaking of
    本当ほんとうに Adverb really / truly
    きれい な-adjective beautiful / clean
    だ Casual copula is
    よね Shared-feeling sentence ending “right?” / “isn’t it?”

    Why って Instead of は?¶

    Both are grammatical:

    日本にほんは本当ほんとうにきれいだよね。
    Japan is really beautiful, isn’t it?

    日本にほんって本当ほんとうにきれいだよね。
    Japan is really beautiful, isn’t it?

    The difference is not the basic meaning. The difference is the conversational feel.

    Version Nuance
    日本にほんは本当ほんとうにきれいだよね。 Neutral. Sounds like a normal statement about Japan.
    日本にほんって本当ほんとうにきれいだよね。 More conversational. Sounds like the speaker is bringing up Japan as a shared topic or personal impression.

    Simple learner rule

    Use は when you want a neutral topic.

    Use って when you want a more casual, spoken, “you know, X is…” feeling.

    Japanese Mindset¶

    English often uses word order to introduce topics:

    “Japan is really beautiful.”

    Japanese often marks the topic explicitly:

    日本にほんはきれい。
    日本にほんってきれい。

    The important point is that って does not just mark grammar. It also creates conversational texture.

    It feels like the speaker is saying:

    “Taking Japan as the thing we’re talking about…”
    “Speaking of Japan…”
    “You know Japan? It’s really beautiful.”

    This is why って often feels warmer, more personal, and more spontaneous than は.

    Japanese mindset

    って often feels like the speaker is picking up a topic with their hand and placing it into the conversation.

    は can sound like the topic is already established.

    って often sounds like the speaker is actively introducing or re-framing the topic.

    って vs は¶

    Core Difference¶

    The simplest contrast is:

    Marker Core Function Feeling
    は Standard topic marker neutral, stable, explanatory
    って Casual topic marker spoken, reactive, conversational

    は — Neutral Topic¶

    日本にほんはきれいです。
    Japan is beautiful.

    This sounds neutral and general. It could appear in a textbook, essay, presentation, or normal conversation.

    って — Casual Spoken Topic¶

    日本にほんってきれいだよね。
    Japan is beautiful, isn’t it?

    This sounds more conversational. It feels like the speaker is sharing an impression.

    When って Sounds Better Than は¶

    って often sounds better when the sentence is casual, emotional, opinion-based, reactive, conversational, spoken aloud, or asking for agreement.

    Common cases where って sounds natural:

    Natural with って Why
    日本にほんってきれいだよね。 Personal impression
    この映画えいがって面白おもしろいよね。 Shared reaction
    大人おとなになるって大変たいへんだね。 Reflective comment
    一人暮ひとりぐらしって楽たのしいけど、ちょっと寂さびしいね。 General life-topic reflection

    って vs について¶

    について — Formal “About / Regarding”¶

    について means “about” or “regarding.” It is more formal, specific, and topic-focused than って.

    The contrast is:

    Pattern Feel
    Topic + って casual topic introduction
    Topic + について about / regarding; formal or structured

    Compare¶

    日本にほんって本当ほんとうにきれいだよね。
    Japan is really beautiful, isn’t it?

    This is casual and emotional.

    日本にほんについて話はなしましょう。
    Let’s talk about Japan.

    This is structured and formal.

    日本にほんについてレポートを書かきました。
    I wrote a report about Japan.

    This is not a casual topic marker. It marks the subject matter of a report, discussion, lecture, or explanation.

    Do not overuse について

    Learners sometimes use について when they simply want to say “as for X.”

    But について often sounds too formal or too report-like for casual emotional comments.

    ❌ 日本にほんについて本当ほんとうにきれいだよね。
    This sounds unnatural because について does not fit a casual emotional reaction.

    ✅ 日本にほんって本当ほんとうにきれいだよね。
    Natural casual speech.

    って vs なんだけど¶

    なんだけど is another way to introduce a topic, but it works differently from って.

    The core difference is:

    Pattern Function
    Topic + って casually marks the topic
    Topic + なんだけど sets up a topic before asking, explaining, complaining, or continuing

    って¶

    この店みせって、けっこう高たかいよね。
    This place is pretty expensive, isn’t it?

    The speaker directly comments on the topic.

    なんだけど¶

    この店みせなんだけど、ちょっと高たかくない？
    About this place… isn’t it kind of expensive?

    なんだけど creates a setup. It often signals that the speaker has something else coming:

    • a question
    • a concern
    • a complaint
    • a request
    • an explanation

    Key difference

    って marks the topic.

    なんだけど opens a conversational lane.

    It often means: “About X…” or “So, regarding X…”

    って vs というのは¶

    というのは is the fuller, more formal version behind many uses of って.

    The formality scale is:

    Form Register
    って casual spoken
    というのは formal / explanatory
    というものは more general / written / philosophical

    Compare¶

    日本にほんって本当ほんとうにきれいだよね。
    Japan is really beautiful, isn’t it?

    日本にほんというのは、本当ほんとうに美うつくしい国くにですね。
    Japan is truly a beautiful country.

    The second sentence sounds much more formal, explanatory, and possibly speech-like or essay-like.

    Useful mental shortcut

    って often behaves like a casual compressed form of というのは.

    But do not translate every って as “というのは.” In real conversation, って is much lighter and more flexible.

    Quote Marker って¶

    って also works as a casual quote marker.

    This is probably the first use many learners encounter.

    The quote-marker contrast is:

    Formal / neutral Casual
    と って

    Examples¶

    明日あした来くるって言いってたよ。
    They said they’re coming tomorrow.

    大丈夫だいじょうぶだって。
    They said it’s okay. / Apparently it’s okay.

    彼かれ、もう帰かえったって。
    He said he already went home. / Apparently he already went home.

    How Quote って Connects to Topic って¶

    The topic-marker use and quote-marker use feel related because both involve “pulling in” some content.

    The difference is what って pulls into the sentence:

    Use What って pulls in
    Quote って pulls in what someone said
    Topic って pulls in what we are talking about

    Compare:

    日本にほんってきれいだよね。
    Japan is beautiful, isn’t it?
    Topic: 日本にほん

    日本にほんって言いってたよ。
    They said “Japan.” / They were saying Japan.
    Quote: 日本にほん

    Context tells you which one it is.

    Register and Formality¶

    Casual¶

    日本にほんって本当ほんとうにきれいだよね。
    Japan is really beautiful, isn’t it?

    Natural between friends, partners, classmates, coworkers in casual mode, or in relaxed spoken conversation.

    Basic Polite¶

    日本にほんって本当ほんとうにきれいですよね。
    Japan is really beautiful, isn’t it?

    Still conversational, but polite. This is common in friendly polite conversation.

    More Neutral / Standard Polite¶

    日本にほんは本当ほんとうにきれいですよね。
    Japan is really beautiful, isn’t it?

    Using は makes the sentence more neutral and less casually topic-introducing.

    Formal / Presentation Style¶

    日本にほんは本当ほんとうに美うつくしい国くにです。
    Japan is truly a beautiful country.

    This is better for formal writing, speeches, essays, or presentations.

    Formality warning

    って is not rude, but it is casual.

    Avoid using topic-marker って in formal writing, reports, business documents, essays, or very polite speech unless the tone is intentionally conversational.

    Nuance Comparison¶

    Here is the same general topic handled with different markers:

    Expression Natural Translation Nuance
    日本にほんはきれいだよね。 Japan is beautiful, isn’t it? neutral topic
    日本にほんってきれいだよね。 Japan is beautiful, isn’t it? casual, conversational, shared impression
    日本にほんについて話はなそう。 Let’s talk about Japan. about / regarding; structured topic
    日本にほんなんだけど、どう思おもう？ About Japan, what do you think? sets up a discussion
    日本にほんというのは美うつくしい国くにです。 Japan is a beautiful country. formal explanatory framing

    Common Patterns¶

    Noun + って + Comment¶

    この映画えいがって、けっこう長ながいね。
    This movie is pretty long, huh?

    日本語にほんごって難むずかしいけど、面白おもしろいね。
    Japanese is hard, but interesting, isn’t it?

    朝あさの電車でんしゃって、いつも混こんでるよね。
    Morning trains are always crowded, aren’t they?

    Question Word + って¶

    って is also common when asking about a word, idea, person, place, or meaning.

    これって何なに？
    What is this?

    山田やまださんって、どんな人ひと？
    What kind of person is Yamada?

    「わびさび」ってどういう意味いみ？
    What does “wabi-sabi” mean?

    Topic + って + Adjective¶

    この服ふくってかわいいね。
    This outfit is cute, isn’t it?

    夏なつの京都きょうとって暑あついよね。
    Kyoto in summer is hot, isn’t it?

    彼かれの話はなしって長ながいけど、面白おもしろいよね。
    His stories are long, but interesting, aren’t they?

    Examples¶

    Basic Examples¶

    日本にほんって本当ほんとうにきれいだよね。
    Japan is really beautiful, isn’t it?

    このカフェって落おち着つくね。
    This café feels relaxing, doesn’t it?

    東京とうきょうって人ひとが多おおいけど、便利べんりだよね。
    Tokyo has a lot of people, but it’s convenient, isn’t it?

    一人暮ひとりぐらしって自由じゆうだけど、たまに寂さびしいね。
    Living alone is freeing, but sometimes lonely, huh?

    日本語にほんごって、聞きけば聞きくほど面白おもしろいね。
    The more you listen to Japanese, the more interesting it gets, huh?

    Mini Dialogue¶

    Speaker 1: 旅行りょこう、どうだった？
    How was the trip?

    Speaker 2: すごくよかった。日本にほんって本当ほんとうにきれいだよね。
    It was really good. Japan is really beautiful, isn’t it?

    Speaker 1: わかる。写真しゃしんだけでも伝つたわる。
    I get that. You can tell even just from the photos.

    Common Mistakes¶

    Mistake 1 — Using について for every “about”

    ❌ 日本にほんについて本当ほんとうにきれいだよね。

    This sounds unnatural because について is too formal and too “subject matter”-like for this casual emotional comment.

    ✅ 日本にほんって本当ほんとうにきれいだよね。

    Mistake 2 — Thinking って always means “said”

    って can mark a quote, but it does not always mean “said.”

    ✅ 日本にほんってきれいだよね。
    Japan is beautiful, isn’t it?

    Here, って is not quoting anyone. It marks 日本にほん as the topic.

    Mistake 3 — Using って in formal writing

    Topic-marker って is natural in speech, but it is usually too casual for formal essays, reports, or business writing.

    Casual:

    日本にほんってきれいですよね。

    Formal:

    日本にほんは美うつくしい国くにです。

    Collocation Notes¶

    って commonly appears with these patterns:

    Pattern Example Use
    Xって + adjective 日本にほんってきれいだね。 casual impression
    Xって + noun phrase 彼かれって先生せんせいなの？ identity / category
    Xって + どういう意味いみ？ これってどういう意味いみ？ asking meaning
    Xって + どんな人ひと？ 山田やまださんってどんな人ひと？ asking about a person
    Xって + よね 日本語にほんごって面白おもしろいよね。 shared opinion
    Xって + さ 日本にほんってさ、本当ほんとうにきれいだよね。 casual topic setup

    ってさ

    ってさ is a more casual, slightly more “bringing this up now” version of って.

    日本にほんってさ、本当ほんとうにきれいだよね。
    You know, Japan is really beautiful, isn’t it?

    It sounds more conversational and intimate than plain って.

    Hidden Points¶

    Hidden point 1 — って often feels more subjective than は

    日本にほんはきれいだよね sounds like a general statement.

    日本にほんってきれいだよね sounds more like the speaker is personally reacting to the idea of Japan.

    This is why って often pairs naturally with よね.

    Hidden point 2 — って can make the sentence feel less stiff

    Learners often overuse は because it is the standard topic marker.

    But in spoken Japanese, って can make the sentence feel more natural when introducing an opinion, impression, or definition.

    日本語にほんごは難むずかしいですね。
    Japanese is difficult, isn’t it?
    Neutral / textbook-like.

    日本語にほんごって難むずかしいですよね。
    Japanese is difficult, isn’t it?
    More conversational.

    Hidden point 3 — って often creates a “shared topic” feeling

    In 日本にほんって本当ほんとうにきれいだよね, the speaker is not just stating an opinion.

    The よね invites agreement, and って makes 日本にほん feel like a shared topic between speaker and listener.

    Alternatives¶

    1. は¶

    日本にほんは本当ほんとうにきれいだよね。
    Japan is really beautiful, isn’t it?

    Change: って → は
    Effect: More neutral and standard. Less conversationally vivid.

    2. ってさ¶

    日本にほんってさ、本当ほんとうにきれいだよね。
    You know, Japan is really beautiful, isn’t it?

    Change: って → ってさ
    Effect: More casual and conversational. Feels like the speaker is gently bringing up the topic.

    3. なんだけど¶

    日本にほんなんだけど、本当ほんとうにきれいだよね。
    About Japan — it’s really beautiful, isn’t it?

    Change: って → なんだけど
    Effect: Sounds like the speaker is setting up a topic. Slightly heavier than って.

    4. については¶

    日本にほんについては、本当ほんとうにきれいだと思おもいます。
    Regarding Japan, I think it is truly beautiful.

    Change: って → については
    Effect: Much more formal and structured. Better for essays, presentations, or reports.

    5. というのは¶

    日本にほんというのは、本当ほんとうに美うつくしい国くにですね。
    Japan is truly a beautiful country.

    Change: って → というのは
    Effect: More formal, explanatory, and reflective.

    Learner Stage¶

    What you should be able to do at Lower N3

    At Lower N3, you do not need to master every advanced use of って.

    Focus on topic-marker って, meaning-question って, and casual quote-marker って.

    1. Topic Marker¶

    日本にほんってきれいだよね。
    Japan is beautiful, isn’t it?

    2. Meaning Question¶

    これってどういう意味いみ？
    What does this mean?

    3. Casual Quote¶

    明日あした来くるって言いってたよ。
    They said they’re coming tomorrow.

    If you can recognize these three uses, most everyday って sentences will become much easier to process.
