    Conversational Topic Marker — 〜でさ¶

    〜でさ — "About [X]..." / "So, regarding [X]..."¶

    JLPT Level¶

    JLPT Level — Lower N3

    The individual components (て-form connective and sentence-final さ) are N4, but the pragmatic function — using でさ to float a topic and pull the listener's attention into a conversation shift — is a lower N3 conversational skill.

    Tier Pattern Description Example
    N4 — Foundation て-form as connective Links clauses; marks reason or means in certain patterns. 聞きいて、わかった。"I heard and understood."
    N4 — Foundation Sentence-final さ Casual emphasis particle; "you know" / "like" — marks information as obvious or asserts a personal stance. そりゃそうさ。"Well, of course."
    N3 — Core ← You are here 〜でさ (topic-introducing) Te-form of だ/です + casual さ — stretches the copula into a soft topic hook that pauses mid-sentence and signals "this is what I want to talk about." リホのことでさ、ちょっと聞ききたいことあるんだけど。"About Riho — there's something I want to ask you about."
    N3 — Extended 〜でさあ Lengthened vowel adds thinking time or mild emotional loading — more reflective or hesitant. あのさあ、今日きょうの件けんなんだけどさあ…"So, about today's thing…"
    N2 — Awareness 〜でありまして Formal equivalent in keigo — である connective + polite て-form. Used in business to introduce a topic respectfully. 先日せんじつの件けんでありまして…"Regarding the matter from the other day…"

    Quick Summary¶

    At a glance

    • 〜でさ is a conversational topic-introducer — it floats a noun or situation into the conversation and pauses, signalling that the speaker is about to say something about it.
    • It's built from the て-form of だ (nominal copula) + casual sentence-particle さ.
    • The function is attention-direction: it marks the preceding item as the conversation's new focus, while the pause (often an ellipsis or a new clause) lets the listener catch up.
    • Extremely common in casual spoken Japanese between friends, family, and close colleagues. You'll hear it constantly in drama, variety shows, and everyday conversation.
    • The input sentence — リホのことでさ、ちょっと聞きたいことあるんだけど — is the canonical pattern: [topic + でさ] + [pause] + [what the speaker wants to say about that topic].

    Core Meaning¶

    Core Rule

    Use でさ after a noun or nominal phrase to introduce it as the topic of the upcoming statement — especially at the start of a conversation, when shifting topics, or when the topic is sensitive and needs a soft approach. The さ reaches toward the listener, signalling "I'm telling you this" or "listen."

    リホのことでさ、ちょっと聞ききたいことあるんだけど。 About Riho — there's something I want to ask you.
    Literal: Riho's matter-regarding-and-you-know, a-little want-to-ask thing exists-but.

    ~Context: The input sentence. The speaker is broaching a topic involving a third person. でさ softens the entry — it's not a blunt "tell me about Riho," it's a careful "so, about Riho…"~

    昨日きのうの件けんでさ、少すこし話はなしたいことがあるんだ。 About what happened yesterday — there's something I want to talk to you about.
    Literal: Yesterday's matter-regarding-and-you-know, a-little want-to-talk thing exists.

    ~Context: Similar structure. A sensitive or important topic being introduced with でさ as the softening hook.~

    In Real Life¶

    In Real Life

    Situation: ハル and ミサキ are coworkers and friends. ハル recently heard something about a mutual colleague, リホ, and wants to check it with ミサキ during lunch. He doesn't want to sound like he's gossiping — just casually raising the topic.

    あのさ、リホのことでさ、ちょっと聞きたいことあるんだけど…

    "Hey, so… about Riho — there's something I wanted to ask you."

    Why でさ fits here: The topic — リホのこと — is a person-related matter, inherently sensitive. でさ achieves three things simultaneously: it marks リホのこと as the topic, it pauses to let ミサキ register the shift in focus, and it softens what could otherwise sound like an interrogation ("tell me about Riho"). The trailing んだけど adds another layer of softening — the speaker isn't demanding an answer, just indicating the conversation isn't complete.

    What makes this natural: The double さ (あのさ + でさ) is extremely common — the first さ gets attention, the second さ hooks the topic. The combination of でさ + んだけど is one of the most frequent patterns for broaching a topic you're not sure how to start.

    How It Works¶

    This pattern is deceptively simple — just で + さ — but each piece carries pragmatic weight. Understanding the layers reveals why this tiny chunk does so much conversational work.

    Layer 1 — で (the te-form of だ): だ is the plain copula ("is/are"). Its te-form, で, functions as a connective — it links a noun to whatever follows. In the pattern 〜で…, the で pauses the sentence mid-way, creating a hook: "It is X, and…" The listener leans in to hear what comes after "and."

    Layer 2 — さ (the attention/reach particle): Sentence-final さ is a casual particle with two main functions: it asserts that the statement is obvious or personally held ("you know"), and it reaches toward the listener, pulling them into the speaker's perspective. In でさ, the さ does the reaching — it makes the topic hook interpersonal, not just structural. "About X, you know? — and here's what I want to say."

    Layer 3 — The pause: After でさ, there is almost always a pause — a comma in writing, a brief silence or あの／えーと in speech. This pause is functional: it lets the listener absorb the topic shift and signals that the main point is about to arrive. Conversely, the pause lets the speaker gauge the listener's reaction before committing to the full statement.

    今日きょうの会議かいぎでさ、気きになったことがあって。 About today's meeting — there was something that bothered me.
    Literal: Today's meeting-regarding-you-know, bothered thing exists-and.
    The でさ hooks the meeting as topic; the pause lets the listener prepare for the concern.

    Real Speech vs. Textbook Form¶

    Textbook Japanese teaches 〜について (regarding) and 〜のことですが (formal topic introduction). These are grammatically correct but socially wrong for casual conversation — they sound stiff and written. でさ is what actual friends use.

    Form Example Note
    Natural spoken リホのことでさ、ちょっと… The canonical casual topic-introducer. Warm, human, unrehearsed.
    Textbook / formal spoken リホのことについてですが、… Grammatically correct but stiff in casual speech. Sounds like a meeting, not a conversation.
    Semi-casual connective リホのことなんだけど、… Also natural. Swaps でさ for なんだけど — slightly more explanatory than attention-getting.
    Overly blunt リホのこと、聞きたい。 Dropping the softening entirely — sounds abrupt and demanding.

    Grammar Analysis¶

    Function in Context¶

    In the input sentence — リホのことでさ、ちょっと聞きたいことあるんだけど — each piece contributes to a carefully soft approach.

    • リホのこと is the topic base. のこと wraps the person's name in "matters concerning," which is already more indirect than naming Riho as a direct object.
    • で attaches the topic to what follows — it says "this is the topic, and I'm about to say something about it."
    • さ reaches toward the listener — "you know? I'm bringing this up." It signals that the speaker considers the topic worth the listener's attention.
    • The pause after でさ lets the listener register the topic before the request arrives.
    • ちょっと聞きたいことあるんだけど is the payload, itself softened by ちょっと, the んだけど trailing form, and the overall indirectness.

    Without でさ: リホのこと聞きたいことあるんだけど is grammatical but lands more abruptly. The でさ creates the conversational space that turns a demand into a shared observation.

    Construction¶

    Component Form Function
    Noun / nominal phrase [X] The topic being introduced
    のこと (optional but common) 〜のこと "Matters concerning X" — softens the topic, especially for people
    Copula だ → te-form で Connective: "It is X, and…"
    Sentence particle さ Casual attention-getter / interpersonal reach
    Pause (…) or comma Lets the listener absorb the topic; signals the main point is coming
    Main clause [statement / request / question] What the speaker wants to say about the topic
    Topic + でさ Meaning
    昨日きのうの件けん 昨日きのうの件けんでさ "About what happened yesterday…"
    仕事しごとのこと 仕事しごとのことでさ "About work…"
    来週らいしゅうの予定よてい 来週らいしゅうの予定よていでさ "About next week's schedule…"
    あの話はなし あの話はなしでさ "About that thing we talked about…"

    でさ only attaches to nouns and nominal phrases

    で is the te-form of the copula だ, so it only follows nouns, na-adjective stems, or nominalised phrases. You cannot attach でさ directly to a verb or i-adjective. For those, use んだけどさ or てさ instead.

    Japanese Mindset¶

    English speakers often introduce a topic bluntly — "About Riho — what's going on with her?" or "I want to talk about work." The directness is unremarkable in English. In Japanese, entering a topic directly — especially a sensitive one or one involving another person — can feel abrupt or invasive.

    でさ solves this by stretching the topic introduction across a soft boundary. The で pauses; the さ reaches out; the listener has a moment to register the topic and signal receptivity (or lack of it) before the speaker commits to the full statement. This is the conversational equivalent of knocking before entering — the speaker announces the topic and waits a beat.

    • Soft entry as social grace. でさ signals that the speaker is aware they're shifting the conversation and respects the listener's attention. The pause isn't hesitation — it's considerateness.
    • さ as interpersonal glue. さ in this position tells the listener "I'm bringing this up with you specifically — this is between us." It closes the distance between speaker and listener, which is why it only works in in-group casual speech.
    • のこと as further softening. When the topic is a person, のこと adds an extra layer — "matters concerning Riho" rather than "Riho herself." This is a recurring pattern in Japanese: people are approached through their circumstances, not directly.

    Nuance Comparison¶

    Grammar Point Core Meaning Emotional / Pragmatic Nuance Trigger Context JLPT
    〜でさ "About X…" — soft topic hook Casual, interpersonal; pauses the sentence and reaches toward the listener Introducing a topic in casual conversation; especially sensitive or important topics N3
    〜について "Regarding X" Formal, written; no interpersonal warmth — pure information structure Formal speech, writing, presentations N4
    〜のことなんだけど "It's about X…" Explanatory; softer than bare copula but less reaching than でさ Casual topic introduction with explanatory nuance N3
    〜のことで "Concerning X…" Same grammatical base as でさ minus the さ — more neutral, less interpersonal Casual topic intro without the explicit "listen" signal N3
    〜でさあ "About X, you know…" Lengthened vowel — more reflective, hesitant, or emotionally loaded Thinking while introducing the topic; mild emotional weight N3
    あのさ "Hey / Listen" Pre-attention getter; frequently precedes 〜でさ Getting attention before the actual topic hook N3

    The closest lookalike is 〜のことなんだけど. Both introduce a topic and pause. The difference is pragmatic: でさ reaches toward the listener with さ; なんだけど explains or pre-justifies the topic introduction. リホのことなんだけど is "it's about Riho (and I'm telling you this is the topic)"; リホのことでさ is "about Riho, you know? — (I'm bringing this up with you)." The さ version feels slightly more interpersonal.

    Alternatives¶

    〜について (formal topic marker)¶

    The standard textbook "regarding." Appropriate for formal speech, writing, and presentations. In casual conversation, it sounds stiff and over-prepared.
    Example リホのことについて、ちょっとお聞ききしたいことがあります。
    English "Regarding Riho, there is something I would like to ask you."
    Intent Formal topic introduction — clean, impersonal, appropriate for workplace or official contexts
    vs. original について strips the interpersonal warmth entirely. Fine for a meeting; cold for lunch with a friend.

    When to use this instead

    Use について in any formal context — presentations, business emails, meetings with superiors. The warmth of でさ would be inappropriate there, so the formal option is not a downgrade but the correct register choice.

    〜のことなんだけど (explanatory softener)¶

    Swaps さ for んだけど — the topic introduction becomes explanatory rather than attention-reaching. Still casual, slightly less "listen to me."
    Example リホのことなんだけど、ちょっと聞ききたいことがあるんだ。
    English "It's about Riho — there's something I want to ask."
    Intent The speaker frames the topic introduction as an explanation: "the reason I'm speaking is Riho-related."
    vs. original なんだけど explains; でさ reaches. Both are natural. でさ feels a touch more like pulling someone aside for a private word.

    〜のことで (bare connective, no さ)¶

    The same structure minus the さ particle. Grammatically identical base, but colder — the topic is introduced as information, not as an interpersonal reach.
    Example リホのことで、ちょっと聞ききたいことがあるんだけど。
    English "About Riho — there's something I want to ask."
    Intent Neutral topic introduction. The で does the connective work; no さ particle adds interpersonal warmth.
    vs. original Subtle but audible to native speakers. With さ, the speaker is actively pulling the listener in. Without it, the topic is just being stated.

    Learner trap — formal でさ

    There is no formal version of でさ, because さ itself is inherently casual. Attempting でさ with です・ます produces a register clash — リホのことでさ、聞きたいことがあります is grammatically possible but pragmatically odd; native speakers would not combine さ with polite forms.

    Register and Formality¶

    でさ is fundamentally casual. It belongs to spoken conversation between people who share an in-group dynamic — friends, family, close colleagues. There is no direct formal equivalent that preserves the interpersonal warmth; formality trades warmth for distance.

    リホのことでさ、ちょっと聞ききたいことあるんだけど。

    "About Riho — there's something I want to ask you."

    Register: Natural casual speech. The input sentence. さ and んだけど mark this as close-friend or family register.

    リホのことなんですが、ちょっとお聞ききしたいことがありまして。

    "It's about Riho — there's something I'd like to ask you."

    Shift: でさ→なんですが (explanatory + polite); 聞きたい→お聞きしたい (humble); あるんだけど→ありまして (polite te-form, softer close). The warmth of さ is absent, replaced by polite distance. Appropriate for a colleague you're not close with.

    先日せんじつのリホの件けんにつきまして、少々しょうしょうお伺うかがいしたい件けんがございまして。

    "Regarding the matter of Riho from the other day, there is something I would like to inquire about."

    Shift: Complete formal restructure. でさ→につきまして (formal "regarding"); リホのこと→リホの件 (formal "matter"); 聞きたい→お伺いしたい (humble "inquire"); ある→ござる (formal existence). Used for official business contexts. No trace of the casual warmth.

    Grammar Lineage¶

    Step Pattern What It Adds Example
    N5 — Foundation だ (copula) States "X is Y" — the base the で comes from リホのことだ。"It's about Riho."
    N4 — Foundation で (te-form of だ) Connects "X is…" to what follows — creates the hook リホのことで、聞ききたいことがある。"It's about Riho, and there's something I want to ask."
    N4 — Foundation Sentence-final さ Casual assertion/reach particle — "you know" 大切たいせつなことさ。"It's an important thing, you know."
    N3 — Core ← You are here 〜でさ (topic-introducing) Merges the connective pause of で with the interpersonal reach of さ — creates a soft topic hook リホのことでさ、ちょっと聞ききたいことあるんだけど。
    N3 — Extended 〜でさあ Lengthened vowel — adds reflection, hesitation, or emotional weight 昨日きのうの件けんでさあ…"About yesterday's thing, you know…"
    N2 — Awareness 〜でありまして Formal equivalent — である + polite te-form; business-appropriate topic intro 先日せんじつの件けんでありまして…"Regarding the matter from the other day…"

    Examples¶

    Japanese English
    来月らいげつの旅行りょこうのことでさ、相談そうだんしたいことがあるんだ。 About next month's trip — there's something I want to go over with you.
    Literal: Next-month's trip's matter-regarding-you-know, consult want-to-do thing exists.
    ~Context: One friend bringing up trip planning with another. でさ flags the topic shift from whatever they were talking about before.~
    今日きょうの授業じゅぎょうでさ、先生せんせいが変へんなこと言いってたんだけど。 About today's class — the teacher said something weird.
    Literal: Today's class-regarding-you-know, teacher strange thing was-saying-but.
    ~Context: A student bringing up something odd that happened in class. The でさ hooks the class as shared context.~
    あのさ、昨日きのう話はなしてた映画えいがでさ、タイトル思おもい出だしたよ。 Hey, about that movie we were talking about yesterday — I remembered the title.
    Literal: Hey, yesterday was-talking-about movie-regarding-you-know, title remembered.
    ~Context: A follow-up to a previous conversation. あのさ gets attention; でさ hooks the shared topic.~
    最近さいきんの様子ようすでさ、何なにか変へんだと思わない？ About how things have been lately — don't you think something's off?
    Literal: Recent state-of-things-regarding-you-know, something strange is, don't-you-think?
    ~Context: Someone broaching a subtle concern about a shared situation. でさ softens the entry into potentially sensitive territory.~
    昨日きのう送おくったメールのことでさ、確認かくにんしてくれた？ About that email I sent yesterday — did you get a chance to check it?
    Literal: Yesterday sent email's matter-regarding-you-know, checked for-me?
    ~Context: Reminding a friend or close colleague about an email. でさ makes the reminder casual rather than nagging.~

    Common Mistakes¶

    1. Using でさ in formal situations¶

    さ is inherently casual. Using でさ with です・ます or in a formal setting creates a register clash that sounds childish or sloppy to native speakers.

    Register clash

    リホのことでさ、お聞ききしたいことがあります。

    ~さ is casual; お聞きしたい and ます are polite. The combination is pragmatically odd — the speaker sounds like they can't decide on the social distance.~

    More appropriate

    リホのことなんですが、お聞ききしたいことがありまして。

    ~Consistent polite register. なんですが replaces でさ cleanly; all elements match in formality.~

    2. Attaching でさ to verbs or adjectives¶

    で is the te-form of だ, which only follows nouns and nominal elements. Attaching it to a verb or i-adjective is ungrammatical.

    Incorrect attachment

    昨日きのう行いった[でさ]{.mistake}…

    ~でさ follows 行った (verb). The copula で cannot attach directly to a verb.~

    Correct

    昨日きのう行いった件けんでさ…

    ~件 (matter/case) nominalises the verb phrase — 行った件 is a noun phrase, so でさ attaches correctly.~

    3. Overusing さ¶

    でさ is a single, functional さ. Adding multiple さ particles or using it in every sentence sounds like a verbal tic — the equivalent of saying "like" after every phrase in English.

    さ overuse

    でさ、リホのことでさ、ちょっとさ、聞ききたいことがさ…

    ~Every pause gets a さ. Sounds scattered and juvenile. One でさ at the topic hook is enough.~

    Clean delivery

    あのさ、リホのことでさ、ちょっと聞ききたいことがあるんだけど。

    ~One あのさ to get attention; one でさ to hook the topic. Everything after is さ-free. Natural and controlled.~

    When NOT to Use It¶

    With people you don't know well

    でさ signals in-group intimacy. Using it with an acquaintance, a superior, or someone you've just met can feel presumptuous — as if you're treating them as closer than they are.

    In writing (except dialogue)

    でさ is a spoken discourse marker. In formal writing, use について or に関して. でさ appears in written dialogue (scripts, captions, casual messages) but never in essays, reports, or formal correspondence.

    Collocation Notes¶

    Collocation Meaning
    あのさ + 〜でさ "Hey, about X…" — double attention-getter; the first さ gets general attention, the second hooks the specific topic
    〜のことでさ "About the matter of X…" — the most common base; のこと adds soft indirection
    でさ… あるんだけど "About X… there's something I want to say/ask" — canonical topic-introduction + trailing request
    でさ… 思ったんだけど "About X… I was just thinking" — softens an opinion by framing it tentatively
    でさあ (lengthened) "About X, you know…" — adds thinking time or mild emotional weight; common when the topic is heavy
    Japanese English
    あのさ、昨日きのうの件けんでさ、ちょっと話はなしたいことがあるんだけど。 Hey, about what happened yesterday — there's something I want to talk about.
    The canonical multi-さ topic-introduction structure. Learn this whole sequence as a chunk.

    Hidden Points¶

    • でさ is doing two jobs at once. It's a topic marker (like は) and an interpersonal signal (like ね) simultaneously. The topic is marked for the conversation flow, and the listener is pulled in for the social dynamic. Neither function alone explains how でさ actually works in conversation.
    • The pause after でさ is negotiable — and that's the point. The speaker pauses; the listener can jump in, ask for clarification, or signal they're listening. The pause makes the topic introduction a small collaborative moment, not a monologue.
    • のこと is doing more softening than it looks like. When the topic is a person, リホのこと (matters concerning Riho) is less direct than リホのこと (Riho as a direct topic). The speaker approaches the person sideways, through their circumstances. This indirection is a recurring feature of Japanese interpersonal grammar.
    • でさ often pairs with んだけど at the other end. The topic hook opens with でさ; the statement closes with んだけど, trailing off to let the listener respond. 〜でさ…〜んだけど is a complete conversational frame worth internalising as a single pattern.
    • あのさ + でさ is the standard opening two-beat. The first さ (in あのさ) gets the listener's attention; the second さ (in でさ) hooks the topic. Breaking these apart or using only one changes the rhythm noticeably.

    Learner Stage¶

    Stage What It Looks Like
    Now — N3 Recognise でさ in speech and drama; understand its topic-introducing and softening function; parse it as で (te-form of だ) + さ (casual particle); produce it in controlled contexts like the input sentence
    Developing — N3→N2 Deploy it naturally when shifting topics or broaching sensitive subjects; feel the difference between でさ, なんだけど, and bare で; use あのさ + でさ as a natural opening sequence
    Automatic — N2+ The pattern arrives as a single discourse move — you don't assemble it, you just begin speaking and でさ appears where the topic needs a soft hook; you also know when it would be too casual and reach for について or なんですが without thinking
    Mastery You modulate the pause length and intonation to gauge listener receptivity; you feel the weight difference between でさ and でさあ; the pattern is fully integrated into your conversational rhythm
