    ～ちゃいなよ — Friendly Encouragement¶

    入はいっちゃいなよ — "Just Go Ahead and Get In"¶

    JLPT Level¶

    DeepSeek · DeepSeek-V3 · 2026-04-28
    N3 Core The input sentence uses ～ちゃいなよ, a casual contraction that bundles three grammatical pieces into one fluid, friendly nudge. At N3, learners move beyond recognizing individual contractions like ～ちゃう and start parsing stacked casual forms in real time.
    N4 Base
    Learner knows ～てしまう as "completely / accidentally / finish doing" and its casual contraction ～ちゃう. 全部ぜんぶ食たべちゃった。 "I ate it all." (completion / unintended result)
    N3 Core
    Learner recognizes ～ちゃいなよ as a warm, casual urging: ～ちゃう + ～なさい (contracted to ～な) + よ. The bundle means "just go ahead and do it — no hesitation needed." 先さきに入はいっちゃいなよ。 "Just go ahead and get in first."
    N3 Upper
    Learner understands the tonal range: ～ちゃいな can be lightly bossy, warmly permissive, or pragmatically efficient depending on relationship and intonation. They can distinguish this from softer alternatives like ～ちゃえば？. 早はやく着替きがえちゃいなよ。 "Just hurry up and get changed." (light impatience, still friendly)
    N2 Awareness
    Learner encounters regional and gendered variants of the contracted ～なさい → ～な form, including the ～なよ ending without ～ちゃう, and its use in Kansai-ben (～しぃな). はよ食たべぇな。 "Just eat already." (Kansai-flavored casual urging)

    Quick Summary¶

    - ～ちゃいなよ is a warm, casual way of saying "just go ahead and do it" or "why don't you just…" — it encourages action without pressure. - It bundles three elements: ～ちゃう (do it and be done with it) + ～なさい contracted to ～な (light command/urging) + よ (friendly emphasis). - The ～ちゃう part injects a sense of decisive completion: the speaker frames the action as something to get done with, which paradoxically makes the urging feel lighter and less demanding. - At N3, this pattern unlocks natural-sounding casual encouragement that sits between the stiff ～てください and the blunt ～て imperative.

    Core Meaning¶

    Mental anchor: ～ちゃいなよ = "just go ahead and do it already — it's fine, I'm telling you." The speaker removes hesitation by framing the action as simple, complete-able, and obviously the right move. The ～ちゃう component does the heavy lifting: it tells the listener the action is a single, clean step — no lingering, no overthinking. In the input sentence:
    お風呂ふろ準備じゅんびできてるから、もう先さきに入はいっちゃいなよ。
    Natural English: The bath's ready, so just go ahead and get in first.
    Literal: "The bath preparation is done, so — just go ahead and enter completely / get it done — I'm telling you."
    Context: A family member or close housemate has prepared the bath. The speaker is offering the listener first use — warm, casual, implying "don't wait for me, don't hesitate."
    A simpler example:
    遠慮えんりょしないで、食たべちゃいなよ。
    Natural English: Don't hold back — just eat up!
    Literal: "Without holding back, just go ahead and eat it completely — I'm telling you."
    Context: A host urging a guest who is hesitating to start eating. The warmth of ～ちゃいなよ pushes past the guest's politeness without being rude.

    How It Works¶

    The pattern works by reframing hesitation as unnecessary. The speaker isn't giving an order — they're signaling that the path forward is clear, the conditions are met, and the only thing standing in the way is the listener's own reluctance. The grammar does this through a layered effect: - ～ちゃう (from ～てしまう): Adds the nuance of "do it and be done with it." The action is presented as a single, complete-able step — something you can cross off the list. This is what makes the urging feel light: "it's not a big deal, just get it done." - ～な (contracted from ～なさい): A mild imperative. At full length (食べなさい) it's a parental "eat your vegetables." Contracted to ～な and bundled with ～ちゃう and よ, it softens into something closer to "go on, do it." - よ: The sentence-final particle that wraps the whole thing in friendly assurance — "I'm telling you, it's fine." ### The Role of もう In the input sentence, もう ("already / now / go ahead") is a frequent partner. It signals that conditions are already met — the bath is ready, there's no reason to wait. もう + ～ちゃいなよ is a highly natural collocation that pushes the listener past their hesitation point. ### Real Speech vs. Textbook Form The spoken form ～ちゃいなよ is a significant contraction from its textbook ancestor. Learners who only know the written forms may not recognize it in fast conversation.
    Textbook / written form 先さきに入はいってしまいなさい。 "Go ahead and get in first." (Stiff, parental, feels like an instruction to a child — rarely used in adult conversation.)
    Natural spoken form もう先さきに入はいっちゃいなよ。 "Just go ahead and get in first." (Warm, casual, natural between family, close friends, or relaxed housemates.)
    The contraction path is: 入ってしまう → 入っちゃう → 入っちゃいなさい → 入っちゃいな → 入っちゃいなよ. Each step strips formality and adds warmth.

    Grammar Analysis¶

    ### Function in Context In the input sentence, 入っちゃいなよ does several things simultaneously: - Signals readiness: The お風呂準備できてるから clause establishes that everything is in place. ～ちゃいなよ follows naturally: conditions are met, so action should follow without delay. - Removes the listener's social hesitation: In a shared living situation, there's often a polite dance about who uses the bath first. The speaker cuts through this by making it clear the offer is genuine and the listener should not defer. - Frames the action as trivial: The ～ちゃう component tells the listener: getting in the bath is not a production — you're not imposing, you're just completing a simple action. This is socially strategic. - Closes the negotiation: The よ at the end puts a full stop on the exchange. The speaker isn't opening a discussion; they're delivering a resolved position: the bath is ready, you're getting in, end of story — said warmly. Without ～ちゃいなよ — if the speaker said 先に入ってください — the sentence would lose all its casual warmth and sound like a formal instruction, which would be strange in this domestic context. If they said 先に入って, it would be plain and direct but miss the "just get it done, it's nothing" nuance that makes the urging feel light. ### Construction {#construction}

    Japanese Mindset¶

    The ～ちゃいなよ pattern reflects a distinctively Japanese way of being pushy and kind at the same time — a combination that can feel contradictory to English speakers. - Permission disguised as instruction: The grammar is structurally imperative (～なさい → ～な), but the communicative function is to release the listener from social obligation. In English, permission-giving ("go ahead," "feel free," "please, you first") uses soft, indirect language. In Japanese, the same function can be achieved with a light command — because the command is between equals in a close relationship, it reads as "I'm close enough to you to tell you what to do, and what I'm telling you is: stop being polite and just do the thing you want to do." - Completion as liberation: The ～ちゃう component encodes a cultural value: getting things done and moved past is desirable. By framing the action as "do it and be done with it," the speaker is saying: this isn't a burden, it's just a task — cross it off and relax. This contrasts with English encouragement, which often focuses on the positive outcome rather than the clean completion. - Warmth through grammar compression: The heavy contraction of ～てしまいなさい into ～ちゃいな is itself a signal of closeness. Over-enunciating the full form in a casual domestic setting would feel cold or stiff. The compression is what makes it affectionate — it's the linguistic equivalent of using someone's nickname. - Why "just" hides the grammar: English translations reach for "just" because there's no single word that bundles "completely," "go ahead," and "I'm telling you" together. The Japanese packs all three into ～ちゃいなよ without any of them being explicitly stated — the grammar itself does the work.

Use ～ちゃいなよ when you're confident the action is right for the listener; use ～ちゃえば？ when you're just putting it on the table.

    Register and Formality¶

    Casual
    お風呂ふろ準備じゅんびできてるから、もう先さきに入はいっちゃいなよ。
    Natural English: The bath's ready, so just go ahead and get in first.
    Shift: The original. Natural between family members, close housemates, or relaxed friends. Warm, slightly pushy in an affectionate way.
    Polite
    お風呂ふろの準備じゅんびができていますので、どうぞ先さきに入はいってください。
    Natural English: The bath is ready, so please go ahead and get in first.
    Shift: ～ちゃいなよ has no direct polite equivalent — the friendly-pushy nuance is inherently casual. The polite version defaults to a standard invitation with どうぞ and ～てください, which is warm and respectful but loses the "just get it done, stop hesitating" flavor.
    Formal
    浴室よくしつのご用意よういが整ととのいましたので、お先さきにどうぞ。
    Natural English: The bath has been prepared, so please go ahead.
    Shift: Formal register — appropriate for a ryokan or service context. ～ちゃいなよ is completely absent here; the urging function is replaced by polite deferential invitation. N2 — awareness only.
    Humble
    先さきに入はいらせていただいてもよろしいでしょうか。
    Natural English: Would it be alright if I went in first?
    Shift: The humble register doesn't use ～ちゃいなよ at all — it's the listener who might use humble language to ask for permission, not the speaker urging. Included here to show the social reversal. When the speaker is in the urging role, keigo doesn't apply; when keigo applies, the speaker is no longer urging.

    Pattern Progression¶

    ### What Leads Here - ～てしまう / ～ちゃう (N4): The foundational contraction. Learner knows that ～てしまう marks completion, unintended results, or "doing and being done with it," and that ～ちゃう is its casual spoken form: 食べてしまった → 食べちゃった. Without this base, the ～ちゃいなよ bundle is opaque. - ～なさい (N4): The polite imperative used from superior to inferior — parent to child, teacher to student. Learner knows that 食べなさい means "eat" as an instruction. This form is the source of the contracted ～な inside ～ちゃいな. - Sentence-final よ (N5): The particle that adds emphasis and shared-context signaling: "I'm telling you." Learner already uses this for friendly insistence and new-information delivery. In ～ちゃいなよ, it's the final layer that makes the whole bundle feel warm rather than barking. ### What This Unlocks - ～ちゃいな (without よ) (N3 Upper): Dropping the よ makes the urging slightly more brusque or efficient — still casual, but closer to "go on, do it" than "go on, do it — I'm telling you." The presence or absence of よ is a fine-tuning tool for warmth. - ～んじゃいなよ (N3 Upper): The voiced variant for む/ぶ/ぬ-ending godan verbs: 飲んじゃいなよ ("just drink up"), 遊んじゃいなよ ("just go play"). The same bundle, but the voiced contraction follows a different phonetic path and learners often hesitate on these forms. - ～ちゃって (N2 — awareness only): A softer, more open-ended variant: 先に入っちゃって ("go ahead and get in" — said as a gentle nudge rather than an urging). The ～て form trails off and feels less directive than ～ちゃいな. Natural in the same casual contexts but even less pushy.

    Examples¶

    暇ひまなら、先さきに映画えいが見みちゃいなよ。後あとで追おいつくから。
    Natural English: If you're free, just go ahead and start the movie. I'll catch up later.
    Literal: "If you're free, just go ahead and watch the movie and be done with it. I'll catch up after."
    Context: Friends planning to watch something together. One is ready now, the other is running late. The speaker releases the friend from waiting.
    気きになるんだったら、本人ほんにんに聞きいちゃいなよ。
    Natural English: If it's bothering you, just go ahead and ask them directly.
    Literal: "If you're concerned about it, just go ahead and ask the person directly and be done with it."
    Context: A friend is stewing over something someone else said or did, hesitating to confront the person. The speaker is pushing them past the hesitation — "rip the band-aid off."
    そんなに疲つかれてるなら、今日きょうはもう寝ねちゃいなよ。
    Natural English: If you're that tired, just go to bed already.
    Literal: "If you're that tired, just go ahead and sleep and be done with today."
    Context: A partner or housemate is visibly exhausted but still puttering around. The speaker is gently insisting they stop and rest.
    迷まよっててもしょうがないよ。好すきな方ほう選えらんじゃいなよ。
    Natural English: No point agonizing over it. Just pick the one you like and be done with it.
    Literal: "Agonizing gets you nowhere. Just go ahead and choose the one you like and be done with it."
    Context: A friend is stuck in indecision — shopping, ordering food, or making a minor life choice. The speaker is closing the deliberation with a friendly shove.

    Common Mistakes¶

    ### Mistake 1: Using the uncontracted form ～てしまいなさい in casual speech Learners who know ～てしまう and ～なさい but haven't internalized the contraction may produce the full form in casual contexts, which sounds stiff, parental, or even sarcastic.
    Unnatural / stiff 先さきに入はいってしまいなさいよ。
    "Go ahead and get in first." (Sounds like a parent instructing a child, or ironically formal — not natural between adults.)
    Natural casual 先さきに入はいっちゃいなよ。
    "Just go ahead and get in first." (Warm, natural, unmarked casual urging.)

    Mistake 2: Using ～ちゃいなよ with strangers or superiors¶

    The pattern is inherently casual and carries a light pushiness. Using it with someone you don't know well, or someone above you in status, can feel presumptuous or overly familiar — as if you're claiming a closeness you don't have.

    Inappropriate (with a superior) 先せん輩ぱい、もう帰かえっちゃいなよ。
    "Senpai, just go home already." (Overly familiar — sounds like you're treating your senpai like a peer or junior.)
    Appropriate (with a superior) 先せん輩ぱい、お先さきに帰かえられてはどうですか。
    <span class="pair-en">"Senpai, why don't you head home first?" (Respectful suggestion, appropriate social distance maintained.)</span>

    Don't Confuse With¶

    ～ちゃダメ (prohibition using ～ちゃ)

    Learners sometimes mishear or misread ～ちゃいなよ as ～ちゃダメ because both begin with ～ちゃ (the contracted ～ては). But ～ちゃいなよ is an urging (from ～てしまいなさい), while ～ちゃダメ is a prohibition (from ～てはいけない). The structural origin is completely different.

    Urging (～ちゃいなよ) 食たべちゃいなよ。 "Just eat up." (Encouragement: 食べてしまいなさい → 食べちゃいな)
    Prohibition (～ちゃダメ) 食たべちゃダメ。 "Don't eat that." (Prohibition: 食べてはいけない → 食べちゃいけない → 食べちゃダメ)

    Collocation Notes¶

    The ～ちゃいなよ pattern frequently pairs with certain signals that frame the action as timely, overdue, or the obvious next step. These combinations are natural enough to learn as chunks. - もう + ～ちゃいなよ: "Already" / "go ahead and… already." The most common collocation. もう signals that conditions are met and further delay is unnecessary. Together they mean "the time has come, just do it."
    準備じゅんびできたし、もう始はじめちゃいなよ。
    Natural English: Everything's ready, so just go ahead and start already.
    Literal: "Preparation is done, so already — just start and be done with it."
    - どうせ + ～ちゃいなよ: "Anyway" / "might as well just…" Used when the speaker frames the action as inevitable or when alternatives have been exhausted. Carries a slight "no point overthinking" edge.
    どうせ後あとでやるんだから、今いま終おわらせちゃいなよ。
    Natural English: You're going to do it later anyway, so you might as well just get it done now.
    Literal: "Since you'll do it later anyway, just go ahead and finish it now and be done with it."

    Hidden Points¶

    - The ～な in ～ちゃいな is not the prohibitive ～な: This is a critical structural point that learners can miss. The prohibitive ～な attaches to the dictionary form (食べるな = "don't eat"), while the ～な in ～ちゃいな is a contraction of ～なさい and attaches to the ～て form via ～ちゃう. The two な endings are etymologically unrelated but phonetically identical, which can cause a split-second misparse in fast speech. - Intonation distinguishes warm urging from impatient pushing: A flat or slightly descending ～ちゃいなよ sounds warm — "go on, it's fine." A sharply stressed ちゃいな with a clipped よ sounds impatient — "just do it already." The difference is entirely in delivery, not vocabulary. N3 learners should be aware that over-flattening their intonation can accidentally make them sound irritable. - The pattern can sound gendered in certain contexts: ～ちゃいなよ itself is relatively gender-neutral in modern casual speech, but the full uncontracted ～てしまいなさい can carry a maternal/stern-feminine association when used by older speakers. In younger speech, both men and women use ～ちゃいなよ freely, but some older male speakers may prefer ～ちゃえよ (using the imperative ～ちゃえ from ～ちゃう) as a rougher alternative. - The ～ちゃう layer also softens the command — paradoxically: At first glance, adding ～ちゃう to an imperative might seem like it would intensify it ("do it completely!"). But in practice, the opposite happens: ～ちゃう reframes the action as a single, clean step, which makes it feel less demanding than a raw ～なさい. 食べなさい is "eat (because I'm telling you to)." 食べちゃいなよ is "just eat up and be done with it — it's nothing." The completion nuance makes the action feel smaller, not larger.

    Learner Stage¶

    - What an N3 learner can reliably do with this pattern now: You can recognize ～ちゃいなよ when you hear it and correctly interpret it as friendly urging — you're not confusing it with the prohibitive ～な or mishearing it as ～ちゃダメ. You can produce it in familiar domestic contexts (with family, close friends, partner) where the social relationship is clearly casual. - What still requires conscious effort at this stage: Choosing between ～ちゃいなよ, ～ちゃえば？, and ～たら？ in real time based on how pushy you want to be and how close you are to the listener. Knowing when the よ is needed for warmth and when dropping it changes the tone from friendly to efficient. Comfortably producing the voiced variants (～んじゃいなよ) without hesitation. - What becomes natural later through N2+ immersion: Hearing the slight intonation shifts that tell you whether a ～ちゃいなよ is affectionately pushy or slightly annoyed. Using the pattern across a wider range of verbs and situations without pre-planning. Understanding when a native speaker uses the bare ～ちゃいな (without よ) and what that signals about the relationship in that moment.

    Reference Call Back¶

    Reference Call Back

    If you've studied ～てしまう／～ちゃう at N4, you learned it marks completion or unintended results. In ～ちゃいなよ, the completion meaning is doing pragmatic work: the speaker calls the action "something you can just complete and be done with," which is what makes the urging feel light and encouraging rather than heavy. The same ～ちゃう that expresses regret in 忘れちゃった ("I forgot — oops, it's done") expresses liberation in 入っちゃいなよ ("get in — just do it and it's done"). This is a common pattern in Japanese: the same grammar can encode opposite emotional tones depending on whether the "completion" is desired or undesired.
