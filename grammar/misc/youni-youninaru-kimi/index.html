    N4-Upper change-of-state potential youni

    Change of State — 〜ように・〜ようになる¶

    From inability to ability, from unknown to known¶

    JLPT Level¶

    JLPT Level — Upper N4 / Lower N3

    The base pattern 〜ようにする is N4 — "try to do" or "make it so that." But the extended usage 〜ようになる for change-over-time, especially the transformative shift from inability to ability, is a lower-N3 pragmatic layer that textbooks under-teach. Native speakers use ようになる constantly; learners often miss the "gradual emergence" nuance.

    Tier Pattern Description Example
    N5 — Foundation 〜できる Direct potential — innate or acquired ability 泳およげる。"I can swim."
    N4 — Foundation 〜ようにする Volitional effort — "try to make it so that" 毎日まいにち泳およぐようにする。"I'll try to swim every day."
    N4 — Core ← You are here 〜ようになる Change-over-time — gradual emergence of ability or state 泳およげるようになった。"I became able to swim."
    N3 — Extended 〜ようにしている Sustained effort — maintaining a practice 毎朝まいあさ走はしるようにしている。"I make it a point to run every morning."
    N3 — Extended 〜ようにしてくれる Request for accommodation — "please do it so that…" 静しずかにするようにしてくれる？
    N2 — Awareness 〜ようになっている Designed state — "is set up to…" 自動じどうで止とまるようになっている。

    Quick Summary¶

    At a glance

    • 〜ようになる describes a change-over-time — a shift from one state to another, especially from inability to ability, from unawareness to awareness, or from non-habit to habit.
    • Unlike 〜できる which states current ability flatly, 〜ようになる narrates the emergence of that ability. It tells a story: there was a time I couldn't, and now I can.
    • The よう nominalizes the preceding clause; the なる adds the change. Together: "It has come to be that [clause]."
    • This pattern is the grammar of growth narratives — how Japanese speakers talk about learning, maturing, adapting, and transforming.

    Core Meaning¶

    Core Rule

    Use 〜ようになる when you want to express that a state has changed over time — especially that someone has gained ability, awareness, or a new pattern. The change is typically gradual, earned, or observed over a period.

    泳およげる。 I can swim [current ability, flat statement].
    Literal: Swim-can.
    Context: Answering "Can you swim?" Neutral, present-tense capability.
    泳およげるようになった。 I've become able to swim [change over time, narrative of growth].
    Literal: Swim-can way became.
    Context: Telling someone about your progress. The ようになった adds the story: there was a before (couldn't swim) and an after (can swim now).
    日本語にほんごが話はなせるようになりたい。 I want to become able to speak Japanese [aspirational change].
    Literal: Japanese speak-can way become-want.
    Context: Expressing a goal. The ようになりたい frames the desire as a transformation, not just a skill acquisition.

    The Two Patterns: 〜ようにする vs. 〜ようになる¶

    These two patterns are often taught together but serve fundamentally different functions. Confusing them is one of the most persistent N4 errors.

    Pattern Structure Core Meaning Subject Example
    〜ようにする [plain/potential] + ようにする Volitional effort — "I will make it so that…" The person making effort 毎日まいにち勉強べんきょうするようにする。"I'll try to study every day."
    〜ようになる [plain/potential] + ようになる Observed change — "It has come to be that…" The situation or person changing 毎日まいにち勉強べんきょうするようになった。"I've come to study every day [it became a habit]."

    The agency distinction

    〜ようにする requires human agency — someone is trying to make something happen. 〜ようになる describes change that may or may not involve effort — the focus is on the result, not the will.

    早はやく起おきるようにしている。 I'm trying to wake up early [making an effort].
    The subject is actively working at this. It may or may not be happening yet.
    早はやく起おきるようになった。 I've come to wake up early [change observed].
    The change has happened. Whether through effort or naturally, the result is now real.

    How 〜ようになる Works¶

    The Nominalization Layer (よう)¶

    The よう in this pattern is a nominalizer — it turns the preceding clause into a noun-like concept. Unlike こと (which nominalizes actions as abstract entities) or の (which nominalizes more loosely), よう specifically nominalizes manner, way, or state.

    When attached to a potential form (泳およげるよう), it nominalizes the state of being able to swim. When attached to a plain verb (分わかるよう), it nominalizes the state of understanding.

    The Change Layer (なる)¶

    なる is the intransitive verb of change — "to become." It describes natural, observed, or gradual transformation. This is crucial: 〜ようになる is never sudden or forced. It is the grammar of emergence.

    分わかる。 I understand [right now].
    Static state. No narrative.
    分わかるようになった。 I've come to understand [over time].
    Narrative of emergence. The understanding developed gradually.

    What Precedes ように¶

    The clause before ように can be:

    Form Example Meaning
    Potential verb 泳およげるように "In a way that can swim" → "able to swim"
    Plain verb 分わかるように "In a way that understands" → "understanding"
    Negative verb 行いかないように "In a way that doesn't go" → "not going"
    Adjective + く 早はやくなるように "In a way that becomes early/fast"

    The potential form preference

    〜ようになる most commonly attaches to potential verbs (泳およげる, 話はなせる, 読よめる) because the pattern's core meaning is ability emerging. But it also attaches to plain verbs for habit formation (毎日まいにち走はしるようになった — "came to run every day").

    In Real Life¶

    Growth narrative

    Situation: ユイ is catching up with a friend she hasn't seen in a year. Last year, she was struggling with Japanese; now she's conversational.

    去年きょねんは全然ぜんぜん話はなせなかったけど、今いまは日常会話にちじょうかいわくらい話はなせるようになったよ。

    "Last year I couldn't speak at all, but now I've become able to handle daily conversation."

    Why ようになった fits here: The sentence explicitly contrasts past inability (全然ぜんぜん話はなせなかった) with present ability. The ようになった is the bridge — it narrates the transformation. Without it, 話はなせるよ would be a flat statement of current ability; with it, the ability is framed as earned growth.

    What the よ adds: The sentence-final よ marks this as shareable news — something the speaker is pleased to report. Combined with ようになった, it creates a small celebration of progress.

    Habit formation

    Situation: ケンタ is explaining to his doctor how his lifestyle has changed.

    医者いしゃさんに言いわれたから、野菜やさいを食たべるようになったんです。

    "Because the doctor told me to, I've come to eat vegetables."

    Why ようになった fits here: The vegetables are now part of Kenta's diet — a change from before. The ようになった captures that this wasn't always true. The んです adds explanatory grounding — he's answering an implied "Why?" or "How come?"

    Natural change

    Situation: ハルコ is talking about her aging father.

    父ちちは最近さいきん、夜よる中なかに目めが覚さめるようになった。

    "My father has recently come to wake up in the middle of the night."

    Why ようになった fits here: The waking is new, observed, and not deliberately caused. The ようになる pattern accommodates natural, even unwanted, changes — not just earned abilities.

    Grammar Analysis¶

    Function in Context¶

    • 去年きょねんは全然ぜんぜん話はなせなかった establishes the "before" state — complete inability. The けど (but) sets up the contrast.
    • 今いまは marks the "after" state — present time frame.
    • 日常にちじょう会話かいわくらい scopes the ability — "daily conversation level." The くらい softens the claim, making it modest.
    • 話はなせるようになった is the core transformation. 話はなせる (potential) + ように (nominalization) + なった (past change).
    • よ closes the sentence as shareable news. The speaker is not just stating ability; they're reporting growth.
    子供こどもの頃ころは苦手にがてだったけど、今いまは納豆なっとうが食たべられるようになった。 I used to be bad with it as a kid, but now I've become able to eat natto.
    Literal: Child time disliked-was but, now natto eat-can way became.
    Context: The classic Japanese "acquired taste" narrative. The ようになった marks the palate's maturation.

    Construction¶

    Preceding Form Structure Example
    Potential verb (できる) [potential] + ようになる 泳およげるようになる
    Plain verb [plain] + ようになる 分わかるようになる
    Negative plain [nai-form] + ようになる 行いかないようになる
    Adjective (i) [stem] + く + ようになる 強つよくなるようになる
    Adjective (na) [stem] + に + ようになる 静しずかになるようになる
    Noun + の [noun] + のようになる 大人おとなのようになる

    The なる vs. する distinction

    〜ようになる (intransitive) describes natural or observed change. 〜ようにする (transitive) describes deliberate effort. Do not confuse them.

    Intransitive (observed) Transitive (deliberate)
    早はやく起おきるようになった 早はやく起おきるようにした
    "I've come to wake up early" "I made an effort to wake up early"
    Change observed, result-focused Effort made, process-focused

    The Full Spectrum of よう¶

    The よう particle serves multiple functions across Japanese grammar. Understanding how 〜ように/〜ようになる fits into this spectrum prevents confusion.

    Pattern Function Example
    〜ようにする Volitional effort 毎日まいにち運動うんどうするようにする。"I'll try to exercise every day."
    〜ようにしている Sustained effort 毎日まいにち運動うんどうするようにしている。"I make it a point to exercise every day."
    〜ようになる Change over time 毎日まいにち運動うんどうするようになった。"I've come to exercise every day."
    〜ようにしてくれる Request for accommodation 静しずかにするようにしてくれる？"Can you try to be quiet?"
    〜ように言ゆう Indirect instruction 早はやく来こいように言いった。"I told him to come early."
    〜ように見みえる Appearance 分わかるように見みえる。"It looks like [you] understand."
    〜ようになっている Designed state 自動じどうで止とまるようになっている。"It's set up to stop automatically."

    Japanese Mindset¶

    English talks about ability with can/could — static, present-oriented. Japanese talks about ability with 〜ようになる — narrative, change-oriented. This reflects a broader cultural preference for process over state, emergence over possession.

    When a Japanese speaker says 泳およげるようになった, they are not just saying "I can swim." They are saying "There was a process, and now swimming is part of me." The grammar demands a story.

    • Growth as narrative. 〜ようになる requires a before and after. Even when unstated, the listener supplies it. The pattern activates a temporal frame automatically.
    • Effort as invisible. Unlike 〜ようにする which foregrounds effort, 〜ようになる backgrounds it. The change simply emerges. This is humbler — the speaker doesn't claim credit for their growth, they merely observe it.
    • Naturalness as virtue. Changes described with 〜ようになる often feel inevitable, organic, even welcomed. The pattern carries a subtle positive valence — things coming into their proper state.

    Nuance Comparison¶

    Grammar Point Core Meaning Emotional / Pragmatic Nuance Trigger Context JLPT
    〜ようになる Change over time — ability/state emerges Narrative of growth; natural emergence; humble observation Describing acquired abilities, habits, or natural changes N4
    〜ようにする Volitional effort — "try to make it so" Active willpower; self-discipline; resolution Setting intentions; making plans; self-improvement goals N4
    〜ようにしている Sustained effort — maintaining practice Ongoing discipline; established routine Describing current maintained habits N3
    〜できる Direct potential — current ability Flat statement; no narrative Answering ability questions; neutral capability N5
    〜られる (passive/potential) Potential (formal) or passive More formal than できる; can be ambiguous Written Japanese; formal speech N4
    〜始はじめる Begin to do Active start; deliberate initiation Starting new actions N4
    〜出だす Start to do (sudden) Sudden emergence; often emotional or physical Sudden changes in state N4
    〜てくる Change approaching speaker Gradual approach; "has come to be" Changes noticed over time, approaching present N4

    The closest lookalike is 〜始はじめる. Both describe change over time, but 〜始はじめる emphasizes the starting point ("I started swimming"), while 〜ようになる emphasizes the resulting state ("I've become able to swim"). 〜始はじめる is about action; 〜ようになる is about capacity.

    Alternatives¶

    〜できる (direct potential)¶

    The flat statement of ability. No narrative, no change.
    Example 日本語にほんごが話はなせる。
    English "I can speak Japanese."
    Intent Neutral capability statement
    vs. ようになる Removes the growth story. Appropriate when the "before" state is irrelevant or unknown.

    When to use this instead

    Use できる when you simply want to state current ability without framing it as achievement. Use ようになる when the journey matters.

    〜ようにする (deliberate effort)¶

    The active counterpart. Emphasizes willpower over result.
    Example 毎日まいにち日本語にほんごを話はなすようにする。
    English "I'll try to speak Japanese every day."
    Intent Setting an intention; making a plan
    vs. ようになる ようにする is about effort; ようになる is about result. They often appear in sequence: first you ようにする, then you ようになる.

    〜てくる (change approaching)¶

    Describes change noticed over time, approaching the present.
    Example 最近さいきん、日本語にほんごが話はなせてくる。
    English "Recently, I've been becoming able to speak Japanese."
    Intent Change approaching the speaker; ongoing emergence
    vs. ようになる 〜てくる emphasizes the approach toward the speaker (temporal proximity); 〜ようになる emphasizes the state change itself. Both can describe the same phenomenon with slightly different framing.

    Register and Formality¶

    The change-of-state meaning is preserved across registers, but the packaging shifts.

    去年きょねんは全然ぜんぜん話はなせなかったけど、今いまは日常会話にちじょうかいわくらい話はなせるようになったよ。

    "Last year I couldn't speak at all, but now I've become able to handle daily conversation."

    Shift: Input example. Casual, narrative, celebratory. The よ marks shareable news.

    去年きょねんは全然ぜんぜん話はなせませんでしたが、今いまは日常会話にちじょうかいわくらい話はなせるようになりました。

    "Last year I could not speak at all, but now I have become able to handle daily conversation."

    Shift: Past negative 話はなせなかった → 話はなせませんでした; なった → なりました. The narrative structure is identical; the politeness is elevated.

    昨年さくねんは全くまったく話はなすことができませんでしたが、現在げんざいは日常会話にちじょうかいわレベルまで話はなせるようになっております。

    "Last year I was entirely unable to speak, but at present I have come to be able to speak at a daily conversation level."

    Shift: 去年きょねん → 昨年さくねん; 全然ぜんぜん → 全まったく; 今いま → 現在げんざい; なった → なっております (humble progressive). The core pattern 〜ようになる is preserved even in ultra-formal register.

    Grammar Lineage¶

    Step Pattern What It Adds Example

    N5 — Foundation 〜なる Intransitive change — "become" 強つよくなる。"Become strong."
    N4 — Foundation 〜ようにする Volitional effort 毎日まいにち泳およぐようにする。"I'll try to swim every day."
    N4 — Core ← You are here 〜ようになる Observed change — ability/state emergence 泳およげるようになった。"I've become able to swim."
    N3 — Extended 〜ようにしている Sustained effort 毎日まいにち泳およぐようにしている。"I make it a point to swim every day."
    N3 — Extended 〜ようにしてくれる Request for accommodation 静しずかにするようにしてくれる？
    N2 — Awareness 〜ようになっている Designed state 自動じどうで止とまるようになっている。"It's set up to stop automatically."

    Examples¶

    子供こどもの頃ころは苦手にがてだったけど、今いまは納豆なっとうが食たべられるようになった。 I used to be bad with it as a kid, but now I've become able to eat natto.
    Literal: Child time disliked-was but, now natto eat-can way became.

    ~Context: The classic Japanese palate-development narrative. The ようになった marks maturation without claiming effort — the change simply happened.~

    毎日まいにち練習れんしゅうしたから、漢字かんじが読よめるようになった。 Because I practiced every day, I've become able to read kanji.
    Literal: Every day practiced because, kanji read-can way became.

    ~Context: Explicit effort → result. The から makes the causal chain explicit, but the ようになった still frames the result as gradual emergence.~

    父ちちは最近さいきん、夜よる中なかに目めが覚さめるようになった。 My father has recently come to wake up in the middle of the night.
    Literal: Father recently, night middle eye wake way became.

    ~Context: Natural, possibly unwanted change. The ようになる accommodates changes that aren't achievements — simply observed shifts.~

    日本にほんに来きてから、刺身さしみが食たべられるようになった。 Since coming to Japan, I've become able to eat sashimi.
    Literal: Japan came-after-from, sashimi eat-can way became.

    ~Context: Environmental influence. The change is attributed to context (being in Japan), not personal effort. The ようになる is neutral about cause.~

    無理むりやり早はやく起おきるようにしてたら、自然しぜんに早はやく起おきるようになった。 I forced myself to wake up early for a while, and then I naturally came to wake up early.
    Literal: Forcibly early wake-up way doing-was, naturally early wake-up way became.

    ~Context: The full arc — deliberate effort (ようにしてた) leading to natural change (ようになった). This is the grammar of habit formation.~

    大人おとなのようになったね。 You've become like an adult, haven't you.
    Literal: Adult way became, right?

    ~Context: Metaphorical change. The よう here is the "like/similar to" meaning rather than pure nominalization. The pattern overlaps with simile.~

    Common Mistakes¶

    1. Confusing 〜ようにする and 〜ようになる¶

    The most common N4 error. Learners produce 泳およぐようにした when they mean 泳およげるようになった, or vice versa.

    Wrong pattern

    毎日まいにち泳およいで、泳およげるようにした。

    "I swam every day and tried to make it so I could swim." — Bizarre. The effort and result are conflated.

    Correct

    毎日まいにち泳およぐようにしてたら、泳およげるようになった。

    "I tried to swim every day, and then I became able to swim." — Effort (ようにする) leads to result (ようになる).

    2. Using 〜ようになる for sudden changes¶

    Pragmatic mismatch

    事故じこで足あしが動うごかないようになった。

    "Due to an accident, my legs became unable to move." — Grammatically possible, but 動うごかなくなった is more natural for sudden traumatic change. 〜ようになる prefers gradual emergence.

    Better

    事故じこで足あしが動うごかなくなった。

    "My legs stopped moving due to an accident." — Direct state change, more appropriate for sudden events.

    3. Overusing 〜ようになる for current ability¶

    Narrative overkill

    私わたしは英語えいごが話はなせるようになっている。

    "I have become able to speak English [and remain so]." — Grammatically odd. 〜ようになっている is for designed states, not personal abilities. Use 話はなせる or 話はなせます.

    Correct

    私わたしは英語えいごが話はなせます。

    "I can speak English." — Simple, appropriate.

    Collocation Notes¶

    Collocation Meaning
    話はなせるようになる "Become able to speak" — language acquisition narrative
    食たべられるようになる "Become able to eat" — palate development, allergy recovery
    分わかるようになる "Come to understand" — comprehension emerging
    読よめるようになる "Become able to read" — literacy development
    聞きこえるようになる "Become able to hear" — hearing recovery, attention shift
    見みえるようになる "Become visible / become able to see" — clarity emerging
    起おきるようになる "Come to wake up [at a certain time]" — habit formation
    寝ねられるようになる "Become able to sleep" — insomnia recovery
    大人おとなのようになる "Become like an adult" — maturation (simile)
    毎日まいにち〜するようになる "Come to do ~ every day" — habit formation

    ~These collocations are worth learning as complete units. The pattern is highly productive for any ability or habit that develops over time.~

    Hidden Points¶

    • 〜ようになる can describe loss of ability. 泳およげなくなった ("became unable to swim") uses the negative potential + なる. The pattern accommodates decline as well as growth — though the valence is usually negative when describing loss.
    • The "before" state is often ellipsed. Japanese speakers frequently say 泳およげるようになった without explicitly stating 前まえは泳およげなかった. The pattern itself activates the "before" frame — listeners automatically supply it.
    • 〜ようになる + たい is aspirational grammar. なりたい ("want to become") + ようになる creates the goal-state construction: 話はなせるようになりたい. This is more humble and process-oriented than 話はなしたい ("want to speak").
    • Simile よう and nominalization よう are related but distinct. 大人おとなのようになる ("become like an adult") uses simile よう; 泳およげるようになる uses nominalization よう. The grammar looks identical but the semantics differ. Context disambiguates.
    • 〜ようになっている is a hidden N2 gem. It describes designed or expected states: 自動じどうで止とまるようになっている ("It's set up to stop automatically"). The ている adds "is currently in the state of being designed to…" This is common in technical descriptions and instructions.

    Learner Stage¶

    Stage What It Looks Like
    Now — N4 Produce 〜ようになる correctly with potential verbs; distinguish from 〜ようにする; narrate simple ability acquisition
    Developing — N4→N3 Deploy naturally in growth narratives; use with plain verbs for habit formation; recognize 〜ようにしている as sustained effort counterpart
    Automatic — N3→N2 The pattern becomes a single conceptual unit — "emergence." You hear ようになった and immediately register a before/after frame without conscious parsing.
    Mastery You control the full よう spectrum: ようにする (effort), ようになる (emergence), ようにしている (sustained practice), ようにしてくれる (request), ようになっている (designed state). You know when できる is flat and when ようになる is narrative. You also recognize simile よう vs. nominalization よう instantly.

    Same Family¶

    Commonly Confused With¶

    • 〜ようにする — Deliberate effort vs. observed change
    • 〜できる — Static potential vs. narrative emergence
    • 〜始はじめる — Active beginning vs. gradual emergence
    • 〜てくる — Change approaching speaker vs. state change
    • 〜になる — Simple becoming vs. manner/state becoming
