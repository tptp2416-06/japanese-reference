    Lowering Yourself to Elevate Others¶

    謙譲語 — Humble and Extra-Modest Speech¶

    Quick Summary¶

    At a glance

    • 謙譲語 (kenjōgo) is the Japanese humble language — it lowers the speaker (or the speaker's in-group) in order to show respect to the listener or to a third party.
    • There are two types of 謙譲語:
      • 謙譲語 I (Humble I): The speaker's action is directed toward the respected person. The lowering highlights the target's elevated status. e.g. ご説明せつめいする (I humbly explain to you).
      • 謙譲語 II (Humble II / Extra-Modest): The speaker lowers themselves as a manner of speaking, regardless of who the action targets. It shows formal politeness to the listener. e.g. 私が参まいります (I will go [formal]).
    • Some verbs have special humble forms — いる → おる, 行く・来る → 参る, 言う → 申す, する → 致す.
    • The お・ご + verb stem + する pattern is the standard way to create humble expressions from most verbs when the action involves the respected person.
    • The reference sentence from the 〜てある grammar page — そのことはもう社長にお話ししてあります — uses this pattern: お + 話し (stem of 話す) + する. It humbly describes the speaker's action toward the company president, while still claiming responsibility through 〜てある.

    Why "Extra Modest"? — The Two Layers of 謙譲語¶

    Many learners first encounter 謙譲語 as simply "humble" — the speaker lowers their own actions when those actions affect someone higher. But Japanese grammar divides this into two distinct categories. Understanding the difference is essential for sounding natural in formal and professional settings.

    謙譲語 I (Humble I) 謙譲語 II (Humble II / 丁重語)
    What it does Lowers the speaker's action toward a respected person. Lowers the speaker's existence/actions as a formal mannerism, showing politeness to the listener (regardless of the action's target).
    Who is respected? The person the action is directed at. The listener (the person you're speaking to).
    Does the action need a target? Yes — the action goes to someone, and that someone is respected. No — the action can be self-contained (going, being, saying).
    Example Verb 伺うかがう (to visit/humble → go to a superior) 参まいる (to go/come → formal self-reference)
    When to use Talking about doing something for/at/to a superior or customer. Formal speech, announcements, business letters, speeches — showing general modesty to the audience.

    The terms

    In Japanese, 謙譲語 I is sometimes called 謙譲語A, and 謙譲語 II is 謙譲語B or 丁重語 (teichōgo). The "extra modest" in the title of this page refers primarily to 謙譲語 II — the layer of formality that goes beyond direct action-target respect.

    Special Humble Verbs — The Core Set¶

    Some of the most frequently used verbs have fixed humble equivalents that you must memorise. The same verb often has different forms depending on whether you're using Humble I or Humble II.

    Humble I vs. Humble II — Side by Side¶

    Plain Verb Humble I (action toward respected person) Humble II / Extra-Modest (formal self-reference)
    いる (to be / to exist) — (rarely used in Humble I) おる
    行く (to go) 伺うかがう (visit a superior) 参まいる (go / come, formal)
    来る (to come) — 参まいる (come, formal)
    言う (to say) 申もうし上あげる (say to a superior) 申もうす (say, formal self-reference)
    する (to do) 致いたす (do for a superior, context-dependent) 致いたす (do, formal)
    見る (to see) 拝見はいけんする (look at / see, humble) —
    聞く (to ask / listen) 伺うかがう (ask / visit) 伺うかがう can also be formal self-reference
    食べる・飲む (to eat/drink) 頂いたく (receive / partake) 頂いたく (formal humble consumption)
    知っている (to know) 存ぞんじ上あげる (I am aware of [your situation]) 存ぞんじる (I know, formal)
    あげる (to give) 差さし上あげる (give to a superior) —
    もらう (to receive) 頂いたく (receive from a superior) —

    おる — the extra-modest いる

    おる is the Humble II equivalent of いる. It lowers the speaker's own existence in formal settings. You will hear it constantly in business Japanese, announcements, and customer service.

    いる おる
    私はここにいます。 私はここにおります。
    "I am here." (neutral/polite) "I am here." (humble/formal — the speaker is lowering themselves to the listener)

    おる is also used to humbly refer to one's own in-group members: 父は家におります ("My father is at home").

    The お・ご + Verb Stem + する Pattern¶

    For verbs that do not have a special humble form, the standard 謙譲語 I construction is:

    お／ご + [verb stem] + する

    This describes the speaker doing something for or directed toward a respected person. It lowers the action because it's being done in service of someone higher.

    Formation Rules¶

    Verb Type Prefix Pattern Example
    Native Japanese verb (和語) お お + stem + する 話はなす → お話しする
    Sino-Japanese verb (漢語, often する-verbs) ご ご + stem + する 説明せつめいする → ご説明する
    Exceptions / special verbs (use special form) — する → 致す, not ご〜する
    Plain Verb Humble (お/ご + stem + する) Meaning
    話はなす お話しする To speak (to a superior)
    伝つたえる お伝えする To convey / inform (to a superior)
    送おくる お送りする To send (to a superior)
    持もつ お持ちする To carry / hold (for a superior)
    説明せつめいする ご説明する To explain (to a superior)
    案内あんないする ご案内する To guide / show (to a superior)
    報告ほうこくする ご報告する To report (to a superior)
    連絡れんらくする ご連絡する To contact (a superior)

    The polite です・ます goes on する

    お話しする → お話しします (polite humble). You conjugate する as usual: お話しして (te-form), お話しした (past), お話ししてある (humble + prepared state).

    The Reference Sentence — Connecting to 〜てある¶

    そのことはもう社長にお話ししてあります。

    This is a perfect fusion of two grammar points: - お話しする = humble form of 話す (the speaker lowers themselves toward the president). - 〜てある = the prepared state construction (the action was done deliberately earlier, and the result — the president being informed — is now in place).

    Together, お話ししてあります means: "I have humbly spoken (to the president) and the matter is now taken care of." The speaker claims responsibility (〜てある) while showing proper deference (お〜する). This combination is extremely common in professional Japanese.

    When to Use 謙譲語 I vs. 謙譲語 II¶

    The choice depends on whether your action has a respected target, and who you are speaking to.

    Situation Use Example
    You are doing something for or to a superior/customer 謙譲語 I (special verb or お/ご + stem + する) 部長を駅までお送りします。 "I'll drive the department chief to the station."
    You are simply stating your own action in a formal setting (announcement, speech, business letter) — no specific superior as target 謙譲語 II (special verbs like おる, 参る, 申す, 致す) 私は田中と申します。 "I am Tanaka." (formal introduction)
    You are talking about your own in-group member to an outsider 謙譲語 II 父は今おりますが、後ほど折り返しお電話致します。 "My father is here now, but I'll have him call you back."
    You are writing a formal email to a client Both — 謙譲語 I for directed actions; 謙譲語 II for general self-modesty 資料をご送付致します。 "I will send you the documents." (送付 = sending, 致す = humble II)

    Humble vs. Respectful — Avoid the Confusion¶

    A common learner error is using humble forms to describe the superior's action, or respectful forms for oneself. The rule is absolute:

    謙譲語 (Humble) 尊敬語 (Respectful)
    Whose action? Speaker's own action (or in-group's) The respected person's action
    Effect Lowers the speaker Elevates the other person
    Example: いる 私がおります (I am here, humbly) 先生がいらっしゃいます (The teacher is here, respectfully)
    Example: 話す 私がお話しする (I speak, humbly) 先生がお話しになる (The teacher speaks, respectfully)
    Example: 見る 私が拝見する (I look, humbly) 先生がご覧になる (The teacher looks, respectfully)

    Deadly error

    先生せんせいがお話しします。

    ~お話しする is humble — it can only describe the speaker's own action. Using it for the teacher's action is grammatically wrong and socially bizarre — you've lowered the person you meant to elevate.~

    Correct

    先生せんせいがお話しになります。

    ~お話しになる is respectful — it elevates the teacher's action.~

    Extra-Modest in Action — Real-Life Scenarios¶

    Scenario 1: Answering the Phone¶

    はい、田中商事でございます。私、佐藤と申します。いつもお世話になっております。

    • ございます = extra-modest for です (there is / it is)
    • 申します = humble II for 言う (I am called)
    • お世話になっております = humble II of 世話になる (to be in someone's care); おる is the base

    Every element is 謙譲語 II — there's no specific superior being targeted; the speaker is lowering the entire self-presentation to show formal politeness to the caller.

    Scenario 2: In a Meeting with a Client¶

    資料しりょうをご覧ください。私からご説明申し上げます。

    • ご覧ください = respectful request (please look) — elevates the client's action
    • ご説明申し上げる = 尊敬語? No, it's humble: ご説明 (humble prefix) + 申し上げる (humble I of 言う — I will humbly explain to you). The action is directed at the client, so 謙譲語 I is used.

    Scenario 3: Talking About Your Own Whereabouts¶

    午後はオフィスにおりますので、いつでもお呼びください。

    • おります = humble II for いる. The speaker states their own location modestly.
    • お呼びください = respectful request — "please call me." Elevates the listener's action.

    The お・ご + Verb Stem + する Pattern — Full Conjugation¶

    Once you form the humble stem with お／ご, you treat the whole thing as a する-verb. This includes the 〜てある combination.

    Form Example (お話しする) Meaning
    Dictionary お話しする To humbly speak
    Polite お話しします I humbly speak
    Negative お話ししない I do not humbly speak
    Polite Negative お話ししません I do not humbly speak (polite)
    Past お話しした I humbly spoke
    Te-form お話しして (connective)
    〜てある お話ししてある／お話ししてあります I have humbly spoken (and the state is in place)
    〜ておく お話ししておく I will humbly speak in advance
    〜ている お話ししている I am humbly speaking (rare; but possible)

    Common Mistakes¶

    1. Using お・ご + stem + する for someone else's action¶

    Incorrect

    社長しゃちょうが私わたしにご説明する。

    ~ご説明する is humble — it can only describe the speaker's (or in-group's) action. The president cannot "humbly explain" to you using this form.~

    Correct

    社長しゃちょうが私わたしにご説明になる／説明してくださる。

    ~Use respectful forms (ご〜になる) or giving verbs (〜てくださる) for the president's action.~

    2. Mixing up お and ご¶

    Incorrect

    お説明せつめいする

    ~説明 is a Sino-Japanese word (kango). It takes ご, not お.~

    Correct

    ご説明せつめいする

    ~General rule: お for native Japanese words, ご for Sino-Japanese words. Exceptions exist (お電話, お料理), but the rule holds most of the time.~

    3. Overusing 謙譲語 II in everyday speech¶

    おります and 申します are formal. Using them with close friends or in casual settings sounds stiff or joking (unless you're being ironically formal). Reserve them for business, customer service, and formal occasions.

    Japanese Mindset — Self-Lowering as Social Glue¶

    The very existence of 謙譲語 II — a layer of language that lowers the speaker without any specific target for that lowering — reveals a deep Japanese cultural value: self-effacement as a default mode of formality. You don't just lower yourself when interacting with a superior; you lower yourself whenever you speak formally in public, as a way of acknowledging the formality of the situation itself.

    • おる is not just a humble いる — it is a posture. Saying 私はここにおります rather than 私はここにいます is the linguistic equivalent of bowing slightly as you enter a room. It signals awareness of the social context.
    • The お・ご + する pattern makes you small, but the grammar makes you clear. The humble form acknowledges the status difference, but the rest of the sentence — especially with constructions like 〜てある — lets you communicate facts and claim responsibility cleanly. Humility doesn't mean vagueness.
    • Humble and assertive can coexist. The reference sentence お話ししてあります demonstrates this perfectly: the humble form shows deference to the president, while 〜てある declares plainly that the task is done. Professional Japanese rewards this combination.

    Pattern Type Purpose Example

    参る Humble II "to go / come" — formal self-movement 私が参ります
    申す Humble II "to say / be called" — formal self-reference 田中と申します
    致す Humble II "to do" — formal self-action 私が致します
    お＋stem＋する Humble I Humble action toward a respected target お話しします
    ご＋stem＋する Humble I Humble action toward a respected target (Sino-Japanese verb) ご説明します
