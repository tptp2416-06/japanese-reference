    N3-Mid ppanashi state-continuation suffix

    State Continuation — 〜っぱなし¶

    テレビが付けっぱなしです。¶

    JLPT Level¶

    JLPT Level — Mid N3

    The core concept of 〜っぱなし is N4-accessible — a suffix indicating a state left unchanged. But the pragmatic layer — the mild reproach, the sense of negligence, the implication that someone should have fixed this — is solidly N3. The form also requires comfort with verb stem manipulation that sits at the N3 boundary.

    Tier Pattern Description Example
    N4 — Foundation 〜たまま "Left as is" — state continuation, neutral or descriptive 窓まどを開あけたまま寝ねた。"Slept with the window open."
    N4 — Foundation 〜ている Ongoing state — progressive or resultative 電気でんきがついている。"The light is on."
    N3 — Core ← You are here 〜っぱなし State left deliberately or negligently unchanged — carries mild reproach or awareness of incompletion テレビてれびが付つけっぱなしです。
    N3 — Extended 〜っぱなし + social reproach Using っぱなし to indirectly criticize someone's carelessness 靴くつを脱ぬぎっぱなしにしないで。"Don't leave your shoes kicked off [everywhere]."
    N2 — Awareness 〜っぱなしの + noun modification Embedded in noun phrases for compound description 開あけっぱなしの扉とびら。"A door left standing open."

    Quick Summary¶

    At a glance

    • 〜っぱなし attaches to the stem of a transitive verb to indicate that the resulting state has been left unchanged — typically through negligence, forgetfulness, or deliberate inaction.
    • Unlike 〜たまま (neutral description), 〜っぱなし carries a subtle emotional charge: someone should have returned things to normal, and didn't. The state feels suspended, waiting for resolution.
    • In the input sentence, 付けっぱなし describes a TV left on — not merely "the TV is on" (付いている), but "the TV was turned on and left that way." The implication: someone should have turned it off.
    • The form is common in household complaints, self-recrimination, and observations of carelessness. It turns a static state into a small narrative of human failing.

    Core Meaning¶

    Core Rule

    Use 〜っぱなし when you want to describe a state that resulted from a transitive action and was then left unchanged — with the implication that the responsible party failed to complete the natural follow-up action (turning off, closing, putting away, etc.).

    窓まどを開あけっぱなしにして出でかけた。 I went out leaving the window open [and shouldn't have].
    Literal: Window open-left-as-is doing went-out.
    Context: Self-recrimination or explanation to someone who noticed. The っぱなし marks the negligence; the して出かけた adds the compounding error of leaving.
    テレビてれびが付つけっぱなしです。 The TV has been left on [someone should have turned it off].
    Literal: TV turned-on-left-as-is is.
    Context: The input sentence. Entering a room and noticing the TV still running. The です softens the observation into a polite report, but the っぱなし carries the unspoken "again?" or "really?"

    In Real Life¶

    In Real Life

    Situation: ユキ comes home from work. Her roommate ハル was supposed to be home all day. Yuki enters the living room and finds the TV blaring, the lights on, and no one in the room. She texts Haru later that evening.

    テレビが付けっぱなしです。

    "The TV's been left on."

    Why 〜っぱなし fits here: Yuki could have said テレビがついています ("The TV is on") — a neutral observation. Or テレビを消さなかったの？ ("Didn't you turn off the TV?") — direct confrontation. 付けっぱなしです strikes a middle path: it reports the state while implying negligence, without accusing directly. The です makes it a polite observation; the っぱなし makes it a gentle reproach.

    What makes this natural: In shared living situations, っぱなし is the grammar of domestic friction — soft enough to avoid open conflict, clear enough to communicate that something is wrong. The implication is always "you (or someone) started this and didn't finish."

    The unspoken continuation: A native speaker hearing this immediately supplies the missing action: 消してほしかった ("I wish you'd turned it off"). The っぱなし leaves the resolution hanging, which is precisely its power — it describes an incomplete story.

    How It Works¶

    〜っぱなし is a suffix that attaches to the stem of transitive verbs (verbs that take a direct object and cause a change of state). It describes the resultant state of that verb being left as-is, typically because the agent failed to perform the natural restoring action.

    Layer 1 — Verb stem: The suffix attaches to the stem form (ren'yōkei), not the te-form or plain form. This creates a compound word that functions as a noun or adjectival noun.

    Layer 2 — っぱなし (the frozen state): The etymology is debated, but ぱなし likely derives from 放す (hanasu, "to release, to leave alone"). The sense is "released into a state and abandoned." The initial っ is a phonological glue that connects tightly to the stem.

    Layer 3 — Implied negligence: Unlike 〜たまま which simply describes a concurrent state, っぱなし implies a deviation from expected behavior. Something was activated or changed, and the responsible party walked away without restoring the default state.

    電気でんきをつけっぱなし The light left on
    The turning-on happened; the turning-off did not. Someone's action created an ongoing state that now needs correction.
    電気でんきがついている The light is on
    Neutral state description. No implication about how it got that way or who is responsible.

    Real Speech vs. Textbook Form¶

    Textbooks often present 〜っぱなし as "leaving something on/unfinished." In real speech, it's a social tool — the grammar of noticing negligence without naming the negligent party.

    Form Example Note
    Textbook (neutral) 窓まどを開あけっぱなしにする。 "To leave the window open" — grammatical but stripped of social context
    Real speech (reproach) また靴くつを脱ぬぎっぱなし？ "Shoes kicked off again?" — the っぱなし + また (again) compounds the criticism
    Real speech (self-recrimination) 冷蔵庫れいぞうこを開あけっぱなしにしちゃった。 "I left the fridge open [careless me]" — ちゃった adds regret to the negligence
    Real speech (polite report) テレビてれびが付つけっぱなしです。 The input sentence — polite observation with embedded criticism

    Grammar Analysis¶

    Function in Context¶

    • テレビが marks the TV as the grammatical subject — but semantically, the TV is the patient, not the agent. It was acted upon (付ける) and left in that acted-upon state.
    • 付けっぱなし is the compound: stem of 付ける (to turn on) + っぱなし. The verb 付ける is transitive (requires an agent), which is mandatory for っぱなし — intransitive states don't take this suffix.
    • です closes the sentence politely. In a more casual or frustrated context, this might be だよね or even just っぱなし with rising intonation: 付けっぱなし？
    • The absence of an explicit agent (who left it on) is functional — the speaker avoids direct accusation. In a household context, the responsible party is usually obvious from context, so naming them would be confrontational.
    • Without っぱなし: テレビがついています is a neutral observation. With っぱなし: the TV becomes evidence of someone's incomplete action — a small domestic crime scene.
    本ほんを広ひろげっぱなしで寝ねちゃった。 I fell asleep with the book spread open [face-down, pages strained, a bibliophile's nightmare].
    Literal: Book spread-open-left-as-is slept [regrettable].
    Context: Self-recrimination. The っぱなし marks the book as victim of the sleeper's negligence; ちゃった compounds the regret.

    Construction¶

    Verb Type Dictionary Form Stem + っぱなし Note
    Godan — く 書かく 書かき 書かきっぱなし "Left written" — unfinished writing
    Godan — ぐ 脱ぬぐ 脱ぬぎ 脱ぬぎっぱなし "Left kicked off" — shoes, clothes
    Godan — す 出だす 出だし 出だしっぱなし "Left out" — things not put away
    Godan — つ 立たつ 立たち 立たちっぱなし "Left standing" — standing idle, or left upright
    Godan — ぬ 死しぬ — — Intransitive — cannot take っぱなし
    Godan — ぶ 呼よぶ 呼よび 呼よびっぱなし "Left called" — phone off-hook, name unretracted
    Godan — む 読よむ 読よみ 読よみっぱなし "Left unread" — started but not finished
    Godan — る 帰かえる 帰かえり 帰かえりっぱなし "Left as returned" — doesn't occur naturally
    Ichidan 食たべる 食たべ 食たべっぱなし "Left eating" — started eating, never stopped
    Irregular — する する し しっぱなし "Left as done" — unfinished business
    Irregular — くる くる き きっぱなし "Left as came" — doesn't occur naturally

    Transitivity requirement

    〜っぱなし requires a transitive verb — one that takes a direct object and changes the state of something. Intransitive verbs (起きる, 死ぬ, 来る, 行く) cannot take っぱなし because there is no agent who "left" the state unresolved. Saying 起きっぱなし is ungrammatical.

    The stem form

    Learners sometimes try to attach っぱなし to the te-form or plain form. The correct attachment is the stem (ren'yōkei — the form used before ます). 付ける → 付けっぱなし, not 付けてっぱなし or 付くっぱなし.

    Noun Modification¶

    〜っぱなし can modify nouns directly, creating compound descriptions of neglected states.

    Pattern Example Meaning
    〜っぱなしの + Noun 開あけっぱなしの扉とびら "A door left standing open"
    〜っぱなしで + Verb 靴くつを脱ぬぎっぱなしで寝ねた "Slept with shoes kicked off"
    〜っぱなしだ (predicative) 窓まどが開あけっぱなしだ "The window is left open"

    Japanese Mindset¶

    Japanese domestic and social harmony depends heavily on restoring default states. Shoes are removed at the genkan and arranged. Lights are turned off when leaving rooms. Doors are closed. These are not merely practical actions — they are social signals that one respects shared space and doesn't burden others with cleanup.

    〜っぱなし is the grammar of failed restoration. It marks a state where the social contract of "leave things as you found them" has been broken. The reproach is rarely explicit because the grammar itself carries the accusation.

    • Default states as social obligation. In Japanese spatial culture, every object has a proper state. A TV is off when not watched. A door is closed when not passed through. っぱなし describes a state that violates this expectation — and the violation is social, not merely physical.
    • Indirect reproach as face-saving. By describing the state (付けっぱなし) rather than accusing the person (あなたが消さなかった), the speaker protects both parties' face. The TV becomes the subject; the human fault is implied but unspoken.
    • Self-recrimination through objectification. When used for oneself (開けっぱなしにしちゃった), the speaker treats their own negligence as an observable external fact. This creates emotional distance — the mistake becomes a situation to be fixed rather than a character flaw to be agonized over.

    Nuance Comparison¶

    Grammar Point Core Meaning Emotional / Pragmatic Nuance Trigger Context JLPT
    〜っぱなし State left unchanged after transitive action Mild reproach, negligence, incompleteness; agent failed to restore default Household observations; shared space complaints; self-recrimination N3
    〜たまま State left unchanged Neutral, descriptive; no implication of fault Concurrent states, accidental situations, literary description N4
    〜ている Ongoing state or progressive action Neutral; simply describes current condition General state descriptions, continuous actions N5
    〜ておく Do something in advance / leave prepared Proactive preparation; the opposite of negligence Preparing for future convenience N4
    〜ずにいる Continue not doing something Deliberate abstention; maintained inaction Choosing not to act over time N3
    〜たきり Did once and never since Abandoned habit or one-time action Something started and then dropped N2

    The closest lookalike is 〜たまま. The key difference is agency and reproach: たまま describes a state that happens to persist (窓が開いたまま — the window happened to stay open); っぱなし describes a state that someone caused and failed to resolve (窓を開けっぱなし — someone opened it and left it that way). The difference is the difference between weather and negligence.

    Alternatives¶

    〜たまま (neutral state continuation)¶

    The descriptive alternative. Same physical state, none of the social judgment.
    Example テレビてれびがついたままです。
    English "The TV is on [and has remained so]."
    Intent Neutral observation — might be describing a state you want to preserve, or simply noting a fact
    vs. original Removes the implication that someone should have turned it off. Safe when you don't want to assign blame or when the state is intentional.

    When to use this instead

    Use たまま when the ongoing state is neutral, accidental, or desired. Use っぱなし when the state resulted from someone's action and implies their failure to complete the natural follow-up.

    〜ている (simple state description)¶

    The stripped-down alternative. No narrative, no implication — just the fact.
    Example テレビてれびがついています。
    English "The TV is on."
    Intent Pure description — no backstory, no blame
    vs. original ついている doesn't tell you how it got that way or whether it should be different. It's the weather report version of the observation.

    〜ておく (deliberate preparation)¶

    The proactive opposite. Where っぱなし is negligence, ておく is foresight.
    Example テレビてれびはつけておいてください。
    English "Please leave the TV on [for a reason]."
    Intent Deliberate state maintenance — the opposite of negligence
    vs. original ておく justifies the ongoing state; っぱなし criticizes it. They are moral opposites.

    〜ずにいる (deliberate inaction)¶

    When someone chooses to maintain a state of not-doing.
    Example 何なにも言いわずにいる。
    English "Remaining without saying anything."
    Intent Sustained silence as a choice — not negligence but deliberate restraint
    vs. original ずにいる describes active maintenance of inaction; っぱなし describes passive failure to complete action.

    Register and Formality¶

    The reproach function — noting negligence without direct accusation — should be preserved across register shifts. The formality changes the packaging, not the social judgment.

    テレビてれび、付つけっぱなしじゃん。

    "The TV's totally been left on."

    Shift: Input sentence with casual bluntness. じゃん (じゃないか) adds emphatic observation — "Can you believe this?" The です is dropped; the reproach is foregrounded.

    テレビてれびが付つけっぱなしです。

    "The TV has been left on."

    Shift: Input sentence as-is. Polite observation with embedded criticism. The です maintains social distance while the っぱなし delivers the message.

    テレビてれびの電源でんげんが入はいったままになっております。

    "The television remains powered on."

    Shift: 付けっぱなし → 電源が入ったまま (formal periphrasis); です → なっております (humble progressive). The reproach is completely erased — this is now a neutral status report appropriate for hotel staff or service contexts. The っぱなし emotional charge is incompatible with formal register.

    Grammar Lineage¶

    Step Pattern What It Adds Example
    N5 — Foundation 〜ている Ongoing state or progressive action 電気でんきがついている。"The light is on."
    N4 — Foundation 〜たまま State left unchanged, neutral 窓まどを開あけたまま寝ねた。"Slept with the window open."
    N4 — Foundation Verb stem + 〜っぱなし The compound form itself 靴くつを脱ぬぎっぱなし。"Shoes kicked off and left."
    N3 — Core ← You are here 〜っぱなし + social reproach Negligence implication; indirect criticism of incomplete action テレビてれびが付つけっぱなしです。
    N3 — Extended 〜っぱなしの + noun Noun modification for compound description 開あけっぱなしの扉とびら。"A door left open."
    N2 — Awareness 〜っぱなしで + verb Embedded in longer clauses 本ほんを広ひろげっぱなしで寝ねた。"Fell asleep with the book open."

    Examples¶

    窓まどを開あけっぱなしにして出でかけた。 I went out leaving the window open [regrettable].
    Literal: Window open-left-as-is doing went-out.

    ~Context: Self-recrimination upon returning home to a cold room or discovering the oversight later. The にして adds the compounding error of leaving while in that state.~

    靴くつを脱ぬぎっぱなしにしないで。 Don't leave your shoes kicked off [everywhere].
    Literal: Shoes kick-off-left-as-is don't-do.

    ~Context: Parent or housemate to someone who repeatedly fails to arrange their shoes properly at the genkan. The っぱなし + negative command is a standard domestic reproach pattern.~

    冷蔵庫れいぞうこを開あけっぱなしにしちゃった。 I left the fridge open [careless me].
    Literal: Refrigerator open-left-as-is did [regrettable].

    ~Context: Self-recrimination upon discovering spoiled food or an overworked compressor. The ちゃった compounds the negligence with regret.~

    本ほんを広ひろげっぱなしで寝ねちゃった。 I fell asleep with the book spread open [pages now ruined].
    Literal: Book spread-open-left-as-is slept [regrettable].

    ~Context: The morning-after discovery. The っぱなし marks the book as victim; the で connects the state to the sleeping; ちゃった adds the regret.~

    電気でんきをつけっぱなしで一晩ひとばん中じゅう。 The light was left on all night long.
    Literal: Electricity turned-on-left-as-is whole-night.

    ~Context: Discovering the waste in the morning. The っぱなし marks the negligence; the 一晩中 compounds the duration and implied waste.~

    話はなしっぱなしで食たべるのを忘わすれた。 I talked and talked and forgot to eat [the food went cold].
    Literal: Talk-left-as-is eating forgot.

    ~Context: Social overindulgence. The っぱなし here describes an action (talking) that continued unchecked and displaced another necessary action (eating).~

    Common Mistakes¶

    1. Using 〜っぱなし with intransitive verbs¶

    The most common structural error. Learners apply っぱなし to verbs that don't take objects, producing ungrammatical forms.

    Incorrect

    電気でんきがつい[っぱなし]{.mistake}

    ~つく is intransitive — it describes a state that occurs, not an action performed by an agent. You cannot "leave" an intransitive state in the っぱなし sense.~

    Correct

    電気でんきをつけっぱなし

    ~つける is transitive — someone turned it on and left it that way. The agent's incomplete action is what っぱなし describes.~

    2. Attaching to wrong verb form¶

    Learners sometimes attach っぱなし to the te-form or dictionary form instead of the stem.

    Incorrect

    開あけてっぱなし

    ~Attaching to te-form is ungrammatical. The correct base is the stem.~

    Correct

    開あけっぱなし

    ~Stem form (ren'yōkei) + っぱなし. 開ける → 開け + っぱなし.~

    3. Overusing 〜っぱなし for neutral descriptions¶

    In contexts where no negligence is implied, っぱなし sounds judgmental or bizarre.

    Register mismatch

    山やまが見みえっぱなしです。

    "The mountain remains visible." — 見える is intransitive, and even if it were transitive, there's no negligence in a mountain being visible. Sounds like you're criticizing the mountain for existing.

    Appropriate usage

    写真しゃしんを広ひろげっぱなしです。

    "The photos are left spread out." — Someone spread them and didn't put them away. Negligence applies.

    Collocation Notes¶

    Collocation Meaning
    つけっぱなし "Left turned on" — lights, TV, appliances; the canonical household negligence
    開けっぱなし "Left open" — windows, doors, refrigerators, books, laptops
    脱ぎっぱなし "Left kicked off" — shoes at genkan, clothes on floor
    出しっぱなし "Left out" — things not put away, drawers not closed
    話しっぱなし "Left talking" — talked incessantly, neglected other duties
    広げっぱなし "Left spread open" — books, maps, newspapers; pages damaged
    しっぱなし (from する) "Left undone / left as is" — general unfinished business
    〜っぱなしにする "To leave [something] in an unchanged state" — the deliberate act of neglecting
    〜っぱなしで + verb "While leaving [something] as is, [do something else]" — compounding the negligence

    ~These collocations are worth learning as complete units. つけっぱなし and 開けっぱなし especially are daily-use household vocabulary.~

    Hidden Points¶

    • 〜っぱなし can describe chronic habits. いつも靴を脱ぎっぱなし ("always leaving shoes kicked off") uses っぱなし not for a single incident but for a repeated pattern. The suffix stretches from momentary negligence to character trait.
    • The absence of agent is functional, not accidental. In テレビが付けっぱなしです, the TV is the subject because naming the human would be confrontational. This grammatical sleight-of-hand is a core Japanese politeness strategy: make the object the subject, hide the human fault in the suffix.
    • 〜っぱなし implies a ghost action. The turning-off (or closing, or putting-away) that didn't happen haunts the sentence. Native speakers automatically supply this missing action when they hear っぱなし — the sentence is incomplete without this phantom verb.
    • Self-directed っぱなし is emotional management. Saying 開けっぱなしにしちゃった ("I left it open, careless me") packages the negligence as an external fact, which reduces self-blame. The grammar becomes a coping mechanism.
    • 〜っぱなし in formal register is nearly impossible. The emotional charge of negligence is too informal for keigo. Formal Japanese replaces it with neutral periphrasis (電源が入ったまま) or simply avoids describing the state altogether.

    Learner Stage¶

    Stage What It Looks Like
    Now — N3 Recognize 〜っぱなし in household contexts; understand the negligence implication; produce correctly with transitive verb stems; distinguish from 〜たまま and 〜ている
    Developing — N3→N2 Deploy っぱなし naturally in indirect reproach; use noun-modification form (開けっぱなしの扉); understand when formal register forces replacement with neutral alternatives
    Automatic — N2+ っぱなし registers immediately as "someone didn't finish what they started" — you hear the ghost action before conscious analysis; the reproach is felt, not decoded
    Mastery You control the social temperature precisely — using っぱなし for gentle domestic friction, たまま for neutral description, and knowing when to drop both for formal distance. You also recognize when someone uses っぱなし to criticize you without naming you.

    See Also¶

    Same Family¶

    • 〜たまま — State left unchanged; neutral, descriptive
    • 〜ている — Ongoing state or progressive action
    • 〜ておく — Deliberate preparation; proactive state maintenance

    Commonly Confused With¶

    • 〜ている — Simple state description; no implication of negligence or incomplete action
    • 〜てある — Deliberately maintained state; the opposite of negligence
    • 〜ずにいる — Deliberate maintained inaction; choice rather than failure
