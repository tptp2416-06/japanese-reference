    N4-Upper conjecture mitai resemblance

    Soft Observation — 〜みたい¶

    Seems like / Looks like / Apparently¶

    JLPT Level¶

    JLPT Level — Upper N4 / Lower N3

    The base meaning of 〜みたい as "resemblance" is N4. But the full pragmatic range — soft observation, hearsay packaging, gentle disagreement avoidance, and the gendered/casual texture that distinguishes it from 〜そう and 〜よう — is solidly lower N3. Native speakers choose みたい over its rivals constantly; learners often default to そう because it's taught first.

    Tier Pattern Description Example
    N5 — Foundation 〜そう (na-adj type) "Seems…" — immediate sensory impression 美味おいしそう。"Looks delicious."
    N4 — Foundation 〜よう（だ） "Seems like…" — reasoned inference, formal 雨あめが降ふるようだ。"It appears it will rain."
    N4 — Core ← You are here 〜みたい Resemblance + soft conjecture; casual, personal, hearsay-friendly 彼かれ、疲つかれてるみたい。"He seems tired."
    N3 — Extended 〜みたいだよね Soft observation + confirmation seeking 明日あした雨あめみたいだよね。
    N3 — Extended 〜みたいな + noun Resemblance modifier 夢ゆめみたいな話はなし。"A dream-like story."
    N2 — Awareness 〜みたいに / 〜みたく Adverbial resemblance 彼かれみたいに上手じょうずに話はなしたい。

    Quick Summary¶

    At a glance

    • 〜みたい expresses resemblance and soft conjecture — "seems like," "looks like," "apparently," "kind of like." It is the most casual and interpersonal of the Japanese conjecture markers.
    • Unlike 〜そう (immediate sensory impression) or 〜ようだ (formal reasoned inference), 〜みたい carries a personal, subjective, often hearsay-tinged quality. It says "This is my impression" rather than "This is what the evidence shows."
    • The form derives from 見みたい ("want to see") but has fully grammaticalized into a resemblance marker. The etymological ghost of "wanting to see" still lingers — みたい often introduces something the speaker has just noticed or is tentatively offering for consideration.
    • In hearsay contexts, 〜みたい packages reported information softly — "I heard something like…" — without committing to the source or the truth. This is gossip grammar, gentle correction grammar, and save-face grammar all in one.

    Core Meaning¶

    Core Rule

    Use 〜みたい when you want to express a personal impression, resemblance, or soft conjecture — especially when you want to avoid sounding certain, when relaying hearsay, or when making an observation feel gentle and shareable rather than analytical and definitive.

    彼かれ、疲つかれてるみたい。 He seems tired [that's my impression].
    Literal: He, tired [te-form] seems-like.
    Context: Soft observation. The speaker isn't diagnosing — they're offering an impression. The みたい makes it tentative and open to correction.
    明日あした雨あめみたいだよね。 Seems like it'll rain tomorrow, right?
    Literal: Tomorrow rain seems-like is, right?
    Context: Weather hearsay or personal prediction. The みたい softens the claim; the よね invites shared acknowledgment.
    夢ゆめみたい。 It's like a dream.
    Literal: Dream seems-like.
    Context: Pure resemblance. No conjecture — just comparison. The みたい here is closest to its etymological root: "like something I might see."

    The Three Conjecture Markers: そう・よう・みたい¶

    Japanese has three major conjecture markers, and choosing between them is one of the most nuanced decisions in casual speech. They are not interchangeable.

    Marker Core Meaning Source of Evidence Register Certainty Example
    〜そう Immediate sensory impression Direct visual/auditory/sensory evidence Neutral High (but superficial) 美味おいしそう。"Looks delicious [I can see it]."
    〜よう（だ） Reasoned inference Logical deduction, formal observation Formal High (analytical) 雨あめが降ふるようだ。"It appears it will rain [based on evidence]."
    〜みたい Personal impression / resemblance Intuition, hearsay, soft observation Casual Low (tentative) 雨あめみたいだね。"Seems like rain, huh [I heard / I feel]."

    The Critical Distinctions¶

    〜そう requires direct sensory access. You say 美味おいしそう while looking at food. You cannot say 明日あす雨あめが降ふりそう based on a weather report — that would require みたい or よう, because you haven't directly sensed tomorrow's rain.

    〜ようだ is analytical and formal. It says "The evidence points to X." It distances the speaker from the claim, making it objective. 雨あめが降ふるようだ is what a meteorologist says — or what you say when you want to sound like one.

    〜みたい is personal and soft. It says "I get the impression of X" or "Someone said something like X." It invites agreement without demanding it. It is the grammar of tentative sharing.

    彼かれ、怒おこってるみたい。 He seems mad [that's my impression / I heard].
    みたい: Personal, soft, possibly hearsay. You might not have seen him yourself.
    彼かれ、怒おこってるようだ。 He appears to be angry [based on observable evidence].
    ようだ: Analytical, formal. You've seen the evidence and drawn a conclusion.
    彼かれ、怒おこってるそうだ。 I heard he's angry [hearsay, formal].
    そうだ: Reported information, formal hearsay. Someone told you.

    How 〜みたい Works¶

    Layer 1: Resemblance (Noun + みたい)¶

    The original function: X is like Y. Pure comparison, no conjecture.

    夢ゆめみたいな場所ばしょ。 A dream-like place.
    Literal: Dream seems-like place.
    The みたい modifies the noun 場所ばしょ through な. Pure resemblance.
    彼かれみたいな人ひとになりたい。 I want to become a person like him.
    Literal: He seems-like person become-want.
    Resemblance as aspiration. The みたい creates the model.

    Layer 2: Soft Conjecture (Clause + みたい)¶

    The grammaticalized function: It seems that… Personal impression, tentative claim.

    彼かれ、知しらないみたい。 He doesn't seem to know [my impression].
    Literal: He, not-know seems-like.
    Soft observation. You're not certain — you're offering what you think.
    明日あした試験しけんがあるみたい。 Seems like there's a test tomorrow [I heard / I think].
    Literal: Tomorrow test exists seems-like.
    Could be hearsay, could be deduction from context. The みたい leaves the source vague.

    Layer 3: Hearsay Packaging (Reported + みたい)¶

    When relaying information from others, みたい softens the claim and distances the speaker from its truth.

    田中たなかさん、転職てんしょくするみたいよ。 Seems like Tanaka is changing jobs [I heard].
    Literal: Tanaka, job-change do seems-like [feminine softener].
    The よ marks this as shareable gossip. The みたい protects the speaker — "This is just what I heard."

    In Real Life¶

    Soft observation

    Situation: ユイ and ハル are at a café. They see their friend ケン across the room. He's staring at his phone, shoulders slumped.

    ケン、疲つかれてるみたいだね。

    "Ken seems tired, huh."

    Why みたい fits here: Yui isn't diagnosing Ken — she's offering an impression for shared consideration. 疲つかれてるそう would require direct sensory evidence she might not have (she's across the room). 疲つかれてるようだ would sound clinical and certain. みたい is exactly right: soft, personal, inviteable.

    What the だね adds: The だ grounds the observation; the ね invites Haru's agreement. Together they create a conspiratorial moment of shared noticing.

    Hearsay gossip

    Situation: マユ tells リナ something she heard at work.

    部長ぶちょう、来月らいげつ異動いどうみたいよ。

    "Seems like the department head is transferring next month [I heard]."

    Why みたい fits here: Mayu didn't see the transfer order. She heard a rumor. みたい packages the rumor as impression rather than fact, protecting both herself and the listener from misinformation liability. The よ marks it as juicy shareable news.

    What そうだ would change: 異動いどうするそうだ would be more formal hearsay — "It is said that…" This sounds like official reporting. みたい sounds like break-room gossip. The choice is social, not factual.

    Gentle correction avoidance

    Situation: シュン thinks the meeting is at 3. ミキ thinks it's at 2. Miki doesn't want to contradict directly.

    私わたし、2時じみたいな気きがするんだけど…

    "I kind of feel like it's 2 o'clock…"

    Why みたい fits here: Miki isn't saying "You're wrong." She's saying "I have an impression of 2 o'clock." The みたい + 気きがする double-softens the contradiction. The んだけど trails off, inviting Shun to correct himself without losing face.

    Grammar Analysis¶

    Attachment Rules¶

    Preceding Form Attachment Example
    Noun noun + みたい 夢ゆめみたい
    Na-adjective (stem) stem + みたい 静しずかみたい
    i-adjective (plain) adj + みたい 高たかいみたい
    Verb (plain) verb + みたい 行いくみたい
    Verb (past) past + みたい 行いったみたい
    Negative (plain) nai + みたい 行いかないみたい

    No だ before みたい with nouns/na-adjectives

    Unlike 〜ようだ which requires だ (静しずかだようだ), 〜みたい attaches directly: 静しずかみたい. Adding だ produces 静しずかだみたい, which is acceptable in very casual speech but non-standard.

    Standard Very casual (acceptable)
    静しずかみたい 静しずかだみたい
    学生がくせいみたい 学生がくせいだみたい

    Modifier Forms¶

    Function Form Example
    Predicate 〜みたい（だ） 疲つかれてるみたいだ。
    Noun modifier 〜みたいな + noun 夢ゆめみたいな話はなし。
    Adverbial 〜みたいに / 〜みたく 彼かれみたいに走はしる。

    The みたく contraction

    みたいに sometimes contracts to みたく in casual speech, especially before adjectives: 彼かれみたく上手うまくなりたい ("I want to become skilled like him"). This is informal but common.

    Japanese Mindset¶

    Japanese communication values tentativeness over certainty in social contexts. Direct claims risk contradiction, embarrassment, or social friction. 〜みたい is the grammar of productive uncertainty — it allows speakers to share observations, relay information, and even disagree without committing to truth.

    The form is deeply interpersonal. Unlike 〜ようだ which can be solitary (you observe, you conclude), 〜みたい almost always implies an audience — someone to share the impression with, someone to confirm or correct.

    • Impression as social gift. Offering a みたい observation ("He seems tired") is not just reporting — it's inviting the listener into your perceptual world. The listener can agree, disagree, or add their own impression. The conversation builds from there.
    • Hearsay as protection. Packaging information as みたい protects both speaker and source. If the rumor is false, the speaker can retreat to "I only said it seemed that way." This is not dishonesty — it is social grace.
    • Resemblance as aspiration. 〜みたいな is the grammar of role models and ideals. 彼かれみたいになりたい doesn't mean "I want to become him" — it means "I want to become the kind of person he represents." The resemblance is categorical, not literal.

    Nuance Comparison¶

    Grammar Point Core Meaning Evidence Source Register Certainty JLPT
    〜みたい Seems like / looks like Personal impression, hearsay, intuition Casual Low (tentative) N4
    〜そう (na-adj type) Looks / seems [sensory] Direct visual/auditory evidence Neutral High (superficial) N4
    〜よう（だ） Appears that / seems that Reasoned inference, formal observation Formal High (analytical) N4
    〜らしい Apparently / reportedly Hearsay, external report Neutral-formal Medium (reported) N3
    〜だろう / でしょう Probably Subjective probability Neutral Medium N4
    〜かもしれない Maybe Possibility Neutral Low (possibility) N4

    The hearsay spectrum: - 〜そうだ (formal hearsay): 転職てんしょくするそうだ — "It is said that…" [official, distanced] - 〜らしい: 転職てんしょくらしい — "Apparently…" [neutral, external source] - 〜みたい: 転職てんしょくみたい — "Seems like…" [casual, personal, gossipy]

    Alternatives¶

    〜そう (sensory impression)¶

    Direct visual evidence only. More certain but more superficial.
    Example 美味おいしそう。
    English "Looks delicious [I can see it]."
    Intent Immediate sensory judgment
    vs. みたい You must be experiencing the stimulus directly. You cannot say 明日あす雨あめが降ふりそう based on a forecast.

    〜よう（だ） (formal inference)¶

    Reasoned, analytical, distanced.
    Example 雨あめが降ふるようだ。
    English "It appears it will rain."
    Intent Objective observation based on evidence
    vs. みたい Removes personal impression. Sounds like a report, not a sharing.

    〜らしい (reported/hearsay)¶

    External source, more formal than みたい.
    Example 転職てんしょくするらしい。
    English "Apparently they're changing jobs."
    Intent Relaying information from an external source
    vs. みたい らしい implies you have a source; みたい leaves the source vague. らしい is more journalistic; みたい is more conversational.

    〜だろう / でしょう (probability)¶

    Subjective guess, often with rising intonation.
    Example 雨あめだろう？
    English "Probably rain, right?"
    Intent Probability judgment, often seeking confirmation
    vs. みたい だろう is more assertive; みたい is more impressionistic.

    Register and Formality¶

    〜みたい is fundamentally casual. Using it in formal contexts creates register clash.

    彼かれ、疲つかれてるみたいだね。

    "He seems tired, huh."

    Shift: Standard casual. Soft observation with confirmation seeking. The みたい is natural and expected.

    彼かれ、疲つかれているようですね。

    "He appears to be tired, doesn't he."

    Shift: みたい → ようです. The observation becomes more analytical and distanced. The ね remains for soft confirmation.

    彼かれは疲労ひろうを感かんじさせる様子ようすです。

    "He gives the impression of experiencing fatigue."

    Shift: Complete lexical replacement. みたい is impossible in this register. The observation is packaged as objective behavioral analysis.

    Step Pattern What It Adds Example

    Examples¶

    彼かれ、知しらないみたい。 He doesn't seem to know [my impression].
    Literal: He, not-know seems-like.

    ~Context: Offering a tentative read of someone's mental state. The みたい protects against being wrong — "This is just how it looks to me."~

    田中たなかさん、来月らいげつ結婚けっこんするみたいよ。 Seems like Tanaka is getting married next month [I heard].
    Literal: Tanaka, next-month marriage do seems-like [feminine softener].

    ~Context: Gossip packaging. The みたい + よ creates shareable, non-committal news. The speaker isn't vouching for the information — just passing along an impression.~

    夢ゆめみたいな場所ばしょだね。 It's a dream-like place, isn't it.
    Literal: Dream seems-like place, right?

    ~Context: Pure resemblance. No conjecture — just comparison. The みたい creates the poetic frame; the だね invites shared wonder.~

    私わたし、間違まちがってるみたい… I seem to be wrong…
    Literal: I, mistaken seems-like…

    ~Context: Self-correction, soft admission. The みたい makes the mistake feel discovered rather than confessed — "It appears I'm wrong" is gentler than "I was wrong."~

    昔むかしみたいに遊あそびたい。 I want to play like we used to.
    Literal: Old-times seems-like play-want.

    ~Context: Nostalgic resemblance. The みたいに creates the temporal comparison — not literally the past, but the quality of the past.~

    彼かれみたいになりたくない。 I don't want to become like him.
    Literal: He seems-like become-want-not.

    ~Context: Negative aspiration. The みたい defines the type of person — the category one rejects.~

    Common Mistakes¶

    1. Using みたい for direct sensory evidence¶

    Evidence mismatch

    このケーキけーき、美味おいしみたい。

    "This cake seems delicious [but I haven't tasted or seen it]." — If you're looking at the cake, 美味おいしそう is correct. みたい implies impression without direct evidence.

    Correct

    このケーキけーき、美味おいしそう。

    "This cake looks delicious." — Direct visual evidence.~

    2. Using みたい in formal writing¶

    Register crash

    本件ほんけんにつきまして、問題もんだいがあるみたいです。

    "Regarding this matter, there seems to be a problem." — In business writing, みたい is inappropriately casual. Use ようです or あるようです.

    Correct

    本件ほんけんにつきまして、問題もんだいがあるようです。

    "Regarding this matter, it appears there is a problem." — Formal, appropriate.~

    3. Adding だ before みたい with nouns¶

    Grammatically non-standard

    学生がくせいだみたい。

    "Seems like a student [with intrusive だ]." — Acceptable in very casual speech but non-standard. Most contexts prefer 学生がくせいみたい.

    Standard

    学生がくせいみたい。

    "Seems like a student." — Clean, standard casual.~

    Collocation Notes¶

    Collocation Meaning
    〜みたいだよね "Seems like…, right?" — soft observation + confirmation
    〜みたいな気きがする "I kind of feel like…" — double-softened impression
    〜みたいなもの "Something like…" — vague categorization
    〜みたいに見みえる "Looks like…" — visual resemblance
    〜みたいに聞きこえる "Sounds like…" — auditory resemblance
    〜みたいに思おもえる "Feels like…" — subjective impression
    夢ゆめみたい "Like a dream" — pure resemblance, wonder
    嘘うそみたい "Like a lie / unbelievable" — skeptical resemblance
    本当ほんとうみたい "Like it's real / seems real" — authenticity judgment

    ~These collocations are worth learning as complete pragmatic units. The みたいな気きがする pattern especially is a daily-use softener.~

    Hidden Points¶

    • 〜みたい is gender-neutral but intonation-coded. The same form can sound masculine (flat, dropped) or feminine (rising, extended) depending on delivery. The grammar itself carries no gender, but the prosody does.
    • The よね combination is socially powerful. みたいだよね transforms personal impression into collective assumption. It says "We both see this, don't we?" — creating shared reality through soft suggestion.
    • 〜みたい can be used for self-deprecating resemblance. 私わたくしみたいな人ひと ("a person like me") is often humble or self-critical. The form allows speakers to categorize themselves without claiming identity.
    • Hearsay みたい protects the source. Unlike 〜そうだ which often implies a specific informant, 〜みたい leaves the information trail muddy. This is useful for gossip, rumors, and sensitive information.
    • 〜みたい can soften corrections to near-invisibility. 違ちがうみたい ("Seems like that's different") is so soft it barely contradicts. The speaker can retreat completely if challenged — "I only said it seemed that way."
    • The etymological ghost of 見みたい ("want to see") still flickers. In some contexts, みたい carries a faint sense of "I want to see if…" or "It looks like something I'd want to see." This is not active meaning but residual flavor.

    Learner Stage¶

    Stage What It Looks Like
    Now — N4 Produce 〜みたい correctly with all verb/adjective types; distinguish from 〜そう and 〜ようだ in basic contexts; use for soft observation and resemblance
    Developing — N4→N3 Deploy みたい naturally in hearsay and gossip; use みたいな for noun modification; recognize when そう or ようだ would be more appropriate; control the だ insertion with nouns
    Automatic — N3→N2 The form arrives as a single social move — you hear みたい and immediately register "soft impression, possibly hearsay." The choice between みたい and らしい becomes intuitive.
    Mastery You control the full conjecture spectrum: そう (sensory), ようだ (analytical), みたい (personal), らしい (reported), だろう (probabilistic). You know when みたい protects face, when it shares gossip, when it creates poetry, and when it avoids confrontation. You also hear the ghost of 見みたい in nostalgic or wistful usage.
