    Topic Markers Compared — Which One Opens the Conversation?¶

    で・は・って・なら・について — When to Use Which¶

    JLPT Level¶

    JLPT Level — N4 to N3

    Individual topic markers are introduced at different levels, but the ability to choose between them based on nuance and context — rather than just grammar rules — is a mid-N3 conversational skill.

    Tier Pattern Primary Function
    N5 — Foundation は Topic marker: "As for X…" — establishes or shifts the discourse topic.
    N4 — Foundation で (copula te-form) Connective topic hook: "It is X, and…" — links a topic to what follows with a pause.
    N4 — Foundation について Formal topic marker: "Regarding X…" — written and formal spoken register.
    N3 — Core って Casual quotative-turned-topic-marker: "X, you say? / About X…" — frames topic as shared or hearsay.
    N3 — Extended なら Conditional-topic: "If it's X (you're talking about / we're dealing with)…" — picks up an existing topic.
    N3 — Extended でさ Casual topic hook + interpersonal reach: "About X, you know?…"

    Quick Summary¶

    At a glance — which marker when?

    You want to… Use Example
    State or shift the general discourse topic は リホは今日きょう休やすみだよ。"As for Riho — she's off today."
    Introduce a topic softly in casual speech, pausing before the main point で／でさ リホのことでさ、ちょっと話はながあるんだけど。"About Riho — there's something I want to talk about."
    Pick up a topic someone else just mentioned なら リホのことなら、もう聞きいたよ。"If it's about Riho — I already heard."
    Frame the topic as shared hearsay or common knowledge って リホって、最近さいきん忙いそがしいんだって？"Riho — I heard she's been busy lately?"
    Mark a topic formally or in writing について リホの件けんについて、ご報告ほうこくがあります。"I have a report regarding the matter of Riho."

    Core Comparison¶

    The Core Distinction

    Topic markers sit at different points on two axes: formality (casual ↔ formal) and discourse function (establishing a new topic ↔ referencing an existing one ↔ reaching toward the listener).

    The Two Axes¶

    Axis Question It Answers
    Formality What is the social distance between speaker and listener? Is this conversation, a meeting, or a written document?
    Discourse function Is this a brand-new topic? A known one? One the listener just mentioned? One the speaker is hesitating to bring up?

    Mapping the markers onto these axes:

    Marker Formality Discourse Function
    は Neutral (works in all registers) Topic establisher / shifter — clean and structural
    でさ Strongly casual Topic hook + interpersonal reach — "I'm bringing this up with you"
    で (bare) Casual to neutral Topic hook without the interpersonal reach — structural pause
    って Casual Shared-knowledge framer — "you know X? well…"
    なら Neutral Topic picker-upper — "if it's X (that you mention / we're dealing with)…"
    について Formal (written / formal spoken) Clean topic demarcation — no interpersonal signal

    When to Use Each — With Examples¶

    は — The Default Topic Marker¶

    Use は when you want to establish or shift the discourse topic cleanly and without emotional loading. It's the most neutral option and works in every register.

    Japanese English
    リホは今日きょう休やすみです。 "As for Riho — she's off today."
    Clean, factual. No emotional stance toward the topic or the listener.

    When to reach for は

    • Any time you're simply stating or shifting a topic without emotional overlay.
    • In any register — casual, polite, formal, written.
    • When you don't need to pause for effect or signal interpersonal warmth.

    で／でさ — The Casual Topic Hook¶

    Use で (or でさ, with added interpersonal reach) when you're introducing a topic in casual conversation and want to pause before delivering the main point. The pause lets the listener register the topic shift. さ adds "I'm telling you this" warmth.

    Japanese English
    リホのことでさ、ちょっと話はながあるんだけど。 "About Riho — there's something I want to talk about."
    リホのことで、ちょっと話はながあるんだけど。 "About Riho — there's something I want to talk about." (same, minus the さ warmth)

    When to reach for で／でさ

    • Casual conversation with friends, family, close colleagues.
    • When the topic is sensitive or important and you want to enter it softly.
    • でさ specifically: when you want the listener to know you're bringing this up with them.
    • Never in formal contexts — でさ in a meeting with superiors would sound childish.

    なら — The Topic Picker-Upper¶

    Use なら when the topic has already been mentioned — by you, by the listener, or by the shared context. なら means "if it's X (that you raise / we're discussing)…" It signals that you're responding to an existing topic, not introducing a new one.

    Japanese English
    A: リホのこと知しってる？ B: リホなら、昨日きのう会あったよ。 A: "Do you know about Riho?" B: "Riho? I saw her yesterday."
    B picks up A's topic with なら — "if it's Riho you're asking about…"

    When to reach for なら

    • When the listener has just mentioned the topic and you want to respond.
    • When you're returning to a topic from earlier in the conversation.
    • When the topic is already "in the air" and you're clarifying that's what you're addressing.
    • Not for introducing a brand-new topic — なら on a new topic sounds like you think the listener should already be thinking about it.

    って — The Shared-Knowledge Framer¶

    Use って when you want to frame a topic as something the speaker and listener share — hearsay, common knowledge, or a known entity. って carries a "you know the one / you've heard of it" flavor. It can also mark quoted speech, which bleeds into its topic-marking use.

    Japanese English
    リホって、最近さいきん彼氏かれしできたんだって？ "Riho — I heard she got a boyfriend recently?"
    って frames Riho as a known person; the topic is built on shared knowledge.

    When to reach for って

    • When the topic is a person or thing both speakers already know about.
    • When the statement involves hearsay, reputation, or common knowledge.
    • When you want to say "you know X? well, [something about X]."
    • Casual only — って is not used in formal speech or writing.

    について — The Formal Topic Marker¶

    Use について when you need to demarcate a topic cleanly and formally — in presentations, business emails, written reports, or polite conversation where emotional warmth is not appropriate.

    Japanese English
    リホの件けんについて、ご報告ほうこくいたします。 "I will report regarding the matter of Riho."
    Clean formal demarcation. No interpersonal signal — pure structure.

    When to reach for について

    • Formal speech: meetings, presentations, addressing superiors or clients.
    • Formal writing: emails, reports, essays, articles.
    • Whenever the casual warmth of でさ or the familiarity of って would be inappropriate.
    • Not for casual friend conversation — について between friends sounds stiff and rehearsed.

    Side-by-Side: Same Topic, Different Markers¶

    The same topic — リホのこと (matters concerning Riho) — introduced with each marker, showing how the nuance shifts:

    Marker Sentence Nuance
    は リホのことは後あとで話はなそう。 "As for the Riho thing — let's talk later." Clean topic demarcation. Neutral.
    で リホのことで、話はながあるんだけど。 "It's about Riho — I have something to tell you." Pauses before the payload. Casual.
    でさ リホのことでさ、話はながあるんだけど。 Same as above + "you know? I'm bringing this up with you." The さ adds interpersonal warmth.
    なら リホのことなら、もう解決かいけつしたよ。 "If it's about Riho — it's already resolved." Picks up an existing topic; responds to it.
    って リホのことって、誰だれから聞きいたの？ "About Riho — who'd you hear that from?" Frames the topic as shared hearsay.
    について リホの件けんについて、ご説明せつめいします。 "I will explain regarding the Riho matter." Formal, clean, no warmth.

    Decision Flowchart¶

    Quick decision guide

    Ask yourself these questions in order:

    1. Is this a formal situation? (meeting, email, superior)

      • Yes → について or は
      • No → go to 2

    2. Is the topic already in the conversation? (just mentioned, or obvious from context)

      • Yes, and I'm responding to it → なら
      • No, I'm introducing it → go to 3

    3. Do I want to pause and signal warmth?

      • Yes, I want a soft entry with interpersonal reach → でさ
      • Yes, I want a soft entry without the extra warmth → で
      • No, I want a clean structural topic → は

    4. Bonus: Is the topic shared hearsay or common knowledge?

      • Yes, and I'm in casual conversation → って (can combine with other choices)

    Common Mistakes¶

    1. Using でさ in formal contexts¶

    Register clash

    会議かいぎで、リホの件けんでさ…

    ~さ is casual. In a meeting, this sounds sloppy and immature.~

    Correct

    会議かいぎで、リホの件けんについて…

    ~について is the appropriate formal choice.~

    2. Using なら to introduce a brand-new topic¶

    Presumptuous topic introduction

    （突然とつぜん）リホのことなら、知しってる？

    ~なら assumes the listener is already thinking about Riho. Starting a conversation this way is confusing.~

    Correct

    あのさ、リホのことでさ、ちょっと聞ききたいことがあるんだけど。

    ~でさ introduces the topic as new — the listener is invited into it, not assumed to be already there.~

    3. Using って with a topic the listener doesn't know¶

    Mismatched assumption

    山田やまださんって、誰だれ？

    ~って assumes shared knowledge. If the listener doesn't know Yamada, the sentence contradicts itself.~

    Correct

    山田やまださんって人ひと、知しってる？

    ~Adding 人 makes it "this person called Yamada" — the shared-knowledge frame now works because the topic is "the existence of a person called Yamada," which both parties can acknowledge.~

    Collocation Notes¶

    Common opening patterns that combine these markers:

    Pattern Function Example
    あのさ + でさ Attention + topic hook あのさ、リホのことでさ…
    [topic] って + [hearsay] んだって Shared-knowledge topic + hearsay リホって結婚けっこんするんだって。
    [topic] + は + [statement] Clean topic + statement (any register) リホは今日きょう休やすみです。
    [topic] + なら + [response] Picking up a raised topic リホのことなら任まかせて。
    [topic] + について + [formal statement] Formal topic demarcation 本件ほんけんについてご説明せつめいいたします。

    Hidden Points¶

    • は is not "subject" — it's "topic." The most fundamental thing to internalise. が marks the grammatical subject; は marks what the sentence is about. Riho wa… means "the discourse topic is now Riho." Everything that follows is understood within that frame until the topic shifts or ends.
    • で is a topic hook, not a topic marker. Unlike は, which cleanly demarcates a topic, で functions as a grammatical connective — it pauses the sentence mid-flow, creating anticipation. You can't end a sentence on で as a topic marker; you can end one on は (though it would be incomplete).
    • なら has an if-then structure baked in. Even when used as a topic picker-upper, the conditional logic is still active — なら means "if it's the case that we're dealing with X, then Y." This is why it only works with existing topics.
    • って is the most versatile casual topic framer. It can replace は, mark hearsay, or signal shared knowledge. Learners often underuse it because textbooks treat it primarily as a quotative particle. In real casual speech, its topic-marking function is equally important.
    • でさ is not taught in most textbooks. It sits in the gap between grammar (で as copula te-form) and discourse (さ as a casual particle). Learners typically acquire it through exposure, not study — but understanding its function accelerates that process.

    Learner Stage¶

    Stage What It Looks Like
    Now — N4/N3 Recognise all markers in speech and text. Use は confidently and について in formal contexts. Understand でさ and って when heard. Begin experimenting with で and なら.
    Developing — N3→N2 Choose between で, でさ, and は based on formality and warmth. Use なら naturally when responding to existing topics. Deploy って for shared-knowledge topics without conscious thought.
    Automatic — N2+ The choice between markers is automatic — you feel the register and discourse function simultaneously and select the right one without analysis.
    Mastery You modulate the pause length with で and でさ to gauge listener receptivity in real time. You shift between について and でさ mid-conversation as the social distance changes.
