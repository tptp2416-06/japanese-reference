# 〜て＋Auxiliary — The Complete Spectrum

## てくれてありがとう・てよかった・てほしい・etc.

---

## JLPT Level {#jlpt-level}

!!! abstract "JLPT Level — N5 Foundation through N3 Extended"
    The te-form itself is N5. The auxiliary verbs attached to it create a staircase of interpersonal, emotional, and directional meaning that dominates N4 and N3. Mastering the te-form auxiliaries means mastering how Japanese speakers talk about ongoing action, social obligation, emotional relief, and change over time.

    | Tier | Pattern | Description | Example |
    |------|---------|-------------|---------|
    | N5 — Foundation | ている / てください / てもいい / てはいけない | Progressive, request, permission, prohibition | <ruby>食<rt>た</rt></ruby>べている。<ruby>座<rt>すわ</rt></ruby>ってください。 |
    | N5 — Foundation | てくる・ていく / てみる / ておく / てしまう | Directional, attemptive, preparation, completion | <ruby>買<rt>か</rt></ruby>ってくる。やってみる。 |
    | **N4 — Core ← You are here** | **てあげる・てもらう・てくれる** | **Giving and receiving of actions** | <ruby>手伝<rt>てつだ</rt></ruby>ってくれた。 |
    | N4 — Core | てほしい / てもらえませんか | Desire directed outward; humble request | <ruby>来<rt>き</rt></ruby>てほしい。<ruby>教<rt>おし</rt></ruby>えてもらえませんか。 |
    | N4 — Core | てくれてありがとう / てすみません / てよかった | Gratitude, apology, emotional evaluation | <ruby>待<rt>ま</rt></ruby>ってくれてありがとう。 |
    | N4 — Core | てある | Resultant state (deliberate) | <ruby>準備<rt>じゅんび</rt></ruby>がしてある。 |
    | N3 — Extended | てくる・ていく (grammaticalized) | Change over time / continuation into future | <ruby>慣<rt>な</rt></ruby>れてきた。<ruby>頑張<rt>がんば</rt></ruby>っていく。 |
    | N3 — Extended | てほしくない / てもらってもいいですか | Negative desire; soft permission request | <ruby>来<rt>き</rt></ruby>てほしくない。 |
    | N3 — Extended | てみせる / てばかりいる / てくれ | Demonstrative; habitual negative; casual imperative | やってみせる。<ruby>遊<rt>あそ</rt></ruby>んでばかりいる。 |

## Quick Summary {#quick-summary}

!!! tip "At a glance"
    - The te-form is Japanese grammatical glue. By attaching auxiliary verbs to it, you create layered meanings: progressive time (ている), social direction (てあげる/てくれる), emotional weight (てよかった/てしまう), preparation (ておく), and change over time (てくる/ていく).
    - N5 patterns describe basic action states: what is happening, what you may do, what you must not do, trying, preparing, finishing.
    - N4 patterns introduce the interpersonal layer: who benefits, who receives, who gives, what you want from others, and how you feel about what happened.
    - N3 patterns add temporal depth and emotional shading: things that start to happen, things that continue, things you show others, habits you criticize, and requests stripped to casual directness.
    - The te-form auxiliary system is not a list to memorize — it is a **social and temporal coordinate system**. Every choice places the action in a specific relationship to time, people, and feeling.

## N5 — Foundation: Action States {#n5-foundation}

### 1. ている — Progressive & Resultant State

Ongoing action or a state that remains after a change.

| <ruby>食<rt>た</rt></ruby>べている。 | I am eating [progressive]. |
|---|---|
| ドアが<ruby>開<rt>あ</rt></ruby>いている。 | The door is open [resultant state]. |
| <ruby>知<rt>し</rt></ruby>っている。 | I know [cognitive state]. |

!!! note "Two faces of ている"
    With action verbs, ている means "in the middle of doing." With change-of-state verbs, it means "has become and remains."

### 2. てください — Polite Request

Standard polite way to ask someone to do something.

| <ruby>座<rt>すわ</rt></ruby>ってください。 | Please sit. |
|---|---|
| <ruby>見<rt>み</rt></ruby>てください。 | Please look. |
| <ruby>静<rt>しず</rt></ruby>かにしてください。 | Please be quiet. |

### 3. てもいい — Permission

Grants permission or asks for it. Often paired with か for questions.

| <ruby>入<rt>はい</rt></ruby>ってもいいですか。 | May I come in? |
|---|---|
| <ruby>写真<rt>しゃしん</rt></ruby>を<ruby>撮<rt>と</rt></ruby>ってもいい。 | You may take photos. |
| ここに<ruby>座<rt>すわ</rt></ruby>ってもいい？ | Is it okay if I sit here? |

### 4. てはいけない — Prohibition

States what must not be done. Formal variant: てはいけません.

| <ruby>触<rt>さわ</rt></ruby>ってはいけない。 | Do not touch. |
|---|---|
| <ruby>入<rt>はい</rt></ruby>ってはいけません。 | You must not enter. |
| <ruby>嘘<rt>うそ</rt></ruby>をついてはいけない。 | You must not lie. |

### 5. てくる・ていく — Directional

Physical movement toward or away from the speaker.

| <ruby>買<rt>か</rt></ruby>い<ruby>物<rt>もの</rt></ruby>に<ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>ってくる。 | I'm going shopping [and will return]. |
|---|---|
| <ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>っていく。 | I'll take this [away with me]. |
| <ruby>連<rt>つ</rt></ruby>れてくる。 | I'll bring [someone] here. |

!!! note "N5: physical direction only"
    At N5, てくる and ていく describe literal movement. The grammaticalized "change over time" meanings are N3.

### 6. てみる — Attemptive

Trying something to see what happens.

| <ruby>食<rt>た</rt></ruby>べてみる。 | I'll try eating it. |
|---|---|
| <ruby>考<rt>かんが</rt></ruby>えてみる。 | I'll think about it. |
| やってみよう。 | Let's give it a try. |

### 7. ておく — Preparation

Doing something in advance for future convenience.

| <ruby>予約<rt>よやく</rt></ruby>しておく。 | I'll make a reservation [in advance]. |
|---|---|
| <ruby>準備<rt>じゅんび</rt></ruby>しておく。 | I'll prepare [ahead of time]. |
| <ruby>言<rt>い</rt></ruby>っておく。 | I'll mention it [so it's handled]. |

### 8. てしまう — Completion

Finishing an action, often with regret or accidental nuance.

| <ruby>食<rt>た</rt></ruby>べてしまった。 | I ate it all [and now it's gone / I regret it]. |
|---|---|
| <ruby>忘<rt>わす</rt></ruby>れてしまった。 | I completely forgot. |
| <ruby>壊<rt>こわ</rt></ruby>してしまった。 | I broke it [accidentally]. |

## N4 — Core: Interpersonal & Emotional {#n4-core}

### 9. てあげる — Outward Giving

Doing something for someone else. The speaker is the giver.

| <ruby>手伝<rt>てつだ</rt></ruby>ってあげる。 | I'll help you [generous, slightly superior]. |
|---|---|
| <ruby>教<rt>おし</rt></ruby>えてあげた。 | I taught [them]. |
| <ruby>買<rt>か</rt></ruby>ってあげよう。 | I'll buy it [for them]. |

!!! warning "てあげる can sound patronizing"
    It implies the speaker is doing a favor from a position of ability or generosity. Use carefully with superiors.

### 10. てもらう — Inward Receiving

Having someone do something for the speaker. The speaker benefits.

| <ruby>先生<rt>せんせい</rt></ruby>に<ruby>教<rt>おし</rt></ruby>えてもらった。 | I was taught by my teacher [received the favor]. |
|---|---|
| <ruby>友達<rt>ともだち</rt></ruby>に<ruby>送<rt>おく</rt></ruby>ってもらう。 | I'll have my friend send it. |
| <ruby>母<rt>はは</rt></ruby>に<ruby>作<rt>つく</rt></ruby>ってもらった。 | I had my mother make it. |

### 11. てくれる — Inward Giving

Someone does something for the speaker (or speaker's group). Unsolicited benefit.

| <ruby>友達<rt>ともだち</rt></ruby>が<ruby>手伝<rt>てつだ</rt></ruby>ってくれた。 | A friend helped me [I'm grateful]. |
|---|---|
| <ruby>彼<rt>かれ</rt></ruby>が<ruby>教<rt>おし</rt></ruby>えてくれた。 | He taught me [I benefited]. |
| <ruby>誰<rt>だれ</rt></ruby>かが<ruby>拾<rt>ひろ</rt></ruby>ってくれた。 | Someone picked it up for me. |

!!! note "The gratitude foundation"
    てくれる is the emotional engine of Japanese social grammar. It encodes that the action came toward the speaker as a gift.

### 12. てほしい — Directed Desire

Wanting someone to do something. Desire aimed at another person's action.

| <ruby>早<rt>はや</rt></ruby>く<ruby>来<rt>き</rt></ruby>てほしい。 | I want you to come quickly. |
|---|---|
| <ruby>静<rt>しず</rt></ruby>かにしてほしい。 | I want you to be quiet. |
| <ruby>一緒<rt>いっしょ</rt></ruby>に<ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>ってほしい。 | I want you to go with me. |

!!! note "てほしい is not an order"
    It expresses the speaker's wish. It is softer than a command but more emotionally exposed than てください.

### 13. てもらえませんか — Humble Request

Asking to receive someone's action. More polite than てください.

| <ruby>写真<rt>しゃしん</rt></ruby>を<ruby>撮<rt>と</rt></ruby>ってもらえませんか。 | Could you take a photo for me? |
|---|---|
| <ruby>送<rt>おく</rt></ruby>ってもらえませんか。 | Could you send it to me? |
| <ruby>教<rt>おし</rt></ruby>えてもらえませんか。 | Could you teach me? |

### 14. てくれてありがとう — Gratitude

Thanking someone for doing something. The くれて embeds the giving frame; ありがとう adds gratitude.

| <ruby>手伝<rt>てつだ</rt></ruby>ってくれてありがとう。 | Thanks for helping me. |
|---|---|
| <ruby>待<rt>ま</rt></ruby>ってくれてありがとう。 | Thanks for waiting. |
| <ruby>送<rt>おく</rt></ruby>ってくれてありがとう。 | Thanks for sending it. |

!!! note "The te-form chain"
    <ruby>手伝<rt>てつだ</rt></ruby>ってくれてありがとう = [help] + [give me] + [thanks]. The two te-forms chain naturally.

### 15. てすみません — Apology

Apologizing for causing trouble through an action.

| <ruby>待<rt>ま</rt></ruby>たせてすみません。 | Sorry for making you wait. |
|---|---|
| <ruby>邪魔<rt>じゃま</rt></ruby>してすみません。 | Sorry for interrupting. |
| <ruby>壊<rt>こわ</rt></ruby>してすみません。 | Sorry for breaking it. |

### 16. てよかった — Emotional Relief

Glad that something happened. Retrospective positive evaluation.

| <ruby>会<rt>あ</rt></ruby>えてよかった。 | I'm glad we could meet. |
|---|---|
| <ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>ってよかった。 | I'm glad I went. |
| <ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>せてよかった。 | I'm glad we could talk. |

!!! note "てよかった vs. てよくなかった"
    The negative form (<ruby>会<rt>あ</rt></ruby>えてよくなかった) is grammatically possible but rarely used. When people regret something, they usually say <ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>かなければよかった ("I shouldn't have gone").

### 17. てある — Deliberate Resultant State

A state that was intentionally created and remains.

| <ruby>窓<rt>まど</rt></ruby>が<ruby>開<rt>あ</rt></ruby>けてある。 | The window has been left open [on purpose]. |
|---|---|
| <ruby>準備<rt>じゅんび</rt></ruby>がしてある。 | Preparations have been made. |
| <ruby>予約<rt>よやく</rt></ruby>がしてある。 | A reservation has been placed. |

!!! warning "てある vs. ている"
    <ruby>窓<rt>まど</rt></ruby>が<ruby>開<rt>あ</rt></ruby>いている = the window is open [state]. <ruby>窓<rt>まど</rt></ruby>が<ruby>開<rt>あ</rt></ruby>けてある = the window has been opened [by someone, and left that way intentionally].

## N3 — Extended: Temporal Depth & Emotional Shading {#n3-extended}

### 18. てくる (Grammaticalized) — Change Over Time

An action or state begins and moves toward the present. "Start to ~ / come to be ~ing."

| <ruby>雨<rt>あめ</rt></ruby>が<ruby>降<rt>ふ</rt></ruby>ってきた。 | It started raining [rain came]. |
|---|---|
| <ruby>寒<rt>さむ</rt></ruby>くなってきた。 | It's getting colder. |
| <ruby>慣<rt>な</rt></ruby>れてきた。 | I'm getting used to it. |

!!! note "From physical to temporal"
    N5: <ruby>買<rt>か</rt></ruby>ってくる = go buy and come back. N3: <ruby>慣<rt>な</rt></ruby>れてきた = become accustomed over time. The directional verb has grammaticalized into a marker of emergent change.

### 19. ていく (Grammaticalized) — Continuation Into Future

An action continues forward from now. "Keep ~ing / proceed with ~ing."

| <ruby>自分<rt>じぶん</rt></ruby>のペースでやっていく。 | I'll keep going at my own pace. |
|---|---|
| <ruby>覚<rt>おぼ</rt></ruby>えていけばいいから。 | You just need to keep learning. |
| これからも<ruby>頑張<rt>がんば</rt></ruby>っていく。 | I'll keep doing my best from here on. |

!!! note "N3 continuation"
    Where N5 <ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>っていく means "take away," N3 やっていく means "continue doing into the future." The spatial metaphor has become temporal.

### 20. てほしくない — Negative Desire

Not wanting someone to do something.

| <ruby>来<rt>き</rt></ruby>てほしくない。 | I don't want you to come. |
|---|---|
| <ruby>触<rt>さわ</rt></ruby>ってほしくない。 | I don't want you to touch it. |
| <ruby>心配<rt>しんぱい</rt></ruby>してほしくない。 | I don't want you to worry. |

### 21. てもらってもいいですか — Soft Permission Request

Asking if it's okay to receive someone's action. Softer than direct request.

| <ruby>使<rt>つか</rt></ruby>ってもらってもいいですか。 | Would it be alright if I had you use this? |
|---|---|
| <ruby>見<rt>み</rt></ruby>てもらってもいい？ | Is it okay if I get you to look at this? |
| <ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>ってもらってもいいですか。 | Could I ask you to hold this? |

### 22. てみせる — Demonstrative

Doing something to show someone, or to prove a point.

| やってみせる。 | I'll do it [and show you]. |
|---|---|
| <ruby>作<rt>つく</rt></ruby>ってみせる。 | I'll make it [to demonstrate]. |
| <ruby>勝<rt>か</rt></ruby>ってみせる。 | I'll win [just watch me]. |

### 23. てばかりいる — Habitual Criticism

Doing nothing but ~, with negative judgment.

| <ruby>遊<rt>あそ</rt></ruby>んでばかりいる。 | All [they] do is play. |
|---|---|
| <ruby>文句<rt>もんく</rt></ruby>を<ruby>言<rt>い</rt></ruby>ってばかりいる。 | All [they] do is complain. |
| <ruby>食<rt>た</rt></ruby>べてばかりいる。 | All [they] do is eat. |

!!! note "The criticism is baked in"
    てばかりいる is almost always used to criticize someone for an imbalance in their behavior.

### 24. てくれ — Casual Imperative

Direct request, often male-coded or urgent. The くれ is the imperative of くれる.

| <ruby>静<rt>しず</rt></ruby>かにしてくれ。 | Be quiet [direct]. |
|---|---|
| <ruby>見<rt>み</rt></ruby>てくれ。 | Look [at this]. |
| <ruby>待<rt>ま</rt></ruby>ってくれ。 | Wait [for me]. |

!!! warning "Not polite"
    てくれ is casual and potentially rough. Do not use with superiors or strangers.

### 25. てもらえる — Potential Receiving

Asking if one can receive a favor. The potential form of てもらう.

| <ruby>教<rt>おし</rt></ruby>えてもらえる？ | Can you teach me? / Can I get you to teach me? |
|---|---|
| <ruby>手伝<rt>てつだ</rt></ruby>ってもらえる？ | Can you help me? |
| <ruby>送<rt>おく</rt></ruby>ってもらえる？ | Can you send it? |

!!! note "Casual potential"
    てもらえる drops the formal ます and creates a casual but still deferential request.

## Japanese Mindset {#japanese-mindset}

The te-form auxiliary system reveals how Japanese conceptualizes action as **socially embedded and temporally unfolding**. English tends to isolate verbs in time and person; Japanese chains them into continuums.

- **Action as gift.** てくれる, てもらう, てあげる frame every action as a transfer of benefit. Even something as simple as "teaching" becomes a social transaction: who gave, who received, who is grateful.
- **Action as preparation.** ておく treats the present as a servant of the future. You do things now so that future-you or future-someone will be okay.
- **Action as emergence.** てくる and ていく treat time as a landscape you move through. Changes approach you (てくる) or you carry actions forward (ていく).
- **Action as emotion.** てよかった and てしまう show that Japanese grammar does not separate "what happened" from "how I feel about it." The feeling is in the verb ending.

## Common Mistakes {#common-mistakes}

### 1. Confusing てあげる with てくれる direction

!!! failure "Social direction error"
    <ruby>先生<rt>せんせい</rt></ruby>が<ruby>教<rt>おし</rt></ruby>えてあげた。 (said by student about teacher)

    ~てあげる implies the speaker (or subject) is giving from above. A teacher teaching a student is not あげる from the student's perspective — it is くれる (the teacher gave the student the favor).~

!!! success "Correct"
    <ruby>先生<rt>せんせい</rt></ruby>が<ruby>教<rt>おし</rt></ruby>えてくれた。

### 2. Using てほしい as an order

!!! failure "Register mismatch"
    <ruby>黙<rt>だま</rt></ruby>ってほしい！ (to a superior)

    ~While grammatically correct, てほしい exposes the speaker's desire directly. With a superior, <ruby>静<rt>しず</rt></ruby>かにしていただけますか is safer.~

!!! success "Appropriate"
    <ruby>少<rt>すこ</rt></ruby>し<ruby>静<rt>しず</rt></ruby>かにしていただけますか。

### 3. Confusing てある with ている

!!! failure "State error"
    ドアが<ruby>開<rt>あ</rt></ruby>いてある。 (when no one opened it intentionally)

    ~てある requires deliberate human action. If the door opened by wind, use <ruby>開<rt>あ</rt></ruby>いている or <ruby>開<rt>あ</rt></ruby>いた.~

!!! success "Correct"
    ドアが<ruby>開<rt>あ</rt></ruby>いている。 (state)
    ドアが<ruby>開<rt>あ</rt></ruby>けてある。 (someone opened it and left it)

### 4. Using N5 directional てくる when N3 emergent is needed

!!! failure "Physical vs. temporal"
    <ruby>日本語<rt>にほんご</rt></ruby>が<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>せてくる。 (sounds like "Japanese comes speaking")

    ~For emergent ability, <ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>せるようになってきた is clearer. <ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>せてくる is not standard.~

!!! success "Correct"
    <ruby>日本語<rt>にほんご</rt></ruby>が<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>せるようになってきた。

## Hidden Points {#hidden-points}

- **ておく often implies "without saying."** <ruby>言<rt>い</rt></ruby>っておく means "I'll say it [and leave it at that]." The おく adds a sense of finality — the speaker handles something so the listener doesn't have to think about it again.
- **てくれてありがとう is structurally recursive.** <ruby>手伝<rt>てつだ</rt></ruby>ってくれて = [help] + [give me] + [te-form connecting to thanks]. The chain is: action → giving frame → gratitude frame. This is standard Japanese emotional syntax.
- **てよかった carries survivor's relief.** <ruby>生<rt>い</rt></ruby>きていてよかった is not just "I'm glad I'm alive" — it is the grammar of having survived something specific. The て marks the continuity of life as a thread that could have been cut.
- **てばかりいる is gendered in perception.** When used for women, it can sound more judgmental than when used for men. The pattern itself is neutral, but social reception varies.
- **てみせる is performative.** It transforms an action into a demonstration. The speaker is not just doing — they are staging the doing for an audience.
- **てほしい cannot be used for third-person desire.** You cannot say <ruby>彼<rt>かれ</rt></ruby>が<ruby>来<rt>き</rt></ruby>てほしい to mean "He wants [someone] to come." For third-person desire, use たがる or reported speech: <ruby>彼<rt>かれ</rt></ruby>は<ruby>来<rt>き</rt></ruby>てほしがっている / <ruby>来<rt>き</rt></ruby>てほしいと<ruby>言<rt>い</rt></ruby>っている.

## See Also {#see-also}

### Same Family

- いる (N5) — auxiliary for progressive and resultant state
- くれる / もらう / あげる (N4) — giving/receiving verbs that attach to te-form
- しまう (N4) — completion/regret auxiliary
- おく (N4) — preparation auxiliary
- みる (N5) — attemptive auxiliary

### Commonly Confused With

- ている vs てある (resultant state deliberate vs. general)
- てくれる vs てもらう (giver's perspective vs. receiver's perspective)
- てほしい vs てください (desire vs. request)
- てくる (N5 directional) vs てくる (N3 emergent)

### Builds From

- Te-form conjugation (N5) — the connective foundation
- Verb stems (N5) — required for recognizing auxiliaries

## Per-Entry Hidden Points {#per-entry-hidden-points}

Insert these immediately after their respective entries in the main body.

### After てあげる (N4 — Outward Giving)

!!! info "Hidden Point — The patronizing trap"
    てあげる frames the speaker as doing a favor from a position of generosity or ability. With peers it sounds kind; with superiors it can sound condescending ("Let me do this for you, since you apparently can't"). When offering help to a boss or elder, お<ruby>手伝<rt>てつだ</rt></ruby>いしましょうか or お<ruby>手伝<rt>てつだ</rt></ruby>いできますか is safer than <ruby>手伝<rt>てつだ</rt></ruby>ってあげます.

### After てくれる (N4 — Inward Giving)

!!! info "Hidden Point — The gratitude engine"
    てくれる is not merely "someone did something" — it is "someone did something *for me*." This makes it the emotional foundation of Japanese social grammar. Even a neutral action like "reminding" becomes a gift when framed with くれる: <ruby>思<rt>おも</rt></ruby>い<ruby>出<rt>だ</rt></ruby>させてくれた ("You reminded me [and I benefited]"). Native speakers feel gratitude structurally, not just lexically.

### After てほしい (N4 — Directed Desire)

!!! info "Hidden Point — Emotional exposure"
    てほしい reveals the speaker's inner want directly. It is softer than a command but more vulnerable than てください because it admits dependency. Saying <ruby>来<rt>き</rt></ruby>てほしい to a romantic partner is intimate; saying it to a colleague is slightly forward unless softened with のですが or んですけど.

### After てもらえませんか (N4 — Humble Request)

!!! info "Hidden Point — The receiving frame as social grace"
    This pattern does not ask "Can you do X?" It asks "Can I receive X from you?" By framing yourself as the recipient, you elevate the listener to the giver. This is why <ruby>教<rt>おし</rt></ruby>えてもらえませんか sounds humbler than <ruby>教<rt>おし</rt></ruby>えてください despite both being requests — the social geometry is reversed.

### After てよかった (N4 — Emotional Relief)

!!! info "Hidden Point — Survivor's grammar"
    <ruby>会<rt>あ</rt></ruby>えてよかった carries a subtle implication that the meeting might not have happened — schedules, distance, or circumstances could have prevented it. The よかった is not generic happiness; it is retrospective relief that a possibility became real. This makes it powerful after reunions, recoveries, and near-misses.

### After てある (N4 — Deliberate Resultant State)

!!! info "Hidden Point — The human intention requirement"
    てある cannot describe natural states. You cannot say <ruby>花<rt>はな</rt></ruby>が<ruby>咲<rt>さ</rt></ruby>いてある because flowers bloom without human agency. You can say <ruby>窓<rt>まど</rt></ruby>が<ruby>開<rt>あ</rt></ruby>けてある because someone opened it deliberately. This intentionality filter is absolute — it is the single most reliable way to distinguish てある from ている.

### After てくる (N3 — Emergent Change)

!!! info "Hidden Point — The approach metaphor"
    The N3 grammaticalized てくる uses the physical metaphor of movement toward the speaker. Change "comes" to you over time: <ruby>寒<rt>さむ</rt></ruby>くなってきた = coldness approached and arrived at my experience. This is why てくる feels more immediate and personal than the neutral なる.

### After ていく (N3 — Continuation Into Future)

!!! info "Hidden Point — The sustainability frame"
    やっていく reframes a task not as something to complete but as something to continue carrying. This is deeply Japanese — the emphasis is on forward endurance rather than achievement. <ruby>頑張<rt>がんば</rt></ruby>っていく is not "I will try hard and succeed"; it is "I will keep striving as I move forward."

### After てばかりいる (N3 — Habitual Criticism)

!!! info "Hidden Point — The imbalance judgment"
    The criticism in てばかりいる is not about frequency alone — it is about *imbalance*. <ruby>遊<rt>あそ</rt></ruby>んでばかりいる implies the person should be doing something else (studying, working) but isn't. The grammar encodes a social expectation of balanced behavior and finds it violated.

### After てくれ (N3 — Casual Imperative)

!!! info "Hidden Point — The favor demand"
    てくれ is grammatically "Do [it] and give [me the action]." It treats the listener as someone who owes the speaker a favor, or at least as someone close enough to receive blunt requests. It is not inherently rude between close male friends, but it is never appropriate for service, workplace, or strangers.
