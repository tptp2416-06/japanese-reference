## Quick Summary {#quick-summary}

<div class="sb-wrap">
<div class="ref-summary">

- <span class="jb">～ちゃいなよ</span> is a warm, casual way of saying <strong>"just go ahead and do it"</strong> or <strong>"why don't you just…"</strong> — it encourages action without pressure.
- It bundles three elements: <strong>～ちゃう</strong> (do it and be done with it) + <strong>～なさい</strong> contracted to <strong>～な</strong> (light command/urging) + <strong>よ</strong> (friendly emphasis).
- The <span class="jb">～ちゃう</span> part injects a sense of <strong>decisive completion</strong>: the speaker frames the action as something to get done with, which paradoxically makes the urging feel lighter and less demanding.
- At N3, this pattern unlocks natural-sounding casual encouragement that sits between the stiff <span class="jb">～てください</span> and the blunt <span class="jb">～て</span> imperative.

</div>
</div>

## Core Meaning {#core-meaning}

<div class="sb-wrap">

<span class="note-callout">Mental anchor: <strong>～ちゃいなよ = "just go ahead and do it already — it's fine, I'm telling you."</strong> The speaker removes hesitation by framing the action as simple, complete-able, and obviously the right move. The <strong>～ちゃう</strong> component does the heavy lifting: it tells the listener the action is a single, clean step — no lingering, no overthinking.</span>

In the input sentence:

<div class="variation-block">
  <div class="jp-quote"><ruby>お<ruby>風呂<rt>ふろ</rt></ruby></ruby><ruby>準備<rt>じゅんび</rt></ruby>できてるから、もう<ruby>先<rt>さき</rt></ruby>に<ruby>入<rt>はい</rt></ruby>っちゃいなよ。</div>
  <div class="translation-pair">
    <span class="nat-eng">Natural English:</span> The bath's ready, so just go ahead and get in first.<br>
    <span class="literal-line">Literal: "The bath preparation is done, so — just go ahead and enter completely / get it done — I'm telling you."</span>
  </div>
  <div class="context-note">Context: A family member or close housemate has prepared the bath. The speaker is offering the listener first use — warm, casual, implying "don't wait for me, don't hesitate."</div>
</div>

A simpler example:

<div class="variation-block">
  <div class="jp-quote"><ruby>遠慮<rt>えんりょ</rt></ruby>しないで、<ruby>食<rt>た</rt></ruby>べちゃいなよ。</div>
  <div class="translation-pair">
    <span class="nat-eng">Natural English:</span> Don't hold back — just eat up!<br>
    <span class="literal-line">Literal: "Without holding back, just go ahead and eat it completely — I'm telling you."</span>
  </div>
  <div class="context-note">Context: A host urging a guest who is hesitating to start eating. The warmth of <span class="jb">～ちゃいなよ</span> pushes past the guest's politeness without being rude.</div>
</div>

## How It Works {#how-it-works}

<div class="sb-wrap">

The pattern works by <strong>reframing hesitation as unnecessary</strong>. The speaker isn't giving an order — they're signaling that the path forward is clear, the conditions are met, and the only thing standing in the way is the listener's own reluctance. The grammar does this through a layered effect:

- <strong>～ちゃう (from ～てしまう):</strong> Adds the nuance of "do it and be done with it." The action is presented as a single, complete-able step — something you can cross off the list. This is what makes the urging feel light: "it's not a big deal, just get it done."
- <strong>～な (contracted from ～なさい):</strong> A mild imperative. At full length (<span class="jb">食べなさい</span>) it's a parental "eat your vegetables." Contracted to <span class="jb">～な</span> and bundled with <span class="jb">～ちゃう</span> and <span class="jb">よ</span>, it softens into something closer to "go on, do it."
- <strong>よ:</strong> The sentence-final particle that wraps the whole thing in friendly assurance — "I'm telling you, it's fine."

### The Role of もう

In the input sentence, <span class="jb">もう</span> ("already / now / go ahead") is a frequent partner. It signals that conditions are already met — the bath is ready, there's no reason to wait. <span class="jb">もう</span> + <span class="jb">～ちゃいなよ</span> is a highly natural collocation that pushes the listener past their hesitation point.

### Real Speech vs. Textbook Form

The spoken form <span class="jb">～ちゃいなよ</span> is a significant contraction from its textbook ancestor. Learners who only know the written forms may not recognize it in fast conversation.

  <div class="pair-item contrast">

## Grammar Analysis {#grammar-analysis}

<div class="sb-wrap">

### Function in Context

In the input sentence, <span class="jb">入っちゃいなよ</span> does several things simultaneously:

- <strong>Signals readiness:</strong> The <span class="jb">お風呂準備できてるから</span> clause establishes that everything is in place. <span class="jb">～ちゃいなよ</span> follows naturally: conditions are met, so action should follow without delay.
- <strong>Removes the listener's social hesitation:</strong> In a shared living situation, there's often a polite dance about who uses the bath first. The speaker cuts through this by making it clear the offer is genuine and the listener should not defer.
- <strong>Frames the action as trivial:</strong> The <span class="jb">～ちゃう</span> component tells the listener: getting in the bath is not a production — you're not imposing, you're just completing a simple action. This is socially strategic.
- <strong>Closes the negotiation:</strong> The <span class="jb">よ</span> at the end puts a full stop on the exchange. The speaker isn't opening a discussion; they're delivering a resolved position: the bath is ready, you're getting in, end of story — said warmly.

Without <span class="jb">～ちゃいなよ</span> — if the speaker said <span class="jb">先に入ってください</span> — the sentence would lose all its casual warmth and sound like a formal instruction, which would be strange in this domestic context. If they said <span class="jb">先に入って</span>, it would be plain and direct but miss the "just get it done, it's nothing" nuance that makes the urging feel light.

## Japanese Mindset {#japanese-mindset}

<div class="sb-wrap">

The <span class="jb">～ちゃいなよ</span> pattern reflects a distinctively Japanese way of being pushy and kind at the same time — a combination that can feel contradictory to English speakers.

- <strong>Permission disguised as instruction:</strong> The grammar is structurally imperative (<span class="jb">～なさい</span> → <span class="jb">～な</span>), but the communicative function is to release the listener from social obligation. In English, permission-giving ("go ahead," "feel free," "please, you first") uses soft, indirect language. In Japanese, the same function can be achieved with a light command — because the command is between equals in a close relationship, it reads as "I'm close enough to you to tell you what to do, and what I'm telling you is: stop being polite and just do the thing you want to do."
- <strong>Completion as liberation:</strong> The <span class="jb">～ちゃう</span> component encodes a cultural value: getting things done and moved past is desirable. By framing the action as "do it and be done with it," the speaker is saying: this isn't a burden, it's just a task — cross it off and relax. This contrasts with English encouragement, which often focuses on the positive outcome rather than the clean completion.
- <strong>Warmth through grammar compression:</strong> The heavy contraction of <span class="jb">～てしまいなさい</span> into <span class="jb">～ちゃいな</span> is itself a signal of closeness. Over-enunciating the full form in a casual domestic setting would feel cold or stiff. The compression is what makes it affectionate — it's the linguistic equivalent of using someone's nickname.
- <strong>Why "just" hides the grammar:</strong> English translations reach for "just" because there's no single word that bundles "completely," "go ahead," and "I'm telling you" together. The Japanese packs all three into <span class="jb">～ちゃいなよ</span> without any of them being explicitly stated — the grammar itself does the work.

</div>

## Nuance Comparison {#nuance-comparison}

<div class="sb-wrap">

| Grammar Point | Core Meaning | Emotional / Pragmatic Nuance | Trigger Context | JLPT |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| <span class="jb">～ちゃいなよ</span> | "Just go ahead and do it" — warm, casual urging | <strong>Friendly push.</strong> Bundles completion + light command + reassurance. Feels close and encouraging. | Family, close friends, relaxed peers — pushing someone past hesitation | N3 |
| <span class="jb">～なさい</span> | "Do it" — direct instruction | <strong>Parent-to-child, teacher-to-student.</strong> Uncontracted form feels stern or institutional in adult conversation. | Authority figure instructing a child or student; stiff if used between adults | N4 |
| <span class="jb">～て</span> | "Do it" — casual request/demand | <strong>Plain and direct.</strong> Can be soft or sharp depending on tone. Lacks the "complete it" nuance of <span class="jb">～ちゃう</span>. | Casual requests between equals; can sound blunt or intimate depending on delivery | N5 |
| <span class="jb">～ちゃえば？</span> | "Why don't you just…?" — suggestion | <strong>Softer and more tentative.</strong> The <span class="jb">～ば</span> conditional makes it a suggestion rather than an urging. Less pushy, more "it's an option." | Suggesting without pushing; when the speaker wants to leave more room for refusal | N3 |
| <span class="jb">～たら？</span> | "What if you…?" — light suggestion | <strong>Even softer.</strong> Often used when the speaker truly doesn't know if the suggestion is right. Can sound detached. | Offering an idea without investment in whether the listener takes it | N4 |

The key distinction from the closest lookalike, <span class="jb">～ちゃえば？</span>, is that <span class="jb">～ちゃいなよ</span> assumes the listener wants to do the thing but is hesitating — so the speaker pushes them past that hesitation. <span class="jb">～ちゃえば？</span> is more neutral: the speaker is offering an idea, and whether the listener takes it or not is genuinely up to them. Use <span class="jb">～ちゃいなよ</span> when you're confident the action is right for the listener; use <span class="jb">～ちゃえば？</span> when you're just putting it on the table.

## Pattern Progression {#pattern-progression}

<div class="sb-wrap">

### What Leads Here

- <strong>～てしまう / ～ちゃう (N4):</strong> The foundational contraction. Learner knows that <span class="jb">～てしまう</span> marks completion, unintended results, or "doing and being done with it," and that <span class="jb">～ちゃう</span> is its casual spoken form: <span class="jb">食べてしまった</span> → <span class="jb">食べちゃった</span>. Without this base, the <span class="jb">～ちゃいなよ</span> bundle is opaque.

- <strong>～なさい (N4):</strong> The polite imperative used from superior to inferior — parent to child, teacher to student. Learner knows that <span class="jb">食べなさい</span> means "eat" as an instruction. This form is the source of the contracted <span class="jb">～な</span> inside <span class="jb">～ちゃいな</span>.

- <strong>Sentence-final よ (N5):</strong> The particle that adds emphasis and shared-context signaling: "I'm telling you." Learner already uses this for friendly insistence and new-information delivery. In <span class="jb">～ちゃいなよ</span>, it's the final layer that makes the whole bundle feel warm rather than barking.

### What This Unlocks

- <strong>～ちゃいな (without よ) (N3 Upper):</strong> Dropping the <span class="jb">よ</span> makes the urging slightly more brusque or efficient — still casual, but closer to "go on, do it" than "go on, do it — I'm telling you." The presence or absence of <span class="jb">よ</span> is a fine-tuning tool for warmth.

- <strong>～んじゃいなよ (N3 Upper):</strong> The voiced variant for む/ぶ/ぬ-ending godan verbs: <span class="jb">飲んじゃいなよ</span> ("just drink up"), <span class="jb">遊んじゃいなよ</span> ("just go play"). The same bundle, but the voiced contraction follows a different phonetic path and learners often hesitate on these forms.

- <strong>～ちゃって (N2 — awareness only):</strong> A softer, more open-ended variant: <span class="jb">先に入っちゃって</span> ("go ahead and get in" — said as a gentle nudge rather than an urging). The <span class="jb">～て</span> form trails off and feels less directive than <span class="jb">～ちゃいな</span>. Natural in the same casual contexts but even less pushy.

</div>

  <div class="jp-quote"><ruby>迷<rt>まよ</rt></ruby>っててもしょうがないよ。<ruby>好<rt>す</rt></ruby>きな<ruby>方<rt>ほう</rt></ruby><ruby>選<rt>えら</rt></ruby>んじゃいなよ。</div>
  <div class="translation-pair">
    <span class="nat-eng">Natural English:</span> No point agonizing over it. Just pick the one you like and be done with it.<br>
    <span class="literal-line">Literal: "Agonizing gets you nowhere. Just go ahead and choose the one you like and be done with it."</span>
  </div>
  <div class="context-note">Context: A friend is stuck in indecision — shopping, ordering food, or making a minor life choice. The speaker is closing the deliberation with a friendly shove.</div>

## Common Mistakes {#common-mistakes}

<div class="sb-wrap">

### Mistake 1: Using the uncontracted form ～てしまいなさい in casual speech

Learners who know <span class="jb">～てしまう</span> and <span class="jb">～なさい</span> but haven't internalized the contraction may produce the full form in casual contexts, which sounds stiff, parental, or even sarcastic.

<div class="minimal-pair">
  <div class="pair-item contrast">
    <span class="pair-label">Unnatural / stiff</span>
    <span class="pair-jp"><ruby>先<rt>さき</rt></ruby>に<ruby>入<rt>はい</rt></ruby>ってしまいなさいよ。</div>
    <span class="pair-en">"Go ahead and get in first." (Sounds like a parent instructing a child, or ironically formal — not natural between adults.)</span>
  </div>
  <div class="pair-item correct">
    <span class="pair-label">Natural casual</span>
    <span class="pair-jp"><ruby>先<rt>さき</rt></ruby>に<ruby>入<rt>はい</rt></ruby>っちゃいなよ。</div>
    <span class="pair-en">"Just go ahead and get in first." (Warm, natural, unmarked casual urging.)</span>
  </div>
</div>

### Mistake 2: Using ～ちゃいなよ with strangers or superiors

The pattern is inherently casual and carries a light pushiness. Using it with someone you don't know well, or someone above you in status, can feel presumptuous or overly familiar — as if you're claiming a closeness you don't have.

<div class="minimal-pair">
  <div class="pair-item contrast">
    <span class="pair-label">Inappropriate (with a superior)</span>
    <span class="pair-jp"><ruby>先<rt>せん</rt></ruby><ruby>輩<rt>ぱい</rt></ruby>、もう<ruby>帰<rt>かえ</rt></ruby>っちゃいなよ。</div>
    <span class="pair-en">"Senpai, just go home already." (Overly familiar — sounds like you're treating your senpai like a peer or junior.)</span>
  </div>
  <div class="pair-item correct">
    <span class="pair-label">Appropriate (with a superior)</span>
    <span class="pair-jp"><ruby>先<rt>せん</rt></ruby><ruby>輩<rt>ぱい</rt></ruby>、お<ruby>先<rt>さき</rt></ruby>に<ruby>帰<rt>かえ</rt></ruby>られてはどうですか。</div>
    <span class="pair-en">"Senpai, why don't you head home first?" (Respectful suggestion, appropriate social distance maintained.)</span>
  </div>
</div>

### Don't Confuse With

<strong>～ちゃダメ (prohibition using ～ちゃ)</strong>

Learners sometimes mishear or misread <span class="jb">～ちゃいなよ</span> as <span class="jb">～ちゃダメ</span> because both begin with <span class="jb">～ちゃ</span> (the contracted <span class="jb">～ては</span>). But <span class="jb">～ちゃいなよ</span> is an urging (from <span class="jb">～てしまいなさい</span>), while <span class="jb">～ちゃダメ</span> is a prohibition (from <span class="jb">～てはいけない</span>). The structural origin is completely different.

<div class="minimal-pair">
  <div class="pair-item correct">
    <span class="pair-label">Urging (～ちゃいなよ)</span>
    <span class="pair-jp"><ruby>食<rt>た</rt></ruby>べちゃいなよ。</span>
    <span class="pair-en">"Just eat up." (Encouragement: 食べてしまいなさい → 食べちゃいな)</span>
  </div>
  <div class="pair-item contrast">
    <span class="pair-label">Prohibition (～ちゃダメ)</span>
    <span class="pair-jp"><ruby>食<rt>た</rt></ruby>べちゃダメ。</span>
    <span class="pair-en">"Don't eat that." (Prohibition: 食べてはいけない → 食べちゃいけない → 食べちゃダメ)</span>
  </div>
</div>

## Collocation Notes {#collocation-notes}

<div class="sb-wrap">

The <span class="jb">～ちゃいなよ</span> pattern frequently pairs with certain signals that frame the action as timely, overdue, or the obvious next step. These combinations are natural enough to learn as chunks.

- <strong><span class="jb">もう</span> + <span class="jb">～ちゃいなよ</span>:</strong> "Already" / "go ahead and… already." The most common collocation. <span class="jb">もう</span> signals that conditions are met and further delay is unnecessary. Together they mean "the time has come, just do it."

<div class="variation-block">
  <div class="jp-quote"><ruby>準備<rt>じゅんび</rt></ruby>できたし、もう<ruby>始<rt>はじ</rt></ruby>めちゃいなよ。</div>
  <div class="translation-pair">
    <span class="nat-eng">Natural English:</span> Everything's ready, so just go ahead and start already.<br>
    <span class="literal-line">Literal: "Preparation is done, so already — just start and be done with it."</span>
  </div>
</div>

- <strong><span class="jb">どうせ</span> + <span class="jb">～ちゃいなよ</span>:</strong> "Anyway" / "might as well just…" Used when the speaker frames the action as inevitable or when alternatives have been exhausted. Carries a slight "no point overthinking" edge.

<div class="variation-block">
  <div class="jp-quote">どうせ<ruby>後<rt>あと</rt></ruby>でやるんだから、<ruby>今<rt>いま</rt></ruby><ruby>終<rt>お</rt></ruby>わらせちゃいなよ。</div>
  <div class="translation-pair">
    <span class="nat-eng">Natural English:</span> You're going to do it later anyway, so you might as well just get it done now.<br>
    <span class="literal-line">Literal: "Since you'll do it later anyway, just go ahead and finish it now and be done with it."</span>
  </div>
</div>

</div>

## Hidden Points {#hidden-points}

<div class="sb-wrap">

- <strong>The ～な in ～ちゃいな is not the prohibitive ～な:</strong> This is a critical structural point that learners can miss. The prohibitive <span class="jb">～な</span> attaches to the dictionary form (<span class="jb">食べるな</span> = "don't eat"), while the <span class="jb">～な</span> in <span class="jb">～ちゃいな</span> is a contraction of <span class="jb">～なさい</span> and attaches to the <span class="jb">～て</span> form via <span class="jb">～ちゃう</span>. The two <span class="jb">な</span> endings are etymologically unrelated but phonetically identical, which can cause a split-second misparse in fast speech.

- <strong>Intonation distinguishes warm urging from impatient pushing:</strong> A flat or slightly descending <span class="jb">～ちゃいなよ</span> sounds warm — "go on, it's fine." A sharply stressed <span class="jb">ちゃいな</span> with a clipped <span class="jb">よ</span> sounds impatient — "just do it already." The difference is entirely in delivery, not vocabulary. N3 learners should be aware that over-flattening their intonation can accidentally make them sound irritable.

- <strong>The pattern can sound gendered in certain contexts:</strong> <span class="jb">～ちゃいなよ</span> itself is relatively gender-neutral in modern casual speech, but the full uncontracted <span class="jb">～てしまいなさい</span> can carry a maternal/stern-feminine association when used by older speakers. In younger speech, both men and women use <span class="jb">～ちゃいなよ</span> freely, but some older male speakers may prefer <span class="jb">～ちゃえよ</span> (using the imperative <span class="jb">～ちゃえ</span> from <span class="jb">～ちゃう</span>) as a rougher alternative.

- <strong>The ～ちゃう layer also softens the command — paradoxically:</strong> At first glance, adding <span class="jb">～ちゃう</span> to an imperative might seem like it would intensify it ("do it completely!"). But in practice, the opposite happens: <span class="jb">～ちゃう</span> reframes the action as a single, clean step, which makes it feel less demanding than a raw <span class="jb">～なさい</span>. <span class="jb">食べなさい</span> is "eat (because I'm telling you to)." <span class="jb">食べちゃいなよ</span> is "just eat up and be done with it — it's nothing." The completion nuance makes the action feel smaller, not larger.

</div>

## Learner Stage {#learner-stage}

<div class="sb-wrap">

- <strong>What an N3 learner can reliably do with this pattern now:</strong> You can recognize <span class="jb">～ちゃいなよ</span> when you hear it and correctly interpret it as friendly urging — you're not confusing it with the prohibitive <span class="jb">～な</span> or mishearing it as <span class="jb">～ちゃダメ</span>. You can produce it in familiar domestic contexts (with family, close friends, partner) where the social relationship is clearly casual.

- <strong>What still requires conscious effort at this stage:</strong> Choosing between <span class="jb">～ちゃいなよ</span>, <span class="jb">～ちゃえば？</span>, and <span class="jb">～たら？</span> in real time based on how pushy you want to be and how close you are to the listener. Knowing when the <span class="jb">よ</span> is needed for warmth and when dropping it changes the tone from friendly to efficient. Comfortably producing the voiced variants (<span class="jb">～んじゃいなよ</span>) without hesitation.

- <strong>What becomes natural later through N2+ immersion:</strong> Hearing the slight intonation shifts that tell you whether a <span class="jb">～ちゃいなよ</span> is affectionately pushy or slightly annoyed. Using the pattern across a wider range of verbs and situations without pre-planning. Understanding when a native speaker uses the bare <span class="jb">～ちゃいな</span> (without <span class="jb">よ</span>) and what that signals about the relationship in that moment.

</div>

## Reference Call Back

<div class="ref-callback">
  <h4 id="reference-call-back">Reference Call Back</h4>
  <p>If you've studied <span class="jb">～てしまう／～ちゃう</span> at N4, you learned it marks completion or unintended results. In <span class="jb">～ちゃいなよ</span>, the completion meaning is doing pragmatic work: the speaker calls the action "something you can just complete and be done with," which is what makes the urging feel light and encouraging rather than heavy. The same <span class="jb">～ちゃう</span> that expresses regret in <span class="jb">忘れちゃった</span> ("I forgot — oops, it's done") expresses liberation in <span class="jb">入っちゃいなよ</span> ("get in — just do it and it's done"). This is a common pattern in Japanese: the same grammar can encode opposite emotional tones depending on whether the "completion" is desired or undesired.</p>
</div>

## See Also {#see-also}

<div class="sb-wrap">

### Same Family
Related patterns within the same grammatical or functional family (casual urging, contractions of ～てしまう + imperative/suggestive forms):
- ～ちゃえば？ — "Why don't you just…?" — Softer suggestion using the conditional
- ～ちゃえ(よ) — "Just do it" — Rougher, more direct casual imperative from ～ちゃう
- ～ちゃって — "Go ahead and…" — Gentle nudge using the ～て form

### Commonly Confused With
Structurally unrelated patterns learners mix up with this one:
- ～ちゃダメ — "Don't…" — Prohibition using contracted ～ては + いけない (not from ～てしまう)
- ～な (prohibitive) — "Don't…" — Dictionary form + な (not related to the contracted ～なさい)

### Builds From
Simpler patterns that lead to this one:
- ～てしまう／～ちゃう (N4) — The completion / unintended-result contraction
- ～なさい (N4) — The polite imperative, source of the ～な contraction
- Sentence-final よ (N5) — Friendly emphasis particle

### Unlocks Next
Patterns that build on this one at the next level:
- ～んじゃいなよ — Voiced variant for む/ぶ/ぬ-ending verbs
- ～ちゃいな (without よ) — Bare efficient urging
- ～しぃな (Kansai-ben) — Regional variant of the same urging pattern [N2 — awareness only]
