# Extra Modest Expressions
## <ruby>謙<rt>けん</rt></ruby><ruby>譲<rt>じょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> I (Kenjōgo) — Show Respect by Lowering Your Own Status

---

## 1. Overview & Nuance

**Extra Modest Expressions** (<ruby>謙<rt>けん</rt></ruby><ruby>譲<rt>じょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby>) are a category of <ruby>敬<rt>けい</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> used when speaking about *your own actions* — or the actions of your in-group — in order to show respect to the listener. Rather than elevating the listener directly (which is the job of Sonkeigo), humble speech works by *lowering the speaker*, creating vertical social distance that flatters the listener by comparison.

The psychological intent is crucial: you are **symbolically bowing through language**. By choosing a verb form that means "I humbly do X" instead of simply "I do X," you communicate awareness of the other person's higher status — in age, position, or social role such as "customer."

In Japanese society, respect is often expressed through the concept of distance. This isn't about being weak; it's about being culturally intelligent. Using these forms demonstrates that you understand the social hierarchy and your place within it.

### How it differs from <ruby>丁<rt>てい</rt></ruby><ruby>寧<rt>ねい</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby>

| Form | Purpose | Direction of Respect |
|---|---|---|
| Teineigo (-masu) | General politeness; neutral distance | Flat — simply polite |
| Kenjōgo (Extra Modest) | Lower yourself to elevate the listener | ↓ Speaker goes down |
| Sonkeigo (Honorific) | Directly elevate the listener's actions | ↑ Listener goes up |

A simple way to think about it: `-masu` is *polite neutrality*. Kenjōgo is *a verbal bow*. If Teineigo is business casual, Kenjōgo is a pressed suit.

## 2. Conjugation Rules

### 2a. Special Modest Verbs

Several high-frequency verbs have **completely irregular humble forms** that must be memorized. These are the ones you will encounter most often in real life — in customer service, business, and formal introductions.

| Plain Verb | Meaning | Humble Form | With -masu |
|---|---|---|---|
| <ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>く / <ruby>来<rt>く</rt></ruby>る | to go / to come | <ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>る | <ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>ります |
| いる | to be / exist (animate) | おる | おります |
| ある | to exist (inanimate) | ございます | ございます |
| です | to be (copula) | でございます | でございます |
| する | to do | <ruby>致<rt>いた</rt></ruby>す | <ruby>致<rt>いた</rt></ruby>します |
| ている | ongoing / continuous | ております | ております |
| <ruby>言<rt>い</rt></ruby>う | to say | <ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>す | <ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>します / <ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>し<ruby>上<rt>あ</rt></ruby>げます |
| <ruby>食<rt>た</rt></ruby>べる / <ruby>飲<rt>の</rt></ruby>む / もらう | to eat / drink / receive | いただく | いただきます |
| <ruby>知<rt>し</rt></ruby>っている | to know | <ruby>存<rt>ぞん</rt></ruby>じております | <ruby>存<rt>ぞん</rt></ruby>じております |
| <ruby>見<rt>み</rt></ruby>る | to see / look | <ruby>拝<rt>はい</rt></ruby><ruby>見<rt>けん</rt></ruby>する | <ruby>拝<rt>はい</rt></ruby><ruby>見<rt>けん</rt></ruby>します |

---

### 2b. The Productive Pattern: お + Verb Stem + する／いたす

For verbs that *don't* have a special humble form, Japanese provides a productive template. Three steps:

1. **Take the -masu stem.** Remove ます from the polite form.<br>
   例: <ruby>書<rt>か</rt></ruby>きます → <ruby>書<rt>か</rt></ruby>き

2. **Add お before the stem.** This prefix is a respect marker.<br>
   例: お<ruby>書<rt>か</rt></ruby>き

3. **Attach する (or the more formal いたす) after the stem.**<br>
   例: お<ruby>書<rt>か</rt></ruby>きする → お<ruby>書<rt>か</rt></ruby>きいたします

| Plain Verb | -masu Stem | Standard Humble | Extra Formal Humble |
|---|---|---|---|
| <ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>つ (to carry) | <ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>ち | お<ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>ちする | お<ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>ちいたします |
| <ruby>送<rt>おく</rt></ruby>る (to send) | <ruby>送<rt>おく</rt></ruby>り | お<ruby>送<rt>おく</rt></ruby>りする | お<ruby>送<rt>おく</rt></ruby>りいたします |
| <ruby>待<rt>ま</rt></ruby>つ (to wait) | <ruby>待<rt>ま</rt></ruby>ち | お<ruby>待<rt>ま</rt></ruby>ちする | お<ruby>待<rt>ま</rt></ruby>ちいたします |
| <ruby>呼<rt>よ</rt></ruby>ぶ (to call) | <ruby>呼<rt>よ</rt></ruby>び | お<ruby>呼<rt>よ</rt></ruby>びする | お<ruby>呼<rt>よ</rt></ruby>びいたします |

!!! note "Sino-Japanese verbs use ご, not お"
    For する verbs with Chinese-origin nouns like <ruby>連<rt>れん</rt></ruby><ruby>絡<rt>らく</rt></ruby>する, <ruby>説<rt>せつ</rt></ruby><ruby>明<rt>めい</rt></ruby>する, <ruby>案<rt>あん</rt></ruby><ruby>内<rt>ない</rt></ruby>する, the prefix changes from お to ご, and you skip the -masu stem logic entirely: **ご + noun + する／いたす**.

    例: ご<ruby>連<rt>れん</rt></ruby><ruby>絡<rt>らく</rt></ruby>いたします = "I will (humbly) contact you."<br>
    例: ご<ruby>案<rt>あん</rt></ruby><ruby>内<rt>ない</rt></ruby><ruby>致<rt>いた</rt></ruby>します = "I will (humbly) guide you."

## 3. Progressive Usage Ladder

The following examples escalate in social weight. Notice how the same grammar is recycled across vastly different contexts — the grammar itself is fixed; what changes is *who you're talking to*.

### Level 1 — Strict / Formal (Interview, Ceremonial Self-Introduction)

!!! example "Example 1 · Self-Introduction"
    <ruby>田<rt>た</rt></ruby><ruby>中<rt>なか</rt></ruby>と<ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>します。アメリカから<ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>りました。<br>
    *"My name is Tanaka. I have come from America."*

    Classic job interview opener. <ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>します replaces <ruby>言<rt>い</rt></ruby>います; <ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>りました replaces <ruby>来<rt>き</rt></ruby>ました.

!!! example "Example 2 · Interview Statement"
    <ruby>現<rt>げん</rt></ruby><ruby>在<rt>ざい</rt></ruby>、ABC<ruby>会<rt>がい</rt></ruby><ruby>社<rt>しゃ</rt></ruby>で<ruby>働<rt>はたら</rt></ruby>いております。<br>
    *"I am currently working at ABC Company."*

    おる is used in its て-form → 〜ており(ます), the humble equivalent of the progressive 〜ている.

!!! example "Example 3 · Showing Respect for Expertise"
    お<ruby>名<rt>な</rt></ruby><ruby>前<rt>まえ</rt></ruby>は<ruby>以<rt>い</rt></ruby><ruby>前<rt>ぜん</rt></ruby>から<ruby>存<rt>ぞん</rt></ruby>じております。<br>
    *"I have known your name for some time."*

    <ruby>存<rt>ぞん</rt></ruby>じております is the humble form of <ruby>知<rt>し</rt></ruby>っています. Crucial in first meetings with someone famous or senior.

---

### Level 2 — Business Polite (Client, Superior, Customer Service)

!!! example "Example 4 · Phone / Email"
    <ruby>明<rt>あ</rt></ruby><ruby>日<rt>した</rt></ruby>、<ruby>資<rt>し</rt></ruby><ruby>料<rt>りょう</rt></ruby>をお<ruby>送<rt>おく</rt></ruby>りいたします。<br>
    *"I will send the documents tomorrow."*

    Textbook example of the お + stem + いたす pattern. Standard in client email.

!!! example "Example 5 · Offering Help"
    お<ruby>荷<rt>に</rt></ruby><ruby>物<rt>もつ</rt></ruby>、お<ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>ちしましょうか。<br>
    *"Shall I carry your luggage for you?"*

    The humble pattern combines naturally with 〜ましょうか to make humble offers. Very common in hotels and stores.

!!! example "Example 6 · Customer Service"
    <ruby>少<rt>しょう</rt></ruby><ruby>々<rt>しょう</rt></ruby>お<ruby>待<rt>ま</rt></ruby>ちください。<ruby>担<rt>たん</rt></ruby><ruby>当<rt>とう</rt></ruby><ruby>者<rt>しゃ</rt></ruby>にご<ruby>連<rt>れん</rt></ruby><ruby>絡<rt>らく</rt></ruby>いたします。<br>
    *"Please wait a moment. I will contact the person in charge."*

    Notice the ご + <ruby>連<rt>れん</rt></ruby><ruby>絡<rt>らく</rt></ruby> + いたす pattern for a Sino-Japanese verb.

!!! example "Example 7 · Referring to Your In-Group (Uchi-Soto in Action)"
    <ruby>社<rt>しゃ</rt></ruby><ruby>長<rt>ちょう</rt></ruby>の<ruby>山<rt>やま</rt></ruby><ruby>田<rt>だ</rt></ruby>はただいま<ruby>席<rt>せき</rt></ruby>を<ruby>外<rt>はず</rt></ruby>しております。<br>
    *"Our president, Yamada, is currently away from his desk."*

    Even though Yamada is your boss, you use humble language about him to an *outside* customer. This is the uchi-soto rule in action — your boss belongs to your group relative to the outsider.

---

### Level 3 — Social Context: Humble Forms in Everyday Formal Speech

!!! note "When does humble speech appear outside the office?"
    The full <ruby>致<rt>いた</rt></ruby>します register would feel stiff between friends. But 〜ております and お + stem + する appear in softer social contexts — introducing yourself at a PTA meeting, speaking to your partner's parents, or making a humble offer to a neighbor.

!!! example "Example 8 · Meeting a Partner's Parents"
    <ruby>東<rt>とう</rt></ruby><ruby>京<rt>きょう</rt></ruby>で<ruby>英<rt>えい</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby>を<ruby>教<rt>おし</rt></ruby>えております。<br>
    *"I teach English in Tokyo."*

    〜ております softens a casual fact into something appropriate for a respectful first meeting.

!!! example "Example 9 · Polite Social Offer"
    タクシーをお<ruby>呼<rt>よ</rt></ruby>びしましょうか。<br>
    *"Shall I call a taxi for you?"*

    Works when hosting older guests — more respectful than タクシーを<ruby>呼<rt>よ</rt></ruby>びましょうか, but not as stiff as business speech.

!!! example "Example 10 · Expressing Gratitude"
    おいしいお<ruby>茶<rt>ちゃ</rt></ruby>をいただきました。ありがとうございます。<br>
    *"I received some delicious tea. Thank you very much."*

    いただく is the quiet workhorse of humble speech — used constantly and perfectly natural even in moderately formal social situations.

!!! example "Example 11 · Awaiting a Reply (Closing an Email)"
    <ruby>連<rt>れん</rt></ruby><ruby>絡<rt>らく</rt></ruby>をお<ruby>待<rt>ま</rt></ruby>ちしております。<br>
    *"I am humbly awaiting your contact."*

    Uses て + おります (the modest て-form). Perfect for closing emails where you are waiting for a decision or reply.

!!! example "Example 12 · Acknowledging a Superior's Message"
    そのメールは、もう<ruby>拝<rt>はい</rt></ruby><ruby>見<rt>けん</rt></ruby>しました。<br>
    *"I have already humbly looked at that email."*

    <ruby>拝<rt>はい</rt></ruby><ruby>見<rt>けん</rt></ruby> is the humble version of <ruby>見<rt>み</rt></ruby>る. Shows you have paid careful attention to the superior's message.

## 4. The Keigo Hierarchy — Side-by-Side

Memorizing these triplets is one of the fastest ways to cement the logic of keigo. **The golden rule: *my* actions → Kenjōgo. *Their* actions → Sonkeigo. Neutral / general description → Teineigo.**

| Meaning | Kenjōgo (Humble — self) | Teineigo (Polite — neutral) | Sonkeigo (Honorific — other) |
|---|---|---|---|
| to be / exist | おります | います | いらっしゃいます |
| to go / come | <ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>ります | <ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>きます / <ruby>来<rt>き</rt></ruby>ます | いらっしゃいます |
| to do | <ruby>致<rt>いた</rt></ruby>します | します | なさいます |
| to say | <ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>します | <ruby>言<rt>い</rt></ruby>います | おっしゃいます |
| to eat / drink | いただきます | <ruby>食<rt>た</rt></ruby>べます / <ruby>飲<rt>の</rt></ruby>みます | <ruby>召<rt>め</rt></ruby>し<ruby>上<rt>あ</rt></ruby>がります |
| to see | <ruby>拝<rt>はい</rt></ruby><ruby>見<rt>けん</rt></ruby>します | <ruby>見<rt>み</rt></ruby>ます | ご<ruby>覧<rt>らん</rt></ruby>になります |
| to know | <ruby>存<rt>ぞん</rt></ruby>じております | <ruby>知<rt>し</rt></ruby>っています | ご<ruby>存<rt>ぞん</rt></ruby>じです |
| to exist (inanimate) | ございます | あります | N/A |
| to be (copula) | でございます | です | N/A |

## 5. The Mistake Monitor

!!! tip "Social Pitfall — The Uchi-Soto Rule"
    Humble speech is for **you and your in-group (<ruby>内<rt>うち</rt></ruby>)** — never for the listener or their group (<ruby>外<rt>そと</rt></ruby>). A common early mistake:

    ❌ お<ruby>客<rt>きゃく</rt></ruby><ruby>様<rt>さま</rt></ruby>が<ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>りました。 (lowers the customer — the opposite of your intent)<br>
    ✅ お<ruby>客<rt>きゃく</rt></ruby><ruby>様<rt>さま</rt></ruby>がいらっしゃいました。 (Sonkeigo — correct)

    Conversely, when referring to *your own boss* while speaking to an outside client, use **humble** speech for your boss — because your boss is in *your* group relative to the outsider. This is counterintuitive for learners from hierarchical cultures that always elevate the boss.

    Remember: use modest speech for your boss when talking *about* your boss to a client, but use honorific speech when talking *directly to* your boss.

!!! warning "Grammatical Pitfall — Keigo Double-Dipping (<ruby>二<rt>に</rt></ruby><ruby>重<rt>じゅう</rt></ruby><ruby>敬<rt>けい</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby>)"
    Do not stack humble and honorific structures, or apply the お〜する pattern on top of a verb that already has a special humble form.

    ❌ お<ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>しいたします。 (<ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>す is already humble — don't add お〜いたす on top)<br>
    ✅ <ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>します。 / <ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>し<ruby>上<rt>あ</rt></ruby>げます。

    Another classic error — applying humble speech to the *listener's* action:

    ❌ <ruby>先<rt>せん</rt></ruby><ruby>生<rt>せい</rt></ruby>はコーヒーをいただきますか。 (uses humble "receive/drink" for the teacher)<br>
    ✅ <ruby>先<rt>せん</rt></ruby><ruby>生<rt>せい</rt></ruby>はコーヒーを<ruby>召<rt>め</rt></ruby>し<ruby>上<rt>あ</rt></ruby>がりますか。 (Sonkeigo — elevating the teacher's action)

!!! warning "Grammar Pitfall — Using お〜する When a Special Verb Exists"
    If a verb has an irregular humble form, use it — don't invent a pattern-based version.

    ❌ お<ruby>食<rt>た</rt></ruby>べします。 (invented pattern — unnatural)<br>
    ✅ いただきます。 (correct special humble form for eating/drinking/receiving)

!!! warning "Social Mismatch — Using Keigo with Friends"
    Extra Modest forms are for formal settings with a clear social gap. Using <ruby>致<rt>いた</rt></ruby>します or おります with close friends creates a cold, yoso-yososhii distance.

    Stick to casual or standard polite speech with peers.

## 6. Practice Drill

1. **Translate to humble Japanese:** "My name is Smith. I come from Canada." (formal self-introduction)
2. **Translate to humble Japanese:** "I will send the report by email tomorrow." (to a client)
3. **Convert:** 「<ruby>今<rt>いま</rt></ruby>、<ruby>会<rt>かい</rt></ruby><ruby>議<rt>ぎ</rt></ruby><ruby>室<rt>しつ</rt></ruby>にいます。」(talking about your own location to a boss on the phone)
4. **Convert:** 「<ruby>駅<rt>えき</rt></ruby>まで<ruby>車<rt>くるま</rt></ruby>で<ruby>送<rt>おく</rt></ruby>りましょうか。」(humble offer to drive someone to the station)
5. **Spot and fix the error:** <ruby>部<rt>ぶ</rt></ruby><ruby>長<rt>ちょう</rt></ruby>の<ruby>鈴<rt>すず</rt></ruby><ruby>木<rt>き</rt></ruby>はただいまいらっしゃいません。(said to an outside client)

    **Question 1**<br>
    スミスと<ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>します。カナダから<ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>りました。<br>
    <ruby>言<rt>い</rt></ruby>う → <ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>す for stating one's own name; <ruby>来<rt>く</rt></ruby>る → <ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>る for humbly "coming." Past tense <ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>りました is correct because the arrival is a completed fact at the moment of speaking. Crucially, drop the -san from your own name when being modest to outsiders.

    ---

    **Question 2**<br>
    <ruby>明<rt>あ</rt></ruby><ruby>日<rt>した</rt></ruby>、レポートをメールでお<ruby>送<rt>おく</rt></ruby>りいたします。<br>
    <ruby>送<rt>おく</rt></ruby>る has no special humble form, so use the productive pattern: お + <ruby>送<rt>おく</rt></ruby>り (stem) + いたす. Using いたす rather than する raises the register to business-appropriate. Both お<ruby>送<rt>おく</rt></ruby>りします and お<ruby>送<rt>おく</rt></ruby>りいたします are grammatically fine; the latter is more formal.

    ---

    **Question 3**<br>
    <ruby>今<rt>いま</rt></ruby>、<ruby>会<rt>かい</rt></ruby><ruby>議<rt>ぎ</rt></ruby><ruby>室<rt>しつ</rt></ruby>におります。<br>
    いる → おる. The -masu form is おります. Used for describing your own location or state — common on the phone.

    ---

    **Question 4**<br>
    <ruby>駅<rt>えき</rt></ruby>まで<ruby>車<rt>くるま</rt></ruby>でお<ruby>送<rt>おく</rt></ruby>りしましょうか。<br>
    Classic お + stem + する pattern combined with 〜ましょうか to make a humble offer. The humble pattern works best when your action directly benefits the listener — which is exactly the case here.

    ---

    **Question 5**<br>
    ❌ <ruby>部<rt>ぶ</rt></ruby><ruby>長<rt>ちょう</rt></ruby>の<ruby>鈴<rt>すず</rt></ruby><ruby>木<rt>き</rt></ruby>はただいまいらっしゃいません。<br>
    ✅ <ruby>部<rt>ぶ</rt></ruby><ruby>長<rt>ちょう</rt></ruby>の<ruby>鈴<rt>すず</rt></ruby><ruby>木<rt>き</rt></ruby>はただいま<ruby>席<rt>せき</rt></ruby>を<ruby>外<rt>はず</rt></ruby>しております。/ おりません。<br>
    Error: いらっしゃいません is Sonkeigo (honorific) — but Buchou Suzuki is *your in-group* relative to the outside client. You must switch to humble speech: おりません, or the more natural <ruby>席<rt>せき</rt></ruby>を<ruby>外<rt>はず</rt></ruby>しております ("is away from his desk"). This is the uchi-soto rule at its trickiest — elevating your own boss to an outsider is a social faux pas in Japanese.
