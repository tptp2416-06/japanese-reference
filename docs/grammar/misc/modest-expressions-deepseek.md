# Lowering Yourself to Elevate Others

## <ruby>謙譲<rt>けんじょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> — Humble and Extra-Modest Speech

---

## Quick Summary {#quick-summary}

!!! tip "At a glance"
    - <ruby>謙譲<rt>けんじょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> (kenjōgo) is the Japanese **humble language** — it lowers the speaker (or the speaker's in-group) in order to show respect to the listener or to a third party.
    - There are **two types** of <ruby>謙譲<rt>けんじょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby>:
        - **<ruby>謙譲<rt>けんじょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> I (Humble I):** The speaker's action is directed **toward** the respected person. The lowering highlights the target's elevated status. e.g. ご<ruby>説明<rt>せつめい</rt></ruby>する (I humbly explain to you).
        - **<ruby>謙譲<rt>けんじょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> II (Humble II / Extra-Modest):** The speaker lowers themselves **as a manner of speaking**, regardless of who the action targets. It shows formal politeness to the listener. e.g. <ruby>私<rt>わたくし</rt></ruby>が<ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>ります (I will go [formal]).
    - Some verbs have **special humble forms** — いる → おる, <ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>く・<ruby>来<rt>く</rt></ruby>る → <ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>る, <ruby>言<rt>ゆ</rt></ruby>う → <ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>す, する → <ruby>致<rt>いた</rt></ruby>す.
    - The **お・ご + verb stem + する** pattern is the standard way to create humble expressions from most verbs when the action involves the respected person.
    - The reference sentence from the 〜てある grammar page — そのことはもう<ruby>社長<rt>しゃちょう</rt></ruby>に**お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>しして**あります — uses this pattern: お + <ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>し (stem of <ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>す) + する. It humbly describes the speaker's action toward the company president, while still claiming responsibility through 〜てある.

---

## Why "Extra Modest"? — The Two Layers of <ruby>謙譲<rt>けんじょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> {#two-layers}

Many learners first encounter <ruby>謙譲<rt>けんじょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> as simply "humble" — the speaker lowers their own actions when those actions affect someone higher. But Japanese grammar divides this into two distinct categories. Understanding the difference is essential for sounding natural in formal and professional settings.

| | <ruby>謙譲<rt>けんじょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> I (Humble I) | <ruby>謙譲<rt>けんじょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> II (Humble II / <ruby>丁重<rt>ていちょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby>) |
|--|----------------------|--------------------------------|
| **What it does** | Lowers the speaker's action toward a respected person. | Lowers the speaker's existence/actions as a formal mannerism, showing politeness to the **listener** (regardless of the action's target). |
| **Who is respected?** | The person the action is **directed at**. | The **listener** (the person you're speaking to). |
| **Does the action need a target?** | Yes — the action goes to someone, and that someone is respected. | No — the action can be self-contained (going, being, saying). |
| **Example Verb** | <ruby>伺<rt>うかが</rt></ruby>う (to visit/humble → go to a superior) | <ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>る (to go/come → formal self-reference) |
| **When to use** | Talking about doing something for/at/to a superior or customer. | Formal speech, announcements, business letters, speeches — showing general modesty to the audience. |

!!! info "The terms"
    In Japanese, <ruby>謙譲<rt>けんじょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> I is sometimes called <ruby>謙譲<rt>けんじょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby>A, and <ruby>謙譲<rt>けんじょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> II is <ruby>謙譲<rt>けんじょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby>B or <ruby>丁重<rt>ていちょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> (teichōgo). The "extra modest" in the title of this page refers primarily to <ruby>謙譲<rt>けんじょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> II — the layer of formality that goes beyond direct action-target respect.

## Special Humble Verbs — The Core Set {#special-verbs}

Some of the most frequently used verbs have **fixed humble equivalents** that you must memorise. The same verb often has different forms depending on whether you're using Humble I or Humble II.

### Humble I vs. Humble II — Side by Side

| Plain Verb | Humble I (action toward respected person) | Humble II / Extra-Modest (formal self-reference) |
|------------|-------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------|
| いる (to be / to exist) | — (rarely used in Humble I) | **おる** |
| <ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>く (to go) | **<ruby>伺<rt>うかが</rt></ruby>う** (visit a superior) | **<ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>る** (go / come, formal) |
| <ruby>来<rt>く</rt></ruby>る (to come) | — | **<ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>る** (come, formal) |
| <ruby>言<rt>い</rt></ruby>う (to say) | **<ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>し<ruby>上<rt>あ</rt></ruby>げる** (say to a superior) | **<ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>す** (say, formal self-reference) |
| する (to do) | **<ruby>致<rt>いた</rt></ruby>す** (do for a superior, context-dependent) | **<ruby>致<rt>いた</rt></ruby>す** (do, formal) |
| <ruby>見<rt>み</rt></ruby>る (to see) | **<ruby>拝見<rt>はいけん</rt></ruby>する** (look at / see, humble) | — |
| <ruby>聞<rt>き</rt></ruby>く (to ask / listen) | **<ruby>伺<rt>うかが</rt></ruby>う** (ask / visit) | **<ruby>伺<rt>うかが</rt></ruby>う** can also be formal self-reference |
| <ruby>食<rt>た</rt></ruby>べる・<ruby>飲<rt>の</rt></ruby>む (to eat/drink) | **<ruby>頂<rt>いた</rt></ruby>く** (receive / partake) | **<ruby>頂<rt>いた</rt></ruby>く** (formal humble consumption) |
| <ruby>知<rt>し</rt></ruby>っている (to know) | **<ruby>存<rt>ぞん</rt></ruby>じ<ruby>上<rt>あ</rt></ruby>げる** (I am aware of [your situation]) | **<ruby>存<rt>ぞん</rt></ruby>じる** (I know, formal) |
| あげる (to give) | **<ruby>差<rt>さ</rt></ruby>し<ruby>上<rt>あ</rt></ruby>げる** (give to a superior) | — |
| もらう (to receive) | **<ruby>頂<rt>いた</rt></ruby>く** (receive from a superior) | — |

!!! tip "おる — the extra-modest いる"
    おる is the Humble II equivalent of いる. It lowers the speaker's own existence in formal settings. You will hear it constantly in business Japanese, announcements, and customer service.

    | いる | おる |
    |------|------|
    | <ruby>私<rt>わたくし</rt></ruby>はここに**います**。 | <ruby>私<rt>わたくし</rt></ruby>はここに**おります**。 |
    | "I am here." (neutral/polite) | "I am here." (humble/formal — the speaker is lowering themselves to the listener) |

    おる is also used to humbly refer to one's own in-group members: <ruby>父<rt>ちち</rt></ruby>は<ruby>家<rt>いえ</rt></ruby>におります ("My father is at home").

## The お・ご + Verb Stem + する Pattern {#o-go-pattern}

For verbs that do not have a special humble form, the standard <ruby>謙譲<rt>けんじょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> I construction is:

> **お／ご + [verb stem] + する**

This describes the speaker doing something **for** or **directed toward** a respected person. It lowers the action because it's being done in service of someone higher.

### Formation Rules

| Verb Type | Prefix | Pattern | Example |
|-----------|--------|---------|---------|
| Native Japanese verb (<ruby>和語<rt>わご</rt></ruby>) | **お** | お + stem + する | <ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>す → **お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>し**する |
| Sino-Japanese verb (<ruby>漢語<rt>かんご</rt></ruby>, often する-verbs) | **ご** | ご + stem + する | <ruby>説明<rt>せつめい</rt></ruby>する → **ご<ruby>説明<rt>せつめい</rt></ruby>**する |
| Exceptions / special verbs | (use special form) | — | する → <ruby>致<rt>いた</rt></ruby>す, not ご〜する |

| Plain Verb | Humble (お/ご + stem + する) | Meaning |
|------------|-------------------------------|---------|
| <ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>す | **お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>し**する | To speak (to a superior) |
| <ruby>伝<rt>つた</rt></ruby>える | **お<ruby>伝<rt>つた</rt></ruby>え**する | To convey / inform (to a superior) |
| <ruby>送<rt>おく</rt></ruby>る | **お<ruby>送<rt>おく</rt></ruby>り**する | To send (to a superior) |
| <ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>つ | **お<ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>ち**する | To carry / hold (for a superior) |
| <ruby>説明<rt>せつめい</rt></ruby>する | **ご<ruby>説明<rt>せつめい</rt></ruby>**する | To explain (to a superior) |
| <ruby>案内<rt>あんない</rt></ruby>する | **ご<ruby>案内<rt>あんない</rt></ruby>**する | To guide / show (to a superior) |
| <ruby>報告<rt>ほうこく</rt></ruby>する | **ご<ruby>報告<rt>ほうこく</rt></ruby>**する | To report (to a superior) |
| <ruby>連絡<rt>れんらく</rt></ruby>する | **ご<ruby>連絡<rt>れんらく</rt></ruby>**する | To contact (a superior) |

!!! warning "The polite です・ます goes on する"
    お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>しする → お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>しします (polite humble). You conjugate する as usual: お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>しして (te-form), お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>しした (past), お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>ししてある (humble + prepared state).

### The Reference Sentence — Connecting to 〜てある

> そのことはもう<ruby>社長<rt>しゃちょう</rt></ruby>に**お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>しして**あります。

This is a perfect fusion of two grammar points:
- **お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>しする** = humble form of <ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>す (the speaker lowers themselves toward the president).
- **〜てある** = the prepared state construction (the action was done deliberately earlier, and the result — the president being informed — is now in place).

Together, お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>ししてあります means: *"I have humbly spoken (to the president) and the matter is now taken care of."* The speaker claims responsibility (〜てある) while showing proper deference (お〜する). This combination is extremely common in professional Japanese.

## When to Use <ruby>謙譲<rt>けんじょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> I vs. <ruby>謙譲<rt>けんじょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> II {#when-to-use}

The choice depends on whether your action has a respected target, and who you are speaking to.

| Situation | Use | Example |
|-----------|-----|---------|
| You are doing something **for or to a superior/customer** | **<ruby>謙譲<rt>けんじょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> I** (special verb or お/ご + stem + する) | <ruby>部<rt>ぶ</rt></ruby><ruby>長<rt>ちょう</rt></ruby>を<ruby>駅<rt>えき</rt></ruby>まで**お<ruby>送<rt>おく</rt></ruby>りします**。 "I'll drive the department chief to the station." |
| You are simply stating your own action in a **formal setting** (announcement, speech, business letter) — no specific superior as target | **<ruby>謙譲<rt>けんじょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> II** (special verbs like おる, <ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>る, <ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>す, <ruby>致<rt>いた</rt></ruby>す) | <ruby>私<rt>わたくし</rt></ruby>は<ruby>田中<rt>たなか</rt></ruby>と**<ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>します**。 "I am Tanaka." (formal introduction) |
| You are talking about **your own in-group member** to an outsider | **<ruby>謙譲<rt>けんじょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> II** | <ruby>父<rt>ちち</rt></ruby>は<ruby>今<rt>いま</rt></ruby>**おります**が、<ruby>後<rt>のち</rt></ruby>ほど<ruby>折<rt>お</rt></ruby>り<ruby>返<rt>かえ</rt></ruby>し**お<ruby>電話<rt>でんわ</rt></ruby><ruby>致<rt>いた</rt></ruby>します**。 "My father is here now, but I'll have him call you back." |
| You are writing a formal email to a client | Both — <ruby>謙譲<rt>けんじょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> I for directed actions; <ruby>謙譲<rt>けんじょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> II for general self-modesty | <ruby>資料<rt>しりょう</rt></ruby>を**ご<ruby>送付<rt>そうふ</rt></ruby><ruby>致<rt>いた</rt></ruby>します**。 "I will send you the documents." (<ruby>送付<rt>そうふ</rt></ruby> = sending, <ruby>致<rt>いた</rt></ruby>す = humble II) |

## Humble vs. Respectful — Avoid the Confusion {#vs-respectful}

A common learner error is using humble forms to describe the superior's action, or respectful forms for oneself. The rule is absolute:

| | <ruby>謙譲<rt>けんじょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> (Humble) | <ruby>尊敬<rt>そんけい</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> (Respectful) |
|--|-----------------|---------------------|
| **Whose action?** | **Speaker's own action** (or in-group's) | **The respected person's action** |
| **Effect** | Lowers the speaker | Elevates the other person |
| **Example: いる** | <ruby>私<rt>わたくし</rt></ruby>が**おります** (I am here, humbly) | <ruby>先生<rt>せんせい</rt></ruby>が**いらっしゃいます** (The teacher is here, respectfully) |
| **Example: <ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>す** | <ruby>私<rt>わたくし</rt></ruby>が**お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>しする** (I speak, humbly) | <ruby>先生<rt>せんせい</rt></ruby>が**お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>しになる** (The teacher speaks, respectfully) |
| **Example: <ruby>見<rt>み</rt></ruby>る** | <ruby>私<rt>わたくし</rt></ruby>が**<ruby>拝見<rt>はいけん</rt></ruby>する** (I look, humbly) | <ruby>先生<rt>せんせい</rt></ruby>が**ご<ruby>覧<rt>らん</rt></ruby>になる** (The teacher looks, respectfully) |

!!! failure "Deadly error"
    <ruby>先生<rt>せんせい</rt></ruby>が**お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>しします**。

    ~お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>しする is humble — it can only describe the speaker's own action. Using it for the teacher's action is grammatically wrong and socially bizarre — you've lowered the person you meant to elevate.~

!!! success "Correct"
    <ruby>先生<rt>せんせい</rt></ruby>が**お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>しになります**。

    ~お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>しになる is respectful — it elevates the teacher's action.~

## Extra-Modest in Action — Real-Life Scenarios {#real-life}

### Scenario 1: Answering the Phone

> はい、<ruby>田中<rt>たなか</rt></ruby><ruby>商事<rt>しょうじ</rt></ruby>で**ございます**。<ruby>私<rt>わたくし</rt></ruby>、<ruby>佐藤<rt>さとう</rt></ruby>と**<ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>します**。いつも**お<ruby>世話<rt>せわ</rt></ruby>になっております**。

- ございます = extra-modest for です (there is / it is)
- <ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>します = humble II for <ruby>言<rt>い</rt></ruby>う (I am called)
- お<ruby>世話<rt>せわ</rt></ruby>になっております = humble II of <ruby>世話<rt>せわ</rt></ruby>になる (to be in someone's care); おる is the base

Every element is <ruby>謙譲<rt>けんじょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> II — there's no specific superior being targeted; the speaker is lowering the entire self-presentation to show formal politeness to the caller.

### Scenario 2: In a Meeting with a Client

> <ruby>資料<rt>しりょう</rt></ruby>を**ご<ruby>覧<rt>らん</rt></ruby>ください**。<ruby>私<rt>わたくし</rt></ruby>から**ご<ruby>説明<rt>せつめい</rt></ruby><ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>し<ruby>上<rt>あ</rt></ruby>げます**。

- ご<ruby>覧<rt>らん</rt></ruby>ください = respectful request (please look) — elevates the client's action
- ご<ruby>説明<rt>せつめい</rt></ruby><ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>し<ruby>上<rt>あ</rt></ruby>げる = <ruby>尊敬<rt>そんけい</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby>? No, it's humble: ご<ruby>説明<rt>せつめい</rt></ruby> (humble prefix) + <ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>し<ruby>上<rt>あ</rt></ruby>げる (humble I of <ruby>言<rt>ゆ</rt></ruby>う — I will humbly explain to you). The action is directed at the client, so <ruby>謙譲<rt>けんじょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> I is used.

### Scenario 3: Talking About Your Own Whereabouts

> <ruby>午後<rt>ごご</rt></ruby>はオフィスに**おります**ので、いつでも**お<ruby>呼<rt>よ</rt></ruby>びください**。

- おります = humble II for いる. The speaker states their own location modestly.
- お<ruby>呼<rt>よ</rt></ruby>びください = respectful request — "please call me." Elevates the listener's action.

## The お・ご + Verb Stem + する Pattern — Full Conjugation {#conjugation}

Once you form the humble stem with お／ご, you treat the whole thing as a する-verb. This includes the 〜てある combination.

| Form | Example (お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>しする) | Meaning |
|------|---------------------|---------|
| Dictionary | お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>しする | To humbly speak |
| Polite | お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>しします | I humbly speak |
| Negative | お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>ししない | I do not humbly speak |
| Polite Negative | お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>ししません | I do not humbly speak (polite) |
| Past | お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>しした | I humbly spoke |
| Te-form | お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>しして | (connective) |
| 〜てある | お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>ししてある／お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>ししてあります | I have humbly spoken (and the state is in place) |
| 〜ておく | お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>ししておく | I will humbly speak in advance |
| 〜ている | お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>ししている | I am humbly speaking (rare; but possible) |

## Common Mistakes {#common-mistakes}

### 1. Using お・ご + stem + する for someone else's action

!!! failure "Incorrect"
    <ruby>社長<rt>しゃちょう</rt></ruby>が<ruby>私<rt>わたし</rt></ruby>に**ご<ruby>説明<rt>せつめい</rt></ruby>する**。

    ~ご<ruby>説明<rt>せつめい</rt></ruby>する is humble — it can only describe the speaker's (or in-group's) action. The president cannot "humbly explain" to you using this form.~

!!! success "Correct"
    <ruby>社長<rt>しゃちょう</rt></ruby>が<ruby>私<rt>わたし</rt></ruby>に**ご<ruby>説明<rt>せつめい</rt></ruby>になる**／**<ruby>説明<rt>せつめい</rt></ruby>してくださる**。

    ~Use respectful forms (ご〜になる) or giving verbs (〜てくださる) for the president's action.~

### 2. Mixing up お and ご

!!! failure "Incorrect"
    お<ruby>説明<rt>せつめい</rt></ruby>する

    ~<ruby>説明<rt>せつめい</rt></ruby> is a Sino-Japanese word (kango). It takes ご, not お.~

!!! success "Correct"
    **ご**<ruby>説明<rt>せつめい</rt></ruby>する

    ~General rule: お for native Japanese words, ご for Sino-Japanese words. Exceptions exist (お<ruby>電話<rt>でんわ</rt></ruby>, お<ruby>料理<rt>りょうり</rt></ruby>), but the rule holds most of the time.~

### 3. Overusing <ruby>謙譲<rt>けんじょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> II in everyday speech

おります and <ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>します are formal. Using them with close friends or in casual settings sounds stiff or joking (unless you're being ironically formal). Reserve them for business, customer service, and formal occasions.

## Japanese Mindset — Self-Lowering as Social Glue {#japanese-mindset}

The very existence of <ruby>謙譲<rt>けんじょう</rt></ruby><ruby>語<rt>ご</rt></ruby> II — a layer of language that lowers the speaker without any specific target for that lowering — reveals a deep Japanese cultural value: **self-effacement as a default mode of formality**. You don't just lower yourself when interacting with a superior; you lower yourself *whenever you speak formally in public*, as a way of acknowledging the formality of the situation itself.

- **おる is not just a humble いる — it is a posture.** Saying <ruby>私<rt>わたくし</rt></ruby>はここにおります rather than <ruby>私<rt>わたくし</rt></ruby>はここにいます is the linguistic equivalent of bowing slightly as you enter a room. It signals awareness of the social context.
- **The お・ご + する pattern makes you small, but the grammar makes you clear.** The humble form acknowledges the status difference, but the rest of the sentence — especially with constructions like 〜てある — lets you communicate facts and claim responsibility cleanly. Humility doesn't mean vagueness.
- **Humble and assertive can coexist.** The reference sentence お<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>ししてあります demonstrates this perfectly: the humble form shows deference to the president, while 〜てある declares plainly that the task is done. Professional Japanese rewards this combination.
