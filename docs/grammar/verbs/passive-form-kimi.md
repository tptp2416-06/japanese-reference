# 受身形 — Passive Sentences

## 太郎が花子に泣かれた。

---

## JLPT Level {#jlpt-level}

!!! abstract "JLPT Level — N4 Core / N3 Extended"
    Direct passive formation is solid N4 — the mechanical shift from active to passive using れる/られる. But the true depth of Japanese passive voice lies in N3: the suffering passive (迷惑の受身), intransitive passive (不及物動詞の受身), agent marker calibration (に・から・によって), and the three-way ambiguity of られる (passive / potential / honorific). This page covers the full N4–N3 passive landscape.

    | Tier | Pattern | Description | Example |
    |------|---------|-------------|---------|
    | N5 — Foundation | Active sentences | Subject performs action | 太郎が花子を殴った。 |
    | N4 — Foundation | 〜てもらう | Receiving benefit (active frame) | 太郎が花子に助けてもらった。 |
    | **N4 — Core ← You are here** | **直接被身** | **Direct passive — subject receives action** | **太郎が花子に殴られた。** |
    | N4 — Core | 尊敬語としての受身 | Honorific passive — elevates subject | 社長が来られた。 |
    | N3 — Extended | **間接受身 / 迷惑の受身** | **Suffering passive — subject adversely affected** | **太郎が花子に泣かれた。** |
    | N3 — Extended | **不及物動詞の受身** | **Intransitive passive — affected by event/phenomenon** | **雨に降られた。** |
    | N3 — Extended | **によって** | **Creator/author marker in passive** | **この本は夏目漱石によって書かれた。** |
    | N3 — Extended | 使役受身 | Causative-passive (forced to do) | 親に勉強させられた。 |
    | N2 — Awareness | ら抜き言葉 | Spoken disambiguation of られる | 食べれる (potential, spoken) |
    | N2 — Awareness | 受身文の間接話法 | Passive as indirect speech / evidential | 彼は天才だと言われている。 |

## Quick Summary {#quick-summary}

!!! tip "At a glance"
    - Japanese passive (受身形) is not merely "be + past participle." It is a **perspective shift** that places the speaker or a third party at the center of an action they did not initiate.
    - **直接被身 (direct passive):** The logical object of an active sentence becomes the grammatical subject. 花子が太郎を殴った → 太郎が花子に殴られた。
    - **迷惑の受身 (suffering passive):** The subject is **adversely affected** by someone else's action — even actions that do not physically touch them. 太郎が花子に泣かれた = "Taro suffered Hanako's crying." This is the most culturally distinctive Japanese passive type.
    - **不及物動詞の受身 (intransitive passive):** Natural phenomena or intransitive events are passivized to express personal affliction. 雨に降られた = "I was rained on (and it bothered me)."
    - **尊敬語としての受身 (honorific passive):** The passive form elevates the subject's action. 社長が来られた = "The president came [honorific]."
    - **Agent markers:** に for persons (most common), から for intransitive agents or organizational sources, によって for creators/authors/inventors.
    - **The られる ambiguity:** At N3, learners must confront that 食べられる can mean "is eaten" (passive), "can eat" (potential), or "(honorable person) eats" (honorific). Context and particles disambiguate.

## Core Meaning {#core-meaning}

!!! note "Core Rule"
    Use 受身形 when you want to shift the narrative camera from the person doing the action to the person affected by it — whether as recipient, sufferer, or honored figure. The passive voice in Japanese encodes social geometry: who is at the center, and who is orbiting them.

| 太郎が花子に殴られた。 | Taro was hit by Hanako [direct passive]. |
|---|---|
| | *Context: The camera is on Taro. Hanako remains in the frame as agent (に), but the grammatical subject — the point of empathy — is Taro.* |

| 太郎が花子に泣かれた。 | Taro was cried-on by Hanako [suffering passive]. |
|---|---|
| | *Context: Hanako cried. Taro did not receive the tears physically, but he suffered the social/emotional consequence of her crying. The passive makes Taro the experiential center.* |

| 雨に降られて、濡れてしまった。 | I got rained on and ended up soaked [intransitive passive]. |
|---|---|
| | *Context: 降る is intransitive — rain falls, it has no object. But passivized, it expresses the speaker's adverse encounter with the rain.* |

| 社長がもうお帰りになられた。 | The president has already returned [honorific passive]. |
|---|---|
| | *Context: The passive form elevates the president's action. Alternative to お帰りになった, carrying a slightly more formal/written tone.* |

## The Three Functions of られる {#the-three-functions-of-rareru}

At N3, every learner hits the same wall: 食べられる looks like one form but carries three distinct social functions. Understanding this ambiguity is essential for both comprehension and production.

| Function | Meaning | Context Clues | Example |
|----------|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Passive** | "is eaten" | Subject is non-volitional; agent marked by に | このケーキは子供に食べられた。 |
| **Potential** | "can eat" | Subject is volitional; no agent; object marked by を/が | 私は納豆を食べられる。 |
| **Honorific** | "(someone honorable) eats" | Subject is high-status person; no agent | 社長はもうお昼を食べられたそうです。 |

!!! note "How native speakers disambiguate in real time"
    - **Passive vs. potential:** If there is an agent marked by に, it is passive. If the subject is a high-status person and the context is formal, it is honorific. If the subject is the speaker and there is no agent, it is potential.
    - **Spoken workaround:** In casual speech, potential られる often drops the ら (食べれる, 見れる). This ら抜き is widespread in spoken Japanese and serves as an instant disambiguator — though it is not accepted in formal writing.

!!! warning "The ambiguity trap"
    先生が食べられた。
    - Passive reading: "The teacher was eaten [by something]."
    - Honorific reading: "The teacher ate [something]."
    - Without context, this is genuinely ambiguous. In writing, surrounding sentences resolve it. In conversation, intonation and shared knowledge resolve it immediately.

## The Four Passive Types {#the-four-passive-types}

### 1. Direct Passive (直接被身)

The logical object of the active sentence becomes the grammatical subject. The agent, if mentioned, is marked by に.

**Active:** 花子が太郎を殴った。  
**Passive:** 太郎が花子に殴られた。

| 本が多くの人に読まれている。 | The book is being read by many people. |
|---|---|
| | *Context: Neutral, objective statement. The book is the center of attention.* |

| 彼は上司に褒められた。 | He was praised by his boss. |
|---|---|
| | *Context: Direct passive with positive valence. Unlike suffering passive, this can be neutral or good.* |

| 窓が誰かに開けられた。 | The window was opened by someone. |
|---|---|
| | *Context: Agent unknown or unimportant. Passive allows omitting or obscuring the actor.* |

!!! note "Direct passive and adversity"
    Even direct passive often carries a hint of affectedness in Japanese. 彼に秘密を話された ("I was told a secret by him") can imply the speaker was burdened by the information. English passive is often neutral; Japanese passive tends to place the subject in an experiential position that can feel slightly vulnerable.

### 2. Suffering Passive (迷惑の受身 / 間接受身)

The subject is **adversely affected** by the agent's action. The action may be transitive or intransitive; the key is that the subject suffers a consequence.

**Transitive suffering:** The subject "loses" something or endures an imposition.  
**Intransitive suffering:** The subject endures a natural or social event.

| 太郎が花子に財布を盗まれた。 | Taro had his wallet stolen by Hanako [transitive suffering]. |
|---|---|
| | *Context: The wallet is the direct object. Taro is the subject suffering the loss. This is "indirect" because Taro is not the direct target of the verb — the wallet is — but grammatically he is the subject.* |

| 電車の中で隣の人にタバコを吸われた。 | On the train, the person next to me smoked [and I suffered it]. |
|---|---|
| | *Context: The smoke invaded the speaker's space. The passive makes the speaker the experiential center of the annoyance.* |

| 子供に泣かれて、眠れなかった。 | I couldn't sleep because a child cried [on me / near me]. |
|---|---|
| | *Context: 泣く is intransitive. The child cried; the speaker suffered. This is the classic "intransitive suffering" pattern that English cannot replicate with passive voice.* |

| 父に死なれて、一人になった。 | My father died on me, and I was left alone. |
|---|---|
| | *Context: 死ぬ is intransitive. The speaker frames their bereavement as a personal affliction. The passive externalizes the event as something that happened *to* the survivor.* |

!!! info "Why Japanese needs suffering passive"
    English handles these with circumlocutions: "I had my wallet stolen," "I had to listen to a crying child," "I lost my father." Japanese wraps the adversity into a single verbal morphology. The suffering passive is not a grammatical curiosity — it is a **social emotion tool**. It allows the speaker to state that an event was personally burdensome without directly confronting the agent.

### 3. Intransitive Passive (不及物動詞の受身)

A subset of suffering passive where the verb is strictly intransitive and the agent is often a natural force, an organization, or an inanimate source. The subject is caught in the event's crossfire.

| 雨に降られて、濡れてしまった。 | I got rained on and ended up soaked. |
|---|---|
| | *Context: 降る has no direct object. Passivizing it creates a personal weather disaster.* |

| 電車に遅れられて、困った。 | I was inconvenienced by the train being late. |
|---|---|
| | *Context: 遅れる is intransitive. The train's lateness becomes a personal affliction.* |

| 隣の家から煙が出られて、目が痛い。 | Smoke came from the neighbor's house and my eyes hurt. |
|---|---|
| | *Context: 出る is intransitive. The から marks the source; the passive marks the speaker's adverse experience.* |

!!! warning "に vs. から with intransitive passive"
    Persons acting on the subject take に: 子供に泣かれた. Sources, organizations, or spatial origins often take から: 隣の家から煙が出られた. But the boundary is fluid, and に is often acceptable with intransitive agents too.

### 4. Honorific Passive (尊敬語としての受身)

The passive form elevates the subject's action as a respectful alternative to お/ご〜になる. It is common with motion verbs and everyday actions.

| 先生が来られた。 | The teacher came [honorific]. |
|---|---|
| | *Context: 来られる is the honorific form of 来る. Slightly more formal than おいでになった in some contexts.* |

| 部長がもうお帰りになられた。 | The department head has already returned [honorific]. |
|---|---|
| | *Context: Double elevation — お帰りになる is already respectful; adding passive なられる makes it ultra-formal. Some native speakers find this excessive (二重敬語), but it persists in conservative business settings.* |

| 社長は何とおっしゃいましたか。→ 社長は何と言われましたか。 | What did the president say? [honorific passive variant] |
|---|---|
| | *Context: 言われる as honorific for 言う. Less common than おっしゃる but used in some formal writing.* |

!!! note "Honorific passive vs. お/ご〜になる"
    Both elevate the subject. お/ご〜になる is the standard N4 respectful form (e.g., お帰りになる). The honorific passive (帰られる, 来られる) is grammatically simpler but can sound either very formal or slightly old-fashioned, depending on the verb. With 来る and 帰る, it is natural. With 食べる, 食べられる as honorific is less common than 召し上がる.

## Agent Markers {#agent-markers}

Japanese passive sentences mark the agent with three distinct particles. Choosing the wrong one immediately signals non-native production.

| Particle | Usage | Example | Nuance |
|----------|-------|---------|--------|
| **に** | Standard agent marker for persons in direct and suffering passive | 太郎が花子に殴られた。 | Neutral, most common |
| **に** | Agent for intransitive verbs (also common) | 雨に降られた。 | Natural force as agent |
| **から** | Source/starting point; organizations; intransitive agents | 隣の家から煙が出られた。 | Emphasizes spatial or institutional origin |
| **から** | Agent with verbs of giving/receiving in passive frame | 友達から助けられた。 | Slightly more emphatic than に |
| **によって** | Creator, author, inventor, designer | この建物は安藤忠雄によって設計された。 | Objective, intellectual attribution |
| **によって** | Means/method in some passive contexts (rare) | 新技術によって作られた。 | "Made by means of new technology" |

!!! warning "Do not use を for the agent"
    !!! failure "Incorrect"
        太郎が花子を殴られた。
    !!! success "Correct"
        太郎が花子に殴られた。

    ~を marks the direct object in active sentences. In passive sentences, the direct object becomes the subject (が), and the agent takes に. Using を for the agent is a direct transfer error from English "by."~

!!! note "によって for objective distance"
    に and から imply personal involvement. によって creates objective, almost scholarly distance. A newspaper uses 警察に逮捕された (arrested by police) for immediacy, but この法は議会によって可決された (this law was passed by parliament) for institutional neutrality.

## In Real Life {#in-real-life}

!!! example "In Real Life — Suffering Passive"
    **Situation:** ユウタ is on a crowded evening train, trying to review documents for a morning meeting. A middle-aged man sits next to him and immediately lights a cigarette — illegal on the train, but the man is large and unapproachable. Yuta texts his colleague.

    &gt; 電車の中で隣の人にタバコを吸われて、資料が臭くなった。

    *"On the train, the guy next to me smoked, and my documents got stinky."*

    **Why the passive fits:** Yuta could have said 隣の人がタバコを吸っていた — a neutral active observation. But that would place the smoker at the center. The passive 吸われて places Yuta at the center and encodes the experience as an imposition. The grammar does the complaining so Yuta doesn't have to confront the man directly.

    **What makes this natural:** The suffering passive is the standard grammar for public-space annoyance in Japan. It externalizes the grievance into syntax rather than interpersonal conflict.

!!! example "In Real Life — Intransitive Passive"
    **Situation:** マリ is walking to an interview without an umbrella. The weather forecast said sunny. It suddenly pours. She arrives at the building lobby, dripping, and speaks to the receptionist.

    &gt; 雨に降られちゃって、こんな格好になっちゃいました。

    *"I got rained on and ended up looking like this."*

    **Why 降られる fits:** 雨が降った is a meteorological fact. 雨に降られた is a personal mishap. The passive transforms weather into adversity. The ちゃって adds helplessness; the こんな格好になっちゃいました adds embarrassed self-deprecation.

    **What から would change:** 雨から降られた is ungrammatical/unnatural. Natural forces acting directly on the person take に. から is reserved for spatial sources (隣から水が漏れた → 隣から水が漏られて).

!!! example "In Real Life — Honorific Passive"
    **Situation:** Two office workers are preparing a conference room. The department head is expected. One worker notices the door opening.

    &gt; 部長が来られましたよ。

    *"The department head has arrived."*

    **Why the honorific passive fits:** 来ました is neutral. おいでになりました is standard respectful. 来られました is a respectful alternative that sounds slightly more formal and written. In a business where おいでになる feels too servile, 来られる hits a middle register of respectful objectivity.

    **The ambiguity check:** Could this mean "The department head was brought by someone"? In this context, no — there is no agent marked by に, and the subject is clearly a high-status person in motion. Context resolves the られる ambiguity instantly.

## Grammar Analysis {#grammar-analysis}

### Function in Context

- 太郎が花子に泣かれた: 太郎 is the subject (が), the experiencer. 花子 is the agent (に), the source of the action. 泣かれた is the passive of 泣く. The verb is intransitive, so there is no direct object — yet the passive form creates a subject affected by the action.
- 本が夏目漱石によって書かれた: 本 is the subject (が). 夏目漱石 is the creator (によって). 書かれた is the passive of 書く. The に particle would sound too personal/intimate for a historical author; によって provides objective distance.
- 社長が来られた: 社長 is the subject (が) and also the semantic agent (the one who came). There is no に-marked agent because this is honorific passive — the subject performs the action, and the grammar elevates them.

## Japanese Mindset {#japanese-mindset}

The Japanese passive is not a syntactic decoration — it is a **social emotion technology**.

- **Adversity without confrontation.** The suffering passive allows the speaker to say "I was negatively affected" without saying "You did something bad." 隣の人にギターを弾かれた complains about the neighbor without accusing them directly. The grammar absorbs the conflict.
- **Experiential centrality.** Japanese grammar often forces the speaker to choose whose perspective matters. Active sentences center the agent. Passive sentences center the experiencer. This choice is never neutral — it is a moral alignment.
- **Honorific passive and hierarchical distance.** Using passive for honorifics (来られる) creates distance by grammatically "displacing" the subject. The subject is both the actor and the grammatically elevated figure — a linguistic paradox that produces respectful elevation.
- **Natural phenomena as personal events.** 雨に降られる treats rain not as meteorology but as personal misfortune. This reflects a cultural tendency to see the self as embedded in environmental events rather than standing apart from them.
- **The bystander as subject.** In 電車の中で子供に泣かれた, the speaker is an innocent bystander who becomes the grammatical subject. This is impossible in English passive grammar. Japanese allows the uninvolved sufferer to claim narrative centrality.

## Nuance Comparison {#nuance-comparison}

| Grammar Point | Core Meaning | Subject Stance | Agent Marked | Adversity | JLPT |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 受身形 (直接被身) | Subject receives action | Non-volitional recipient | に (person) | Neutral or negative | N4 |
| 迷惑の受身 | Subject suffers from agent's action | Adversely affected | に (person) | Strongly negative | N3 |
| 不及物動詞の受身 | Subject affected by event/phenomenon | Victim of circumstance | に・から | Negative | N3 |
| 尊敬語としての受身 | Subject's action is elevated | Honored | None | Neutral (formal) | N4 |
| 〜てもらう | Receiving benefit (active) | Grateful recipient | に | Positive | N4 |
| 〜てくれる | Someone does for me (active) | Grateful recipient | が | Positive | N4 |
| 使役 (〜させる) | Making/letting someone do | Controlling | に・を | Neutral | N4 |
| お/ご〜になる | Respectful form | Honored | None | Neutral | N4 |
| 〜られる (potential) | Can do | Capable | None | Neutral | N4 |

**Passive vs. 〜てもらう:** Both involve someone else doing something. 助けてもらった = "I received help" (grateful, active frame). 助けられた = "I was helped" (neutral or passive frame). The emotional valence is opposite.

**Passive vs. 使役:** 親に勉強させられた (causative-passive, N3) = "I was forced to study by my parents." This is not passive voice — it is causative + passive combined. It appears in the lineage but is a separate grammatical beast.

**Honorific passive vs. お/ご〜になる:** Both elevate. お帰りになる is standard, warm, service-industry. 帰られる is cooler, more formal, slightly bureaucratic. A hotel might use お帰りになる for guests, but a government office might use 帰られる in a memo.

## Register and Formality {#register-and-formality}

=== ":material-chat-outline: Casual"
    隣のやつにギター弾かれて、うぜえ。

    *"The neighbor's playing guitar on me — annoying."*

    **Shift:** Suffering passive with slang. やつ for agent, うぜえ for emotional reaction. The passive does the complaining; the vocabulary does the venting.

=== ":material-chat-outline: Casual (softened)"
    雨に降られちゃった。

    *"Got rained on."*

    **Shift:** ちゃった adds helplessness and slight cuteness. Standard casual adversity.

=== ":material-tie: Basic Polite"
    電車の中で隣の方にタバコを吸われて、困りました。

    *"On the train, the person next to me smoked, and I was troubled."*

    **Shift:** 方 elevates the agent slightly; 困りました is polite but honest. The suffering passive remains the core complaint structure.

=== ":material-briefcase-outline: Extra Polite"
    会議中に社長に来られて、話が止まってしまいました。

    *"In the middle of the meeting, the president came [honorific], and our conversation stopped."*

    **Shift:** Honorific passive for the president (来られて). The speaker is not suffering from the president's arrival in a negative sense, but the grammar acknowledges the president's action as an event that disrupted the speaker's space. This is a very Japanese register — respectful and slightly self-deprecating.

=== ":material-briefcase-outline: Extra Polite (written / objective)"
    この小説は昭和の作家によって書かれた。

    *"This novel was written by a Showa-era author."*

    **Shift:** によって for creator attribution. Written, objective, academic. No emotion, no adversity — pure informational passive.

## Grammar Lineage {#grammar-lineage}

| Step | Pattern | What It Adds | Example |

## Examples {#examples}

| 太郎が花子に殴られた。 | Taro was hit by Hanako [direct passive]. |
|---|---|
| | *Context: Neutral report of physical conflict. Perspective is on Taro.* |

| 太郎が花子に泣かれた。 | Taro was cried-on by Hanako [suffering passive]. |
|---|---|
| | *Context: Hanako cried; Taro suffered the social/emotional fallout. Intransitive verb in passive frame.* |

| 雨に降られて、風邪を引いた。 | I got rained on and caught a cold [intransitive passive]. |
|---|---|
| | *Context: Personal weather disaster. The rain is the agent; the speaker is the afflicted subject.* |

| 社長がもうお帰りになられた。 | The president has already returned [honorific passive]. |
|---|---|
| | *Context: Ultra-formal business setting. Passive form elevates the president's departure.* |

| 隣の家から騒音が出られて、眠れない。 | Noise comes from the neighbor's house and I can't sleep [intransitive passive]. |
|---|---|
| | *Context: 出る is intransitive; から marks the spatial source. The speaker is the sufferer.* |

| 彼は上司に褒められた。 | He was praised by his boss [direct passive, positive]. |
|---|---|
| | *Context: Direct passive is not always negative. Praise is a welcome reception of action.* |

| 電車の中で子供に足を踏まれた。 | On the train, a child stepped on my foot [suffering passive]. |
|---|---|
| | *Context: The foot (足) is the direct object; the speaker is the subject. A classic transitive suffering passive.* |

| 友達に来られて、勉強ができなかった。 | A friend came over [to my place] and I couldn't study [suffering/honorific ambiguity]. |
|---|---|
| | *Context: Without further context, this could be suffering ("friend invaded my space") or honorific ("my honored friend came"). In casual peer speech, it is almost always suffering — the friend dropped by unannounced.* |

| この映画は有名な監督によって作られた。 | This film was made by a famous director [objective passive]. |
|---|---|
| | *Context: によって for creator attribution. Neutral, informational, written style.* |

| 知らない人に名前を呼ばれて、驚いた。 | I was surprised when a stranger called my name [direct passive]. |
|---|---|
| | *Context: Direct passive with emotional reaction. The surprise is the speaker's response to being acted upon.* |

## Common Mistakes {#common-mistakes}

### 1. Using を for the agent

!!! failure "Agent particle error"
    太郎が花子を殴られた。

    ~を marks the direct object. In passive, the direct object becomes the subject (が), and the agent takes に. This sentence would mean "Taro was hit, and Hanako was somehow the object of that hitting" — ungrammatical.~

!!! success "Correct"
    太郎が花子に殴られた。

### 2. Confusing passive られる with potential られる

!!! failure "Ambiguity error"
    私は納豆を食べられた。

    ~Without context, this is parsed as passive: "I was eaten by natto" (nonsense) or "Natto was eaten by me" (wrong particle). If the speaker means potential ("I can eat natto"), the object should be marked by が in many contexts, or the sentence should use ら抜き (食べれる) in casual speech.~

!!! success "Correct (potential)"
    私は納豆が食べられる。 / 私は納豆を食べれる。

!!! success "Correct (passive)"
    納豆は私に食べられた。 (Natto was eaten by me.)

### 3. Using honorific passive for first person

!!! failure "Social direction error"
    私が来られた。

    ~This means either "I was come [by someone]" (nonsense) or attempts to use passive as honorific for oneself. Honorific passive points upward at others. Using it for yourself is a category error — like bowing to yourself.~

!!! success "Correct"
    私が来ました。 (I came — neutral.)
    社長が来られました。 (The president came — honorific.)

### 4. Overusing suffering passive for neutral events

!!! failure "Adversity overloading"
    彼に助けられて、嬉しかった。

    ~Grammatically fine, but 助けてもらって is the natural choice for grateful reception. Using passive for help received makes it sound like the help was an imposition.~

!!! success "Correct"
    彼に助けてもらって、嬉しかった。 (I received help from him and was happy.)

### 5. Using に for creator/agent when によって is required

!!! failure "Register mismatch"
    この本は夏目漱石に書かれた。

    ~に is too personal/intimate for a historical creator. It sounds like "This book was written for Natsume Soseki" or implies a personal relationship.~

!!! success "Correct"
    この本は夏目漱石によって書かれた。

### 6. Forgetting that intransitive verbs need passive for "affected" meaning

!!! failure "Active frame error"
    雨が降って、濡れた。

    ~Grammatically correct but semantically odd. It sounds like the rain got wet. The speaker should be the subject of the wetness, framed through the rain's action: 雨に降られて、濡れた.~

!!! success "Correct"
    雨に降られて、濡れてしまった。

## Hidden Points {#hidden-points}

- **The suffering passive is the grammar of Japanese complaint culture.** 隣に住まれる, 客に逃げられる, and 子供に泣かれる are not literary flourishes — they are daily emotional tools. The passive lets the speaker say "this happened to me" without saying "someone wronged me."
- **Passive sentences often omit the agent.** 財布を盗まれた (My wallet was stolen) is a complete sentence without に. The agent is unknown or unimportant. English requires "by someone"; Japanese simply drops it.
- **The られる ambiguity creates genuine comedy.** 先生が食べられた, without context, is a perfect linguistic prank — it could mean the teacher was eaten or the teacher ate. Native speakers exploit this ambiguity for humor.
- **Honorific passive with いる/ある is impossible.** Stative verbs of existence do not passivize. You cannot say 社長がいられました to mean "The president was [honorific]." You must use おいでになりました or いらっしゃいました.
- **Some transitive verbs resist suffering passive.** Verbs that inherently require the subject's consent or active participation (勉強する, 考える, 信じる) sound odd in suffering passive because the subject cannot be a passive victim of someone else's studying or thinking.
- **によって can mark method as well as creator.** 新技術によって治された can mean "cured by new technology" (method) or "cured by means of new technology." Context disambiguates.
- **The passive is used in scientific Japanese to create objectivity.** 実験によって確認された ("It was confirmed by the experiment") removes the researcher from the sentence, making the finding sound like a natural discovery rather than a human achievement.
- **Suffering passive with family members is emotionally complex.** 父に死なれた carries not just grief but a hint of abandonment — the father "did" dying to the survivor. This is culturally specific; English "I lost my father" centers the speaker's action, while Japanese centers the father's action and the speaker's affliction.
- **Direct passive in headlines is standard.** 男が警察に逮捕された (Man arrested by police) is the standard news headline structure. Active headlines (警察が男を逮捕した) sound editorialialized; passive sounds factual.
- **The passive form of 分かる (分かられる) exists but is vanishingly rare.** Because 分かる is a stative verb of cognition, passivizing it is almost impossible in natural speech. If you need "was understood by," use 理解された.

## Learner Stage {#learner-stage}

| Stage | What It Looks Like |
|-------|-------------------|
| **Now — N4** | Form direct passive correctly with godan and ichidan verbs; use に for agent; distinguish passive from active; produce basic honorific passive (来られる, 帰られる) |
| **Developing — N4→N3** | Deploy suffering passive naturally for complaints and adversity; use intransitive passive for weather and events; calibrate に vs. から vs. によって; recognize られる ambiguity in context |
| **Automatic — N3→N2** | Passive forms arrive as perspective choices, not mechanical transformations. You hear 隣に住まれる and immediately register "complaint about neighbors." You choose passive not because the textbook says so, but because you want to center the experiencer. |
| **Mastery** | You control the full passive spectrum with precision — using direct passive for neutral/objective statements, suffering passive for adversity without confrontation, honorific passive for upward elevation, and によって for creator attribution. You never confuse passive られる with potential in production, and you know when 〜てもらう is the emotionally correct choice over passive. |
