# Relative Clause vs. Nominalization
## Verb + Noun and Verb + の

---

## 1. The Core Distinction

Japanese presents two ways for a verb to interact with a noun that follows it. They look deceptively similar to English speakers because both involve a verb placed before a noun-like element. The difference is structural and fundamental.

| Pattern | Form | What Happens |
|---|---|---|
| **Relative Clause** | [Verb] + **Noun** | The verb describes, narrows down, or classifies the noun. The noun is a real, independent word. |
| **Nominalization** | [Verb] + **の** | The verb becomes a noun concept. の is a grammatical tool, not a real noun. |

**The critical visual cue:** If the word after the verb is a content noun (intention, student, book, job), the verb is modifying it. If the word after the verb is の, the verb has been turned into a noun.

---

## 2. The Anchor Sentence

&gt; ごめんね、ハルを…<ruby>泣<rt>な</rt></ruby>かせるつもりじゃなかったんだ。

**Pattern used:** Relative clause
**Modifying clause:** ハルを<ruby>泣<rt>な</rt></ruby>かせる (make Haru cry)
**Head noun:** つもり (intention)
**Sentence core:** [that intention] + じゃなかったんだ (it wasn't, you see)

**Natural meaning:** "Sorry, I didn't mean to make Haru cry."
**Literal architecture:** "[Haru-make-cry] intention was-not."

The verb phrase <ruby>泣<rt>な</rt></ruby>かせる sits directly in front of つもり with no particle and no の in between. This is the signature of a relative clause: the verb modifies the noun that follows it.

---

## 3. Pattern A — Relative Clause: Verb Modifies Noun

In this pattern, the noun is a **genuine lexical item**. It exists in the world as a person, place, thing, time, or abstract concept. The verb phrase in front acts like a giant adjective, telling you which one or what kind.

### Structural Properties
- The verb is in **plain form** (non-past, past, negative, or any derived form like causative or passive).
- There is **nothing** between the verb and the noun—no particle, no の.
- The noun often has a semantic relationship to the verb: it can be the doer, the undergoer, the time, the place, or the **content**.

### The Content Relationship (Key for This Example)
With nouns like つもり、<ruby>予定<rt>よてい</rt></ruby>、<ruby>計画<rt>けいかく</rt></ruby>、<ruby>夢<rt>ゆめ</rt></ruby>、or <ruby>習慣<rt>しゅうかん</rt></ruby>, the noun is a **container** for an action. The relative clause fills the "content" slot of that container.

- **つもり** = an intention. Intentions contain actions.
- **<ruby>泣<rt>な</rt></ruby>かせるつもり** = an intention whose content is "making someone cry."

This is different from a noun that participates in the action:

| Relationship | Example | Gap Role |
|---|---|---|
| Agent | <ruby>本<rt>ほん</rt></ruby>を<ruby>読<rt>よ</rt></ruby>む<ruby>学生<rt>がくせい</rt></ruby> | <ruby>学生<rt>がくせい</rt></ruby> performs <ruby>読<rt>よ</rt></ruby>む |
| Object | <ruby>母<rt>はは</rt></ruby>が<ruby>作<rt>つく</rt></ruby>る<ruby>料理<rt>りょうり</rt></ruby> | <ruby>料理<rt>りょうり</rt></ruby> is produced by <ruby>作<rt>つく</rt></ruby>る |
| Time | <ruby>彼<rt>かれ</rt></ruby>に<ruby>会<rt>あ</rt></ruby>う<ruby>日<rt>ひ</rt></ruby> | <ruby>日<rt>ひ</rt></ruby> is when <ruby>会<rt>あ</rt></ruby>う happens |
| Content | <ruby>泣<rt>な</rt></ruby>かせるつもり | つもり contains <ruby>泣<rt>な</rt></ruby>かせる as its content |

### Additional Relative Clause Examples
- <ruby>日本語<rt>にほんご</rt></ruby>を<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>す<ruby>機会<rt>きかい</rt></ruby> (opportunity to speak Japanese)
- <ruby>子供<rt>こども</rt></ruby>を<ruby>驚<rt>おどろ</rt></ruby>かせる<ruby>話<rt>はなし</rt></ruby> (story that surprises children)
- <ruby>自分<rt>じぶん</rt></ruby>で<ruby>決<rt>き</rt></ruby>める<ruby>問題<rt>もんだい</rt></ruby> (problem that one decides oneself)
- <ruby>終<rt>お</rt></ruby>わらせないとマズい<ruby>仕事<rt>しごと</rt></ruby> (job that must be finished)

## 4. Pattern B — Nominalization: Verb + の

In this pattern, **の is not a real noun**. It is a grammatical nominalizer that converts the entire verb phrase into a noun-like entity. This entity can then serve as a subject, object, topic, or any other nominal role in the sentence.

### Structural Properties
- の follows the verb in plain form.
- After の, there **must** be a particle (が, を, は, に, etc.) or a copula, because the nominalized phrase now functions as a noun and needs case marking.
- The verb phrase becomes a conceptual "thing" or "event" that can be talked about as a whole.

### Why Japanese Requires This
Japanese predicates demand nominal elements for subject and object slots. You cannot say:

- \* <ruby>本<rt>ほん</rt></ruby>を<ruby>読<rt>よ</rt></ruby>むが<ruby>好<rt>す</rt></ruby>き (ungrammatical)
- \* ハルを<ruby>泣<rt>な</rt></ruby>かせるは<ruby>悪<rt>わる</rt></ruby>い (ungrammatical)

The verb must be "nounified" before it can occupy these slots. の performs that function.

### Nominalization Examples
- <ruby>本<rt>ほん</rt></ruby>を<ruby>読<rt>よ</rt></ruby>むのが<ruby>好<rt>す</rt></ruby>き (I like reading books)
- ハルを<ruby>泣<rt>な</rt></ruby>かせるのは<ruby>悪<rt>わる</rt></ruby>いことだ (Making Haru cry is a bad thing)
- <ruby>日本語<rt>にほんご</rt></ruby>を<ruby>話<rt>はな</rt></ruby>すのは<ruby>難<rt>むずか</rt></ruby>しい (Speaking Japanese is difficult)
- <ruby>待<rt>ま</rt></ruby>つのをやめた (I stopped waiting)

---

## 5. Why You Cannot Insert の Into a Relative Clause

A common learner error is attempting to add の between the verb and the noun, producing ungrammatical forms like \*<ruby>泣<rt>な</rt></ruby>かせるのつもり. This fails because the two patterns operate on different structural principles.

| Attempt | Result | Why It Fails |
|---|---|---|
| <ruby>泣<rt>な</rt></ruby>かせるつもり | Correct | つもり is a noun. The verb modifies it directly. |
| \* <ruby>泣<rt>な</rt></ruby>かせるのつもり | Ungrammatical | の tries to nominalize <ruby>泣<rt>な</rt></ruby>かせる first, creating a noun phrase that cannot then modify つもり without a particle. |
| <ruby>泣<rt>な</rt></ruby>かせるのがつもり | Nonsensical | Even with a particle, it would mean "The act of making cry is the intention," which is not the natural expression. |

**Rule of thumb:** If the following word is a content noun that describes an action's container (intention, plan, habit, dream), the verb modifies it directly. If you need the action itself to become a subject or object, use の.

---

## 6. Diagnostic Tests

Use these three tests to determine which pattern you are looking at.

### Test 1 — The Following Word Test
Look at the word immediately after the verb phrase.
- **Content noun** (<ruby>学生<rt>がくせい</rt></ruby>、<ruby>仕事<rt>しごと</rt></ruby>、つもり、<ruby>日<rt>ひ</rt></ruby>、<ruby>話<rt>はなし</rt></ruby>) → Relative clause.
- **の followed by a particle** → Nominalization.

### Test 2 — The Replacement Test
Replace the entire [Verb + Noun] or [Verb + の] block with a simple pronoun or demonstrative.
- Relative clause: <ruby>泣<rt>な</rt></ruby>かせるつもり → そのつもり (that intention). The noun survives independently.
- Nominalization: <ruby>泣<rt>な</rt></ruby>かせるの → それ (that thing/act). The concept survives as a nominal entity.

### Test 3 — The Particle Insertion Test
Try to insert a particle between the verb and what follows.
- Relative clause: You cannot insert anything. \*<ruby>泣<rt>な</rt></ruby>かせるがつもり is ungrammatical.
- Nominalization: の is already functioning as the nominal element. You cannot remove it: \*<ruby>本<rt>ほん</rt></ruby>を<ruby>読<rt>よ</rt></ruby>むが<ruby>好<rt>す</rt></ruby>き is ungrammatical.

## 7. Action Nouns and the Gray Area

Some nouns describe events or actions. Learners often expect these to require nominalization because they feel "verby." They do not. These nouns have a built-in semantic slot for content that is filled by a direct relative clause.

| Noun | Meaning | Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| つもり | intention | [V] + つもり | <ruby>泣<rt>な</rt></ruby>かせるつもり |
| <ruby>予定<rt>よてい</rt></ruby> | plan | [V] + <ruby>予定<rt>よてい</rt></ruby> | <ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>く<ruby>予定<rt>よてい</rt></ruby> |
| <ruby>計画<rt>けいかく</rt></ruby> | plan | [V] + <ruby>計画<rt>けいかく</rt></ruby> | <ruby>留学<rt>りゅうがく</rt></ruby>する<ruby>計画<rt>けいかく</rt></ruby> |
| <ruby>習慣<rt>しゅうかん</rt></ruby> | habit | [V] + <ruby>習慣<rt>しゅうかん</rt></ruby> | <ruby>早起<rt>はやお</rt></ruby>きする<ruby>習慣<rt>しゅうかん</rt></ruby> |
| <ruby>夢<rt>ゆめ</rt></ruby> | dream | [V] + <ruby>夢<rt>ゆめ</rt></ruby> | <ruby>世界<rt>せかい</rt></ruby>を<ruby>回<rt>まわ</rt></ruby>る<ruby>夢<rt>ゆめ</rt></ruby> |

**Contrast with nominalization:** When these same concepts need to be the **subject** or **object** of a larger sentence, the verb phrase must be nominalized with の, and the noun becomes part of the predicate.

- <ruby>留学<rt>りゅうがく</rt></ruby>する<ruby>計画<rt>けいかく</rt></ruby>がある (Relative clause: plan to study abroad exists)
- <ruby>留学<rt>りゅうがく</rt></ruby>するのが<ruby>計画<rt>けいかく</rt></ruby>だ (Nominalization: to study abroad is the plan)

## 8. Processing Order: Japanese vs. English

### Japanese Listener
1. Hears the verb phrase: ハルを<ruby>泣<rt>な</rt></ruby>かせる (make Haru cry).
2. Hears the noun: つもり (intention).
3. Assembles: "An intention whose content is making Haru cry."
4. Hears the predicate: じゃなかったんだ (it wasn't, you see).

The description piles up **before** the noun, and the sentence judgment comes at the very end.

### English Speaker
1. Establishes the frame: "I didn't mean…"
2. Delivers the action: "…to make Haru cry."
3. The noun "intention" is implied inside the modal verb "mean."

English places the main action concept early and wraps the details in an infinitive phrase. Japanese places the details first and lets the noun carry them into the sentence-final predicate.

## 9. Additional Examples

### Relative Clause — Verb + Noun
- <ruby>母<rt>はは</rt></ruby>が<ruby>買<rt>か</rt></ruby>った<ruby>本<rt>ほん</rt></ruby> (book that mother bought)
- <ruby>毎朝<rt>まいあさ</rt></ruby><ruby>走<rt>はし</rt></ruby>る<ruby>公園<rt>こうえん</rt></ruby> (park where [I] run every morning)

- <ruby>自分<rt>じぶん</rt></ruby>で<ruby>選<rt>えら</rt></ruby>んだ<ruby>道<rt>みち</rt></ruby> (path that one chose oneself)

### Nominalization — Verb + の
- <ruby>母<rt>はは</rt></ruby>が<ruby>買<rt>か</rt></ruby>ったのは<ruby>高<rt>たか</rt></ruby>かった (What mother bought was expensive)
- <ruby>毎朝<rt>まいあさ</rt></ruby><ruby>走<rt>はし</rt></ruby>るのは<ruby>大変<rt>たいへん</rt></ruby>だ (Running every morning is tough)
- <ruby>誰<rt>だれ</rt></ruby>も<ruby>知<rt>し</rt></ruby>らないのは<ruby>問題<rt>もんだい</rt></ruby>だ (The fact that nobody knows is a problem)

## 10. Why English Speakers Confuse These

English relative clauses use relative pronouns (that, which, who) and appear **after** the noun. English gerunds (-ing forms) can act as nouns but do not require a special particle. Consequently:

- An English speaker sees <ruby>泣<rt>な</rt></ruby>かせるつもり and wants to translate it as "making cry's intention" or insert "of" or "to" in ways that don't match Japanese structure.
- An English speaker sees <ruby>泣<rt>な</rt></ruby>かせるの and wants to know why "making cry" needs a の when English "making someone cry" works as a bare gerund.

**The shift in mindset:** Japanese does not mark relative clauses with pronouns; it marks them with **position** (verb before noun). Japanese does not allow bare verbs in nominal slots; it marks nominalization with **の**.

---

## 11. Summary

| | Relative Clause | Nominalization |
|---|---|---|
| **Form** | [Verb] + Noun | [Verb] + の |
| **Noun status** | Real, independent noun | の is a grammatical nominalizer |
| **What follows** | Nothing (bare noun) | Particle or copula |
| **Function** | Describes which noun | Turns action into a concept |
| **Example** | <ruby>泣<rt>な</rt></ruby>かせるつもり | <ruby>泣<rt>な</rt></ruby>かせるのが<ruby>悪<rt>わる</rt></ruby>い |
| **English parallel** | Intention to make cry | Making cry (is bad) |

**One-sentence takeaway:** If the word after the verb is a content noun, the verb is modifying it directly as a relative clause. If the word after the verb is の, the verb has been turned into a noun concept. Never insert の between a verb and a content noun like つもり、<ruby>予定<rt>よてい</rt></ruby>、or <ruby>仕事<rt>しごと</rt></ruby>.
