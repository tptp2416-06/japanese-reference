# Extra Modest Expressions — <ruby>謙譲語<rt>けんじょうご</rt></ruby>

## おる & お・ご〜する

---

## JLPT Level {#jlpt-level}

!!! abstract "JLPT Level — Upper N4 / Lower N3"
   The individual patterns (おる, お〜する, ご〜する) are N4 grammar. But deploying them correctly — knowing which verbs have special humble forms, which use お vs. ご, and when extra modesty becomes excessive — is solidly lower N3. This is the first major keigo hurdle where learners must abandon one-to-one translation and think in social layers.

   | Tier | Pattern | Description | Example |
   |------|---------|-------------|---------|
   | N5 — Foundation | です／ます | Basic polite forms — distance without humility | <ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>きます。*"I go [polite]."* |
| N4 — Foundation | <ruby>謙譲語<rt>けんじょうご</rt></ruby> I — お〜する | Humble prefix + stem + する for group 1 verbs | <ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>つ → <ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>ちます → <ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>ち<ruby>致<rt>いた</rt></ruby>す → お<ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>ちします |
| **N4 — Core ← You are here** | **<ruby>謙譲語<rt>けんじょうご</rt></ruby> II — Special verbs & おる** | **Verbs with dedicated humble forms; いる→おる** | <ruby>私<rt>わたし</rt></ruby>は<ruby>社長室<rt>しゃちょうしつ</rt></ruby>に<ruby>居<rt>お</rt></ruby>ります。 |
| N3 — Extended | お〜する vs. ご〜する | Choosing prefix based on verb origin (<ruby>和語<rt>わご</rt></ruby> vs. <ruby>漢語<rt>かんご</rt></ruby>) | <ruby>連絡<rt>れんらく</rt></ruby>する → ご<ruby>連絡<rt>れんらく</rt></ruby>する |
| N3 — Extended | <ruby>謙譲語<rt>けんじょうご</rt></ruby> overuse & avoidance | When humility sounds servile or when <ruby>丁寧語<rt>ていねいご</rt></ruby> is safer | <ruby>食<rt>た</rt></ruby>べる → <ruby>頂<rt>いただ</rt></ruby>く (humble receive) vs. お<ruby>食<rt>た</rt></ruby>べする (unnatural) |
| N2 — Awareness | <ruby>謙譲語<rt>けんじょうご</rt></ruby> + <ruby>受身形<rt>うけみけい</rt></ruby> (double keigo) | お<ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>ちいただく — humble + receive | <ruby>資料<rt>しりょう</rt></ruby>をお<ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>ちいただきます。 |

## Quick Summary {#quick-summary}

!!! tip "At a glance"
- <ruby>謙譲語<rt>けんじょうご</rt></ruby> (kenjougo / humble language) lowers the speaker's status relative to the listener or a third party. Unlike <ruby>丁寧語<rt>ていねいご</rt></ruby> (polite language) which simply adds distance, humble language actively degrades the speaker's actions.
- **Special humble verbs** replace common verbs entirely: いる→<ruby>居<rt>お</rt></ruby>る, <ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>く・<ruby>来<rt>く</rt></ruby>る→<ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>る, する→<ruby>致<rt>いた</rt></ruby>す, <ruby>言<rt>い</rt></ruby>う→<ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>す, <ruby>食<rt>た</rt></ruby>べる・<ruby>飲<rt>の</rt></ruby>む→<ruby>頂<rt>いただ</rt></ruby>く, <ruby>知<rt>し</rt></ruby>る→<ruby>存<rt>ぞん</rt></ruby>じる, <ruby>見<rt>み</rt></ruby>る→<ruby>拝見<rt>はいけん</rt></ruby>する.
   - **お〜する** (for wago / native Japanese verbs) and **ご〜する** (for kango / Sino-Japanese nouns/verbs) create humble forms for verbs that lack special dedicated forms.
   - おる is the humble form of いる — not merely "to be" but "to humbly be." Using it positions the speaker as occupying space in the listener's world at the listener's tolerance.
- The boundary between appropriate humility and servile overuse is thin. Native speakers often prefer <ruby>丁寧語<rt>ていねいご</rt></ruby> (ます) over <ruby>謙譲語<rt>けんじょうご</rt></ruby> when the social distance is unclear.

## Core Meaning {#core-meaning}

!!! note "Core Rule"
Use <ruby>謙譲語<rt>けんじょうご</rt></ruby> when describing your own actions (or your in-group's actions) in the presence of someone whose status is higher than yours, or when you want to show deference through self-deprecation. The key principle: **lower yourself, elevate the other.**

| <ruby>私<rt>わたし</rt></ruby>は<ruby>明日<rt>あした</rt></ruby><ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>ります。 | I will come/go tomorrow [humble]. |
|---|---|
| | *Literal: I tomorrow humbly-come/go [polite].* |
| | *Context: Speaking to a superior about visiting them. <ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>る replaces both <ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>く and <ruby>来<rt>く</rt></ruby>る in humble contexts.* |

| <ruby>私<rt>わたし</rt></ruby>は<ruby>社長室<rt>しゃちょうしつ</rt></ruby>に<ruby>居<rt>お</rt></ruby>ります。 | I am in the president's office [humbly occupying space]. |
|---|---|
| | *Literal: I president's-office-in humbly-am [polite].* |
| | *Context: The input pattern. おる replaces いる. The nuance: "I am present here at the company's tolerance, in the president's space."* |

| <ruby>資料<rt>しりょう</rt></ruby>をお<ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>ちします。 | I will bring the materials [humbly performing the bringing]. |
|---|---|
| | *Literal: Materials [object] humble-bringing do [polite].* |
| | *Context: Speaking to a superior. <ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>つ→お<ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>ちする. The お elevates the action; the する degrades the agent.* |

## The Two Systems: Special Verbs & お・ご〜する {#the-two-systems}

<ruby>謙譲語<rt>けんじょうご</rt></ruby> operates through two parallel systems. Mastering both — and knowing when each applies — is the core N4/N3 keigo challenge.

### System 1: Special Humble Verbs

Certain common verbs have dedicated humble forms that must be memorized. These are not derived from the original verb; they are separate lexical items with built-in humility.

| Plain | Humble | Meaning | Nuance |
|-------|--------|---------|--------|
| いる | <ruby>居<rt>お</rt></ruby>る (おる) | to be, to exist (animate) | Humble existence — occupying space at others' tolerance |
| <ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>く / <ruby>来<rt>く</rt></ruby>る / いる | <ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>る | to go / to come / to be | Humble movement — approaching or being in another's space |
| する | <ruby>致<rt>いた</rt></ruby>す | to do | Humble action — performing at another's service |
| <ruby>言<rt>い</rt></ruby>う | <ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>す | to say | Humble speech — speaking to a superior |
| <ruby>食<rt>た</rt></ruby>べる / <ruby>飲<rt>の</rt></ruby>む | <ruby>頂<rt>いただ</rt></ruby>く | to eat / to drink / to receive | Humble receiving — accepting what is given from above |
| <ruby>知<rt>し</rt></ruby>る | <ruby>存<rt>ぞん</rt></ruby>じる | to know | Humble knowledge — knowing as a lesser person |
| <ruby>見<rt>み</rt></ruby>る | <ruby>拝見<rt>はいけん</rt></ruby>する | to see / to look | Humble seeing — viewing what is above one's station |
| <ruby>聞<rt>き</rt></ruby>く | <ruby>伺<rt>うかが</rt></ruby>う | to hear / to ask / to visit | Humble hearing/asking — receiving information from above |
| あげる | <ruby>差<rt>さ</rt></ruby>し<ruby>上<rt>あ</rt></ruby>げる | to give (to superior) | Humble giving — offering upward |
| もらう | <ruby>頂<rt>いただ</rt></ruby>く / <ruby>戴<rt>いただ</rt></ruby>く | to receive (from superior) | Humble receiving — accepting from above |
| <ruby>知<rt>し</rt></ruby>っている | <ruby>存<rt>ぞん</rt></ruby>じて<ruby>上<rt>あ</rt></ruby>げる | to know (humble progressive) | Progressive humble knowledge |

!!! warning "おる is not casual"
   In some dialects (especially Kansai), おる is used casually as a plain form equivalent to いる. In standard keigo, however, おる is strictly humble. Using it casually in Tokyo sounds dialectal or rough; using it formally without keigo intent sounds bizarre.

### System 2: お〜する & ご〜する

For verbs without special humble forms, create one with prefix + stem + する.

| Pattern | Applies To | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| お + [verb stem] + する | Wago (<ruby>和語<rt>わご</rt></ruby> / native Japanese verbs) | <ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>つ → お<ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>ちする |
| ご + [noun/verb] + する | Kango (<ruby>漢語<rt>かんご</rt></ruby> / Sino-Japanese) | <ruby>連絡<rt>れんらく</rt></ruby>する → ご<ruby>連絡<rt>れんらく</rt></ruby>する |

!!! warning "お vs. ご — the origin rule"
This is the single most common keigo error. お attaches to native Japanese (<ruby>和語<rt>わご</rt></ruby>) verb stems. ご attaches to Sino-Japanese (<ruby>漢語<rt>かんご</rt></ruby>) nouns and verbs. Saying お<ruby>連絡<rt>れんらく</rt></ruby>する or ご<ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>ちする is ungrammatical.

| Correct | Incorrect | Why |
|---------|-----------|-----|
| お<ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>ちする | ご<ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>ちする | <ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>つ is wago — takes お |
| ご<ruby>連絡<rt>れんらく</rt></ruby>する | お<ruby>連絡<rt>れんらく</rt></ruby>する | <ruby>連絡<rt>れんらく</rt></ruby> is kango — takes ご |
| お<ruby>書<rt>か</rt></ruby>きする | ご<ruby>書<rt>か</rt></ruby>きする | <ruby>書<rt>か</rt></ruby>く is wago — takes お |
| ご<ruby>紹介<rt>しょうかい</rt></ruby>する | お<ruby>紹介<rt>しょうかい</rt></ruby>する | <ruby>紹介<rt>しょうかい</rt></ruby> is kango — takes ご |

!!! tip "The wago/kango test"
If the verb can be written in pure hiragana without <ruby>漢字<rt>かんじ</rt></ruby> (<ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>つ→もつ), it's likely wago and takes お. If it's already in <ruby>漢字<rt>かんじ</rt></ruby> and feels "compound-y" (<ruby>連絡<rt>れんらく</rt></ruby>, <ruby>紹介<rt>しょうかい</rt></ruby>, <ruby>説明<rt>せつめい</rt></ruby>), it's likely kango and takes ご. This is a rough heuristic but works for most N4/N3 vocabulary.

## In Real Life {#in-real-life}

!!! example "In Real Life"
**Situation:** <ruby>田中<rt>たなか</rt></ruby> (Tanaka), a junior sales representative at a trading company, is speaking to <ruby>山本部長<rt>やまもとぶちょう</rt></ruby> (Yamamoto, the department head). Tanaka has been summoned to the director's office and is now reporting back.

   > <ruby>私<rt>わたし</rt></ruby>は<ruby>今<rt>いま</rt></ruby>、<ruby>社長室<rt>しゃちょうしつ</rt></ruby>の<ruby>前<rt>まえ</rt></ruby>に<ruby>居<rt>お</rt></ruby>ります。お<ruby>呼<rt>よ</rt></ruby>びでしょうか。

   *"I am now humbly positioned in front of the president's office. Might this be a summons?"*

   **Why おる fits here:** Tanaka could have said います — grammatically correct, socially acceptable. But おります signals awareness of hierarchy: he is in the president's space, at the president's building, on the president's time. His very existence there is contingent. The おる acknowledges this power geometry.

**Why お<ruby>呼<rt>よ</rt></ruby>び:** <ruby>呼<rt>よ</rt></ruby>ぶ (to call/summon) has no special humble form, so Tanaka uses お〜する. But he doesn't complete the pattern — he says お<ruby>呼<rt>よ</rt></ruby>びでしょうか ("Is it a calling?"), using the noun form + copula rather than the full verb (お<ruby>呼<rt>よ</rt></ruby>びしますでしょうか would be ungrammatical). This is a common keigo compression: the humble prefix elevates the noun, and the copula handles the predication.

**What makes this natural:** The combination of おる (special humble) and お<ruby>呼<rt>よ</rt></ruby>び (derived humble) shows <ruby>田中<rt>たなか</rt></ruby> operating in two keigo systems simultaneously. Native speakers do this automatically; learners must build the pattern recognition.

## How It Works {#how-it-works}

<ruby>謙譲語<rt>けんじょうご</rt></ruby> is not merely "polite Japanese." It is a **status negotiation system** where every verb choice repositions the speaker relative to the listener. Understanding this repositioning is more important than memorizing forms.

**Layer 1 — Self-deprecation:** The speaker describes their own actions as lesser, smaller, or performed in service. This is not self-hatred; it is social grace. By lowering yourself, you create space to elevate the other.

**Layer 2 — Elevation of other:** The <ruby>謙譲形<rt>けんじょうけい</rt></ruby>implies a <ruby>受取人<rt>うけとりにん</rt></ruby> or <ruby>観察者<rt>かんさつしゃ</rt></ruby> who is <ruby>高<rt>たか</rt></ruby>い. お<ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>ちしますdoesn't just mean "I bring" — it means "I bring [for you, who are above me]." The other <ruby>人<rt>ひと</rt></ruby> is built into the <ruby>文法<rt>ぶんぽう</rt></ruby>.

**Layer 3 — Spatial humility:** Many humble verbs encode physical or social space. <ruby>居<rt>お</rt></ruby>る (to humbly be) implies occupying space at another's tolerance. <ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>る (to humbly go/come) implies moving within another's sphere. <ruby>伺<rt>うかが</rt></ruby>う (to humbly hear/ask/visit) implies approaching another's presence.

| <ruby>私<rt>わたし</rt></ruby>が<ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>きます。 | I will go [polite, neutral]. |
|---|---|
| | *The speaker is going. The destination and purpose are neutral.* |

| <ruby>私<rt>わたし</rt></ruby>が<ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>ります。 | I will go/come [humble, deferential]. |
|---|---|
| | *The speaker's movement is framed as approaching a superior's space. The going is an act of service or obligation.* |

| <ruby>私<rt>わたし</rt></ruby>はここに<ruby>居<rt>お</rt></ruby>ります。 | I am here [humbly existing]. |
|---|---|
| | *The speaker's presence is contingent, tolerated, perhaps even intrusive. The おる acknowledges this.* |

### Real Speech vs. Textbook Form

Textbooks present keigo as a fixed system: "Use <ruby>謙譲語<rt>けんじょうご</rt></ruby> for your own actions, <ruby>尊敬語<rt>そんけいご</rt></ruby> for others' actions." Real <ruby>speech<rt>スピーチ</rt></ruby> is messier — speakers often avoid keigo entirely when uncertain, or mix levels within a single utterance.

| Form | Example | Note |
|------|---------|------|
| Textbook (pure keigo) | <ruby>私<rt>わたし</rt></ruby>は<ruby>明日<rt>あした</rt></ruby><ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>ります。 | Clean, correct, slightly stiff |
| Real speech (keigo + polite) | <ruby>明日<rt>あした</rt></ruby>、お<ruby>邪魔<rt>じゃま</rt></ruby>させていただきます。 | "I will humbly receive the favor of disturbing you tomorrow" — <ruby>謙譲語<rt>けんじょうご</rt></ruby> + <ruby>使役<rt>しえき</rt></ruby> + <ruby>受身形<rt>うけみけい</rt></ruby>, extremely common in business |
| Real speech (keigo avoidance) | <ruby>明日<rt>あした</rt></ruby><ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>きますね。 | Neutral polite — when the relationship is close or the keigo boundary unclear |
| Real speech (overkeigo) | <ruby>私<rt>わたし</rt></ruby>がお<ruby>食<rt>た</rt></ruby>べいたします。 | Excessive — <ruby>食<rt>た</rt></ruby>べるhas special form <ruby>頂<rt>いただ</rt></ruby>く; お<ruby>食<rt>た</rt></ruby>べするis unnatural and sounds like parody |

## Grammar Analysis {#grammar-analysis}

### Function in Context

- <ruby>私<rt>わたし</rt></ruby>は marks the speaker as topic — standard. In keigo contexts, first-person pronouns are often dropped because the humble verb itself implies "I."
- <ruby>社長室<rt>しゃちょうしつ</rt></ruby>の<ruby>前<rt>まえ</rt></ruby>に locates the speaker spatially. The location (in front of the president's office) is high-status space; the おる acknowledges this.
- おります is the humble form of いる, conjugated to polite ます-form. The stem is おり; the ます attaches to the i-stem (おり → おります). This is a regular Godan conjugation despite the verb's special status.
- お<ruby>呼<rt>よ</rt></ruby>びでしょうか uses the derived humble pattern incompletely — <ruby>呼<rt>よ</rt></ruby>ぶ→お<ruby>呼<rt>よ</rt></ruby>び (stem + prefix), then treated as a noun with copula でしょうか. This is a common keigo compression: rather than "Will you summon me?" (direct, potentially demanding), the speaker asks "Is this a summons?" (indirect, self-effacing).
- Without おる: 私は<ruby>社長室<rt>しゃちょうしつ</rt></ruby>の<ruby>前<rt>まえ</rt></ruby>にいます would be a neutral location report. With おる: the location becomes a statement of social position — "I am positioned here, aware of my place."
- Without お<ruby>呼<rt>よ</rt></ruby>び: <ruby>呼<rt>よ</rt></ruby>ばれるんですか ("Will I be called?") uses passive and sounds passive-aggressive or anxious. お<ruby>呼<rt>よ</rt></ruby>びでしょうか is proactively humble.

| <ruby>社長<rt>しゃちょう</rt></ruby>、お<ruby>戻<rt>もど</rt></ruby>りですか。 | President, have you returned [to your esteemed presence]? |
|---|---|
| | *Literal: President, humble-return is?* |
| | *Context: Staff to president. <ruby>戻<rt>もど</rt></ruby>る→お<ruby>戻<rt>もど</rt></ruby>り, then nominalized with copula. The staff does not ask "Did you come back?" — they ask about the state of the president's presence.* |

| Wago Ichidan | <ruby>見<rt>み</rt></ruby>る | <ruby>見<rt>み</rt></ruby> | お | お<ruby>見<rt>み</rt></ruby>する (but <ruby>拝見<rt>はいけん</rt></ruby>する preferred) |

!!! warning "Not all verbs take お〜する"
Verbs with special humble forms (<ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>く→<ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>る, <ruby>見<rt>み</rt></ruby>る→<ruby>拝見<rt>はいけん</rt></ruby>する) should use those forms instead of お〜する. Saying お<ruby>見<rt>み</rt></ruby>する or お<ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>きする is unnatural — not ungrammatical, but like wearing a tuxedo t-shirt.

## Japanese Mindset {#japanese-mindset}

Keigo is not a costume you put on for formal occasions. It is a **social positioning system** that operates constantly, even unconsciously. The choice between います and おります is not about politeness level — it is about acknowledging that you exist in a web of relationships where some positions are higher than others.

The humble speaker does not say "I am less than you" (which would be self-abasement). They say "I am performing a role in which my actions are oriented toward you." The humility is **relational**, not personal.

- **Space as social geometry.** おる is humble because it implies occupying space that belongs to another. When Tanaka says <ruby>社長室<rt>しゃちょうしつ</rt></ruby>の<ruby>前<rt>まえ</rt></ruby>におります, he is not saying "I am small" — he is saying "I am in the president's space, positioned by his authority." The humility is spatial and relational.
- **Action as service.** お<ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>ちします doesn't mean "I am a lowly being who brings." It means "My bringing is an act performed in your service." The action is redefined by its orientation toward the superior.
- **The paradox of keigo fluency.** Native speakers often claim keigo is "difficult" even while using it flawlessly. This is because keigo is not a set of rules but a **social intuition** — the forms are easy, but the calibration is hard. Knowing when おります is respectful and when it is servile requires reading the room, not the textbook.

| おる | Special humble for いる | Spatial humility; occupying space at another's tolerance | Being present in superior's space; formal self-location | N4 |
| <ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>る | Special humble for <ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>く/<ruby>来<rt>く</rt></ruby>る | Movement as approach to superior | Visiting, coming, going in service contexts | N4 |
| お〜い<ruby>致<rt>いた</rt></ruby>す | Double humble (お〜する + <ruby>致<rt>いた</rt></ruby>す) | Extreme humility; potentially servile | Ultra-formal business; risk of overdoing | N2 |

## Alternatives {#alternatives}

### <ruby>丁寧語<rt>ていねいご</rt></ruby> (neutral polite) — ます／です

The safe default when keigo boundaries are unclear. No status negotiation, just distance.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Example** | <ruby>私<rt>わたし</rt></ruby>は<ruby>明日<rt>あした</rt></ruby><ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>きます。 |
| **English** | "I will go tomorrow [polite]." |
| **Intent** | Neutral, respectful, no implication of hierarchy |
| **vs. humble** | Removes the self-deprecation. Safe when you don't know if the listener expects keigo or would find it excessive. |

!!! tip "When to use this instead"
Use <ruby>丁寧語<rt>ていねいご</rt></ruby> when the social relationship is unclear, when speaking to strangers of unknown status, or when you want to be polite without implying hierarchy. Many Japanese prefer this in ambiguous situations.

### <ruby>尊敬語<rt>そんけいご</rt></ruby> (respectful language) — いらっしゃる

Elevates the *other person's* actions, not your own. Often paired with <ruby>謙譲語<rt>けんじょうご</rt></ruby> in the same sentence.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Example** | <ruby>社長<rt>しゃちょう</rt></ruby>は<ruby>今<rt>いま</rt></ruby>お<ruby>戻<rt>もど</rt></ruby>りになりました。 |
| **English** | "The president has now returned [respectful]." |
| **Intent** | Elevates the president's action. Contrast with <ruby>私<rt>わたし</rt></ruby>は<ruby>戻<rt>もど</rt></ruby>りました (humble) or <ruby>社長<rt>しゃちょう</rt></ruby>は<ruby>戻<rt>もど</rt></ruby>りました (neutral). |
| **vs. humble** | <ruby>尊敬語<rt>そんけいご</rt></ruby> and <ruby>謙譲語<rt>けんじょうご</rt></ruby> are complementary, not alternatives. Use both in the same sentence: <ruby>私<rt>わたし</rt></ruby>がお<ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>ちします (humble self) + <ruby>社長<rt>しゃちょう</rt></ruby>がお<ruby>戻<rt>もど</rt></ruby>りになります (respectful other). |

### <ruby>使役受身形<rt>しえきうけみけい</rt></ruby> (causative-passive) — させていただく

Extreme humility — "I will humbly receive the favor of being allowed to do X."

| | |
|---|---|
| **Example** | <ruby>説明<rt>せつめい</rt></ruby>させていただきます。 |
| **English** | "I will take the liberty of explaining [humble + receive permission]." |
| **Intent** | The speaker frames their action as something they are being allowed to do by a superior. Ultra-humble. |
| **vs. お〜する** | ご<ruby>説明<rt>せつめい</rt></ruby>します is humble. <ruby>説明<rt>せつめい</rt></ruby>させていただきます is *ultra*-humble — the speaker is not even the agent, just the recipient of permission. |

## Register and Formality {#register-and-formality}

<ruby>謙譲語<rt>けんじょうご</rt></ruby> is by definition formal, but its intensity varies. The key is matching the humility level to the social gap.

=== ":material-chat-outline: Casual (keigo inappropriate)"
   <ruby>私<rt>わたし</rt></ruby>、<ruby>明日<rt>あした</rt></ruby><ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>くよ。

   *"I'm going tomorrow, okay?"*

**Shift:** Plain form, no keigo. Appropriate for friends, family, peers. Using <ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>る here would be bizarre — like bowing to your roommate.

=== ":material-tie: Basic Polite (<ruby>丁寧語<rt>ていねいご</rt></ruby> — safe default)"
   <ruby>私<rt>わたし</rt></ruby>は<ruby>明日<rt>あした</rt></ruby><ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>きます。

   *"I will go tomorrow [polite]."*

   **Shift:** です／ます only. No keigo. Appropriate for most workplace interactions where hierarchy is present but not rigid, or when unsure of keigo boundaries.

=== ":material-briefcase-outline: Extra Polite (<ruby>謙譲語<rt>けんじょうご</rt></ruby> — humble)"
   <ruby>私<rt>わたし</rt></ruby>は<ruby>明日<rt>あした</rt></ruby><ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>ります。

   *"I will humbly go/come tomorrow."*

**Shift:** <ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>く→<ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>る. Appropriate for speaking to clear superiors or in formal service contexts. The humility is explicit but not excessive.

=== ":material-crown: Ultra-Formal (double keigo)"
   <ruby>明日<rt>あした</rt></ruby>、お<ruby>邪魔<rt>じゃま</rt></ruby>させていただけますでしょうか。

   *"Might I be permitted to humbly disturb you tomorrow?"*

**Shift:** <ruby>邪魔<rt>じゃま</rt></ruby>する→お<ruby>邪魔<rt>じゃま</rt></ruby>させていただく (humble + causative + receive). The speaker is not even the agent of the action — they are the recipient of permission to perform it. Appropriate for highest-stakes formal requests.

## Examples {#examples}

| <ruby>私<rt>わたし</rt></ruby>は<ruby>会議室<rt>かいぎしつ</rt></ruby>に<ruby>居<rt>お</rt></ruby>ります。 | I am in the meeting room [humbly present]. |
|---|---|
| | *Literal: I meeting-room-in humbly-am [polite].* |

~Context: Speaking to a superior who is looking for you. The おる acknowledges that your presence is at their disposal.*~

| <ruby>資料<rt>しりょう</rt></ruby>をお<ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>ちします。 | I will bring the materials [humbly]. |
|---|---|
| | *Literal: Materials [object] humble-bringing do [polite].* |

~Context: To a <ruby>superior<rt>しゅぺりおる</rt></ruby> or <ruby>client<rt>くらいえんと</rt></ruby>. <ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>つ→お<ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>ちする. The お elevates the <ruby>materials<rt>まてりある</rt></ruby>; the する degrades the <ruby>agent<rt>えーじぇんと</rt></ruby>.*~

| <ruby>明日<rt>あした</rt></ruby>、ご<ruby>連絡<rt>れんらく</rt></ruby>します。 | I will contact you tomorrow [humbly]. |
|---|---|
| | *Literal: Tomorrow, humble-contact do [polite].* |

~Context: To a superior or customer. 連絡する→ご連絡する. Kango takes ご, not お.*~

| <ruby>社長<rt>しゃちょう</rt></ruby>、お<ruby>戻<rt>もど</rt></ruby>りですか。 | President, have you returned [to your esteemed presence]? |
|---|---|
| | *Literal: President, humble-return is?* |

~Context: Staff to president. 戻る→お戻り, nominalized with copula. The question is about the state of the president's presence, not the action of returning.*~

| <ruby>何<rt>なに</rt></ruby>かご<ruby>質問<rt>しつもん</rt></ruby>がございましたら、お<ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>し<ruby>付<rt>つ</rt></ruby>けください。 | If you have any questions, please humbly command me [to answer them]. |
|---|---|
| | *Literal: Something questions if-humbly-exist, humble-saying-attach please.* |

~Context: Ultra-formal service language. 質問がある→ご質問がございましたら (humble existence); 言う→申す (humble speech); 付ける→お申し付けください (humble command). The speaker positions themselves as entirely at the listener's disposal.*~

| <ruby>私<rt>わたし</rt></ruby>どもでございますが… | It is we [humble we] who… |
|---|---|
| | *Literal: We-humble [humble copula] but…* |

~Context: Formal self-introduction by company representative. <ruby>私<rt>わたくし</rt></ruby>ども (humble "we") + でございます (humble copula). The entire self-reference is wrapped in humility.*~

## Common Mistakes {#common-mistakes}

### 1. Using お〜する for verbs with special humble forms

The most common keigo error — creating derived humble forms when dedicated ones exist.

!!! failure "Unnatural"
   お<ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>きします

~<ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>く has special humble form <ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>る. お<ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>きします sounds like a learner's overcorrection — grammatically comprehensible but socially off-key.~

!!! success "Correct"
   <ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>ります

~Use the special form. Memorize the list: <ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>く/<ruby>来<rt>く</rt></ruby>る→<ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>る, する→<ruby>致<rt>いた</rt></ruby>す, <ruby>言<rt>い</rt></ruby>う→<ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>す, <ruby>食<rt>た</rt></ruby>べる/<ruby>飲<rt>の</rt></ruby>む→<ruby>頂<rt>いただ</rt></ruby>く, <ruby>見<rt>み</rt></ruby>る→<ruby>拝見<rt>はいけん</rt></ruby>する, <ruby>知<rt>し</rt></ruby>る→<ruby>存<rt>ぞん</rt></ruby>じる.~

### 2. Confusing お and ご prefixes

Mixing wago and kango prefixes produces ungrammatical forms that immediately mark the speaker as non-native.

!!! failure "Incorrect"
   お<ruby>連絡<rt>れんらく</rt></ruby>する

~<ruby>連絡<rt>れんらく</rt></ruby> is kango (Sino-Japanese) — must take ご, not お.~

!!! success "Correct"
   ご<ruby>連絡<rt>れんらく</rt></ruby>する

   ~Kango → ご prefix. The wago/kango distinction is non-negotiable in standard keigo.~

!!! failure "Incorrect"
   ご<ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>ちする

~<ruby>持</ruby>つ is wago (native Japanese) — must take お, not ご.~

!!! success "Correct"
   お<ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>ちする

   ~Wago → お prefix.~

### 3. Overusing <ruby>謙譲語<rt>けんじょうご</rt></ruby> in inappropriate contexts

Excessive humility can sound servile, sarcastic, or simply foreign.

!!! failure "Socially awkward"
   <ruby>友達<rt>ともだち</rt></ruby>に<ruby>会<rt>あ</rt></ruby>いに<ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>ります。

*"I will humbly go to meet my friend."* — Using <ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>る for peer relationships sounds bizarre, like bowing to your cat.

!!! success "Appropriate"
   <ruby>友達<rt>ともだち</rt></ruby>に<ruby>会<rt>あ</rt></ruby>いに<ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>きます。

   *"I'm going to meet a friend."* — Neutral polite. Keigo is for hierarchical relationships, not intimacy.

## Hidden Points {#hidden-points}

- **おる carries dialectal baggage.** In Kansai and some other regions, おる is plain-form casual (equivalent to standard いる). A Kyoto shopkeeper saying おるで is not being humble — they're being local. In Tokyo standard, おる is keigo only. This regional split causes genuine confusion among native speakers too.
- **<ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>る means both "go" and "come"** — but only in humble contexts. It replaces <ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>く when the movement is toward a superior's space, and <ruby>来<rt>く</rt></ruby>る when the movement is from the speaker's perspective toward the listener. The directionality is determined by social geometry, not physical direction.
- **<ruby>頂<rt>いただ</rt></ruby>く (いただく) is the Swiss Army knife of humility.** It means "to humbly eat," "to humbly drink," "to humbly receive," and even "to humbly be allowed to do" (〜させていただく). Its semantic range is vast because the core meaning is simply "to accept from above."
- **<ruby>存<rt>ぞん</rt></ruby>じる has two readings.** <ruby>存<rt>ぞん</rt></ruby>じる (ぞんじる) for "to know" and <ruby>存<rt>ぞん</rt></ruby>じ<ruby>上<rt>あ</rt></ruby>げる (ぞんじあげる) for "to know [about you, respectfully]." ご<ruby>存知<rt>ぞんじ</rt></ruby>です (そんちです) is actually <ruby>尊敬語<rt>そんけいご</rt></ruby> (respectful), not humble — it elevates the listener's knowledge. The <ruby>存<rt>ぞん</rt></ruby>じ family is a keigo minefield.
- **お〜する can be split by particles.** You can say お<ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>ちします (continuous) or お<ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>ちいたします (humble + extreme humble). You can also nominalize: お<ruby>持<rt>も</rt></ruby>ちすること (the act of humbly bringing). The pattern is productive once internalized.
- **The "humble we" (<ruby>私<rt>わたくし</rt></ruby>ども) vs. "humble I" (<ruby>私<rt>わたくし</rt></ruby>).** Using <ruby>私<rt>わたくし</rt></ruby>ども for a single speaker sounds archaic or ultra-formal in some contexts, but standard in company representatives. The ども (<ruby>共<rt>とも</rt></ruby> / "together") historically meant "companions" but now functions as a humble pluralizer.

## Learner Stage {#learner-stage}

| Stage | What It Looks Like |
|-------|-------------------|
| **Now — N4** | Memorize special humble verbs (<ruby>おる<rt>おる</rt></ruby>, <ruby>参<rt>まい</rt></ruby>る, <ruby>致<rt>いた</rt></ruby>す, <ruby>申<rt>もう</rt></ruby>す, <ruby>頂<rt>いただ</rt></ruby>く, <ruby>拝見<rt>はいけん</rt></ruby>する, <ruby>存<rt>ぞん</rt></ruby>じる, <ruby>伺<rt>うかが</rt></ruby>う); produce お〜する and ご〜する correctly; distinguish wago from kango for prefix selection |

| **Mastery** | You control the full keigo spectrum from casual plain form through double keigo (させていただく). You know when humility sounds sincere and when it sounds servile. You can read the social geometry of any room and calibrate accordingly. |
